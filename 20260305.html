TL;DR

Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis President Neel Kashkari indicates that while inflation is generally trending toward the 2% target, new geopolitical uncertainties and tariff-related price pressures have complicated the path for interest rate cuts, leaving him with a projection of only one rate reduction for the remainder of the year.

Inflation Outlook and Geopolitical Shocks

  • Geopolitical Uncertainty: Kashkari is monitoring whether recent oil price spikes will mirror the massive commodity shock of the Russia-Ukraine invasion or the more contained impact of the Hamas-Israel conflict.
      • Implication: A prolonged spike in headline inflation could eventually bleed into core inflation as energy costs filter into manufacturing and services.
  • Core Inflation Components:
      • Housing: High confidence that this is trending down as new lease data eventually reflects in official statistics.
      • Core Goods: Currently trending up, largely attributed to the impact of tariffs and supply chain adjustments.
      • Services: Slowly trending down, closely tied to a cooling labor market.
  • Tariff Impact: The Supreme Court ruling on tariffs has introduced uncertainty. While Kashkari hasn't seen evidence of a "new round" of elevated inflation, he notes that core goods inflation has climbed linearly rather than remaining in its historical disinflationary trend.

Monetary Policy and the "Neutral Rate"

  • Rate Cut Projections: Kashkari’s "dot" in the Summary of Economic Projections (SEP) currently accounts for only one interest rate cut in 2024.
  • The Neutral Rate: Kashkari suggests the neutral rate (the rate at which policy is neither stimulative nor restrictive) is likely higher than previously thought.
      • Evidence: The U.S. economy has "powered through" aggressive rate hikes without the expected slowdown, suggesting that current policy is not as restrictive as modeled.
  • The 1990s Comparison: He references the Greenspan era, noting that even during a productivity boom, the Fed held rates at roughly 5%. He cautioned against the idea that productivity gains automatically necessitate immediate rate cuts.

Labor Market Dynamics and AI

  • Signs of Softening: For the first time in 10 years, businesses in North Dakota (traditionally worker-short) report being fully staffed, suggesting a "low hire, low fire" environment and cooling organic demand for labor.
  • AI and Capital Reallocation: Kashkari views AI as a "massively capital-intensive" technology.
      • Macro Impact: In the near term, AI may push the neutral rate higher as capital is reallocated from sectors like housing into data centers and compute infrastructure.
      • Business Adoption: CEOs report using AI for specific efficiency pockets, which is beginning to influence their long-term hiring decisions.

Fed Leadership and Institutional Change

  • Kevin Warsh Nomination: Kashkari expressed openness to Warsh’s potential leadership, noting his deep knowledge of the institution.
  • Balance Sheet Debate: Kashkari is willing to revisit the debate between an "ample reserves" regime and a "scarce reserves" regime, noting that the current system is not as simple as originally intended.
  • Communication Policy: Regarding Warsh’s criticism of frequent Fed official speeches, Kashkari defended the practice as essential for transparency and democratic accountability to regional constituents.
  • Jay Powell’s Status: If a successor is not confirmed by the end of Powell’s term, the Board of Governors would likely vote to have Powell serve as "Chair Pro Tem" to ensure continuity.
