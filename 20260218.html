TL;DR

San Francisco Fed President Mary Daly views AI as a potential driver of sustained productivity and disinflation, but emphasizes that the Federal Reserve remains in an "evidence-based" observation mode, waiting for clear data before adjusting monetary policy.

AI and Economic Productivity

  • Productivity Trends: Recent U.S. productivity has tracked at an annual rate of roughly 2.7% over the last 10 quarters, significantly higher than the 50-year average of 1.9%.
      • Causality Gap: Daly notes it is currently impossible to definitively link these gains to AI versus general cost management or one-time pandemic-era adjustments.
  • Level vs. Growth: A key distinction exists between "one-time adjustments" (e.g., automating a single loan process) and "sustained productivity growth" where technology fundamentally changes revenue generation and product design across all industries.
  • Marginal Protection: Firms are likely using AI to protect profit margins as their power to pass through price increases to consumers diminishes.

Inflation and the "Next-Later" Energy Crisis

  • The Inflation Tug-of-War: AI investment creates a dual impact on inflation.
      • Demand-Side Pressure: The massive build-out of data centers raises prices for construction labor and raw materials (metals).
      • Supply-Side Relief: Long-term productivity gains from AI act as a disinflationary force by lowering the cost structure of firms.
  • Energy Imbalance: Within the 12th District (which includes Silicon Valley), demand for electricity is currently outstripping supply.
      • Infrastructure Delays: Supply is lagging due to aging infrastructure, regulation, and supply chain issues for components like transformers.
      • Disinflationary Energy: Daly argues that energy will only become disinflationary once supply catches up to or exceeds demand, which is a "next and later" prospect.

Labor Market: Replace, Augment, and Create

  • Task Displacement: Daly identifies a three-stage evolution of AI in the workforce: Replace (tasks), Augment (workers), and Create (new roles).
  • Net Employment: Historically, no technology has ever reduced net employment; instead, it shifts the nature of work.
      • Current Environment: The U.S. is in a "low firing, low hiring" state, which contributes to worker anxiety despite low unemployment.
      • Skill Longevity: Roles like "prompt engineers" are already being automated, signaling that workers must focus on "durable skills" and stay "AI-ready."
  • Sectoral Concentration: Most current job growth is concentrated in healthcare and education, while other sectors show stagnation or slight declines, creating a "vulnerability" for workers trained in non-growing fields.

AI at the Federal Reserve

  • Operational Integration: The Fed is actively using AI for internal operations, including coding assistance, research discovery, and back-office efficiency (e.g., Fedwire and ACH systems).
  • Exclusion from Monetary Policy: AI is not used to set interest rates or determine the "neutral rate" of interest.
      • Model Limitations: Current models produce a massive range for the neutral rate (from -3% to +11%), making them theoretical constructs rather than surgical tools for policy.
  • Fiduciary Stewardship: The Fed maintains "ring-fenced" proprietary environments to test AI, ensuring they do not introduce risk to public funds or trust.

Monetary Policy Outlook

  • Evidence-Based Approach: While monetary policy is forward-looking, Daly insists it must remain evidence-based. The Fed will not cut rates based on *anticipated* AI gains until those gains manifest in disaggregated inflation data.
  • Restrictive Stance: Policy is currently "modestly restrictive." If the neutral rate is approximately 3%, the Fed has roughly 75 basis points to go before reaching a neutral stance, though this depends on inflation reaching the 2% target.
