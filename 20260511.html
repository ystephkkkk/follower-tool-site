TL;DR

FICO’s aggressive price hikes—increasing mortgage credit score costs from $0.60 to $10.00 per pull since 2022—have triggered a regulatory and competitive backlash that is ending its decades-long monopoly in favor of a duopoly with the lower-cost VantageScore.

The Collapse of the FICO Monopoly

  • Aggressive Pricing: Since 2022, FICO has implemented a 16x price increase in the mortgage vertical, moving from approximately $0.60 per score pull to $10.00.
      • The "Performance Model": FICO is attempting to transition to a back-end loaded model ($4.95 upfront plus a $33–$65 "success fee" upon loan closing), which averages out to roughly $12.00 per pull.
  • Regulatory Shift: The Federal Housing Finance Agency (FHFA), under new Director Bill Pulte, has pivoted from requiring two scores to allowing lenders to choose between FICO and VantageScore.
      • Lender Adoption: Twenty-one major lenders (including Rocket Mortgage and PennyMac), representing a significant portion of the market, have already been approved for a pilot program to use VantageScore.
  • The Bureau Conflict: FICO’s "FICO Direct" program attempted to bypass credit bureaus (Equifax, TransUnion, and Experian). In response, the bureaus—who own VantageScore—now often sell FICO scores at 0% margin, giving them a massive incentive to migrate lenders to VantageScore.

VantageScore: The Emerging Competitor

  • Cost Advantage: VantageScore 4.0 is priced at approximately $1.00 per pull, a fraction of FICO’s $10.00+ cost.
  • Market Expansion: VantageScore claims to score a larger share of the population, particularly "thin-file" consumers and first-time homebuyers who may not have a traditional FICO score.
  • Secondary Market Acceptance: While FICO argues that Mortgage-Backed Security (MBS) investors demand FICO scores, industry feedback suggests investors care about the *risk band* (e.g., 700 vs. 600) rather than the specific brand of the score, as long as the data is translatable.

Sector Outlook: Financial Information Services

  • MSCI (Top Pick): Kelsey Zhu identifies MSCI as a top long pick for 2025. Despite a slowdown in the ESG narrative and exposure to a weak European market, the company maintains a "legendary" management team and a dominant position in indexing.
  • Verisk: This insurance data provider is viewed as highly defensible. Its "loss-cost" data is essentially a monopoly, and the P&C insurance industry is more conservative and relationship-driven than the mortgage sector, making it harder to disrupt.
  • FactSet: Facing headwinds from the broader shift of active-to-passive investing and a downturn in investment banking "seats," FactSet remains a strong workflow tool but faces more skepticism regarding AI disruption than its peers.

The Role of AI: Threat vs. Opportunity

  • Productivity Gains: While the market narrative focuses on AI disrupting data incumbents, Zhu argues AI provides a "meaningful productivity uplift" for developers, which can be reinvested into margins or new products.
  • Incumbent Defense: Established players like Verisk have been utilizing machine learning for a decade. Their vast proprietary "contributory networks" (where lenders/insurers provide data to get data back) remain difficult for AI startups to replicate.
  • Investment Timing: Steve Eisman notes that the market is currently in a "narrative-driven" phase regarding AI, where stocks move on sentiment because concrete data on AI’s long-term impact on earnings may not be clear for 12–24 months.

The Sequence of Strategy, Culture, and "Winning"

James Gorman argues that corporate culture cannot be established in a vacuum; it must be preceded by a viable business model.

  • Strategy as a Prerequisite: Strategy earns a leader the right to talk about culture. It is difficult to preach values to employees if the business is failing or if layoffs are imminent.
  • The "Winning" Catalyst: Nothing drives culture more effectively than winning.
      • High-performing employees ("goers") need to know they are part of a winning organization to remain motivated.
      • Winning creates an infectious environment where people are willing to do exceptional things.
  • The Reinvention Cycle: Gorman views a company's longevity as a series of train cabins: strategy, then culture, then a new strategy, then culture. Strong cultural "bones" provide the confidence needed to reinvent the business over decades.

From "Tenants" to "Landlords"

A central "Gormanism" involves shifting the mindset of employees—particularly in high-finance—away from short-term personal gain toward institutional health.

  • Institutional Loyalty: Employees should stop acting like "tenants" looking for their next bonus and start acting like "landlords" of the institution.
  • The 50/50 Brain Test: Gorman advocates for a balance where an employee is 50% driven by personal ego/confidence and 50% driven by the institution’s success.
      • A culture dominated by individual pieces (98% self-interest) results in a "war of attrition" that damages the firm.
      • Alignment is reinforced by stock-based compensation, where personal wealth is tied to the firm's long-term performance.

Mentorship and the Return to Office

Gorman remains a staunch advocate for physical office presence, viewing it as a moral and professional obligation for senior leaders.

  • The Mentoring Gap: Young employees are "nuts" to stay home because they miss the soft cues and mentorship that occur in person.
  • Senior Responsibility: Senior leaders have an obligation to be present to develop the next generation, just as they were once mentored.
  • The "Restaurant" Standard: During the pandemic, Gorman famously argued that if employees were comfortable going out to restaurants or gyms, they should be comfortable returning to the office.
      • He noted the inequity of senior leaders working from comfortable homes while junior staff struggled in cramped, shared apartments with no privacy.

Best Practices in Succession Planning

Drawing from his experiences at Morgan Stanley and Disney, Gorman outlines a rigorous framework for leadership transition.

  • The Three Pillars: Success requires Clarity, Transparency, and Intentionality.
      • Transparency of Process: Use a formal succession committee with clear start, transition, and finish dates.

TL;DR

Keri Findley, founder of Tacora Capital, leverages a "ratings arbitrage" strategy to invest in highly illiquid credit assets that traditional institutions cannot hold, generating alpha by solving complex financing problems for venture-backed startups. Her reputation for rational, "age-adjusted" trading led Peter Thiel to anchor her first fund as his largest investment outside of Founders Fund, based on personal trust and her ability to structure unconventional risks.

The Investment Philosophy: Risk vs. Illiquidity

  • The Illiquidity Spectrum: Findley differentiates her strategy by moving along the illiquidity spectrum rather than the risk spectrum. She targets assets that lack the ratings required by insurance companies or the liquidity needed by mutual funds.
  • Ratings Arbitrage: A core strategy involves "finding, creating, and analyzing" assets that the market misprices due to regulatory constraints.
      • 2009 Mortgage Bond Case: Findley bought triple-A bonds downgraded to "D" (default) for 60 cents on the dollar. By restructuring them into trusts, she could get 60% of the bond re-rated as "single-A" to sell to mutual funds at 95 cents, effectively acquiring the remaining high-yield "mez" bonds for nearly nothing.
  • The Advantage of "No Scars": Findley attributes her early success at Third Point to being a young trader during the 2008 crisis. Lacking the "battle scars" and emotional trauma of older traders, she could rationally analyze data (e.g., that 97% of mortgage defaults occur by month 60) to identify "fulcrum securities" others were too terrified to touch.

Strategic Operations at Tacora Capital

  • The "North Star" Portfolio: Tacora maintains a diversified split across three primary sectors:
      • PropTech (1/3): Real estate-related credit.
      • InsurTech (1/3): Statutory capital and surplus notes for niche insurance products with low loss ratios.
      • FinTech (1/3): Financial services, invoice purchasing, and receivables finance.
  • Solving the Scaling Problem: Tacora focuses on deals in the $30 million range, a "sweet spot" where they face less competition. Larger private credit firms (Ares, Apollo, Blackstone) have scaled so much they now require $150M+ deal sizes to move the needle, leaving the mid-market underserved.
  • Venture Debt Alternative: Tacora provides an alternative to dilutive equity. Instead of a Series A company giving away 20-30% of the business to fund a lending product, Tacora provides the warehouse line in exchange for a small equity piece (warrants) and interest.

Relationship-Based Fundraising and the "Thiel Anchor"

  • The Peter Thiel Partnership: Findley secured Thiel as her anchor investor without a formal pitch. After building trust over several years through discussions on ideas and lending, Thiel offered to be her biggest investor, stating he was comfortable being the *only* investor if necessary.
  • The Power of Trust: Findley notes that Thiel invests more in the person and their ambition than the specific asset class. His family office’s proximity to her early work with SoFi allowed him to observe her "stewardship of capital" firsthand.
  • Institutional Constraints: Despite a stellar track record at Third Point, Findley found raising a first-time credit fund exceptionally difficult due to the high "quantum of capital" required to achieve necessary portfolio diversity (at least $100M+ to start).

Risk Management and Counterparty Ethics

  • Avoiding Hard Assets: A key lesson from Fund 1 was to never fund mobile equipment (e.g., tractors, trucks). Findley discovered that when a company fails, physical assets in rural areas are easily stolen or hidden by disgruntled employees. She now focuses exclusively on financial assets like insurance notes and invoices.
  • The "Ethical Counterparty" Rule: Findley argues that credit "brings out the worst in people" during defaults. She prioritizes working with founders who have ethical VC board members (e.g., Andreessen Horowitz), as these VCs act as a "golden ticket" to ensure the company behaves professionally during restructuring to protect their own reputations.
  • Portfolio Expectations: Tacora constructs portfolios with roughly 15-21 deals, expecting approximately one-third to wind down or default. The goal is to ensure the equity upside from the successful "winners" (which can return 9 or 10 figures) offsets any credit losses.

The Memory Bottleneck and Structural Shift

  • Supply-Demand Imbalance: The current shortage in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and DRAM is expected to persist through 2026–2028 due to the three-to-five-year lead time required to build new fabrication plants.
  • End of Cyclicality: Historically, memory was a "boom and bust" industry driven by consumer electronics (phones, TVs).
      • Data Center Dominance: Micron’s revenue share from data centers has jumped from 15% to 65% in just a few years.
      • Contract Stability: Short-term consumer contracts are being replaced by long-term data center agreements, leading to a "re-rating" of the sector's valuation.
  • The "Vera Rubin" Effect: Newer generations of AI chips (like Nvidia's upcoming Rubin architecture) require significantly more memory than current Blackwell chips, further tightening supply.

Earnings Momentum and Valuations

  • Earnings-Backed Growth: Despite triple-digit price increases in names like Micron, the rally is supported by massive upward earnings revisions.
      • Reasonable Pricing: Analysts suggest that while prices are up 300%, earnings expectations for some firms have risen six-to-eightfold, making current multiples appear reasonable.
      • S&P 500 Impact: Micron alone has contributed approximately $5 to the S&P 500’s earnings growth this year.
  • International Opportunities: The "narrowing" of the AI trade outside the U.S. points toward South Korea (Kospi) and Taiwan as the primary beneficiaries of the memory and semiconductor demand.

The "AI Value Chain" and Secondary Plays

  • Industrial Enablers: Beyond chips, the "back-end" of data center construction offers significant opportunities.
      • Infrastructure: Companies providing concrete, plumbing, and electrical work for data centers currently possess high pricing power and improved efficiency.
  • Optical and Photonics: For semiconductor chips to function effectively in clusters, high-speed communication is required.
      • Key Players: Roundhill’s Generative AI ETF (CHAT) identifies companies like Credo, Coherent, and Lumentum as essential for chip-to-chip communication.
  • The Defensive Rotation: Mega-cap growth (the "Mag Seven") is increasingly viewed as a defensive "safe haven" during periods of macroeconomic uncertainty or high oil prices.

Macroeconomic Risks and Geopolitical Hedges

  • Geopolitical Insulation: While global conflicts (Iran, Ukraine) create volatility, U.S. large-cap growth remains relatively insulated compared to Europe.
  • Energy vs. Technology: Experts suggest buying oil as a tail-risk commodity hedge, but emphasize that mega-cap tech is fundamentally driven by silicon rather than energy costs.
  • Sustained Demand: AI and data center buildouts are now significant contributors to U.S. GDP, suggesting that demand may remain resilient despite broader economic cooling.
