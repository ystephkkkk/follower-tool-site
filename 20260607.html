TL;DR

The secondary market has reached record volumes, doubling its 2021 peak to become a primary liquidity mechanism that actively competes with IPOs. As companies choose to stay private longer, structured secondary markets are shifting from a shadow market into a highly regulated, democratized asset class, forcing venture capitalists to adopt active public-style selling strategies to return capital to LPs.

## The Exploding Scale of Secondary Markets

  • Record-Breaking Volume: Secondary market transactions have reached record highs, doubling the previous peak observed at the end of 2021.
  • Competing with Traditional Exits: Employee and early investor secondaries now represent 31% of all primary venture activity in 2025, solidifying secondaries as a major alternative to traditional IPOs and M&A.
  • Shift to Premium Pricing: In previous years, secondary shares traded at an average 20% discount (80 cents on the dollar) to incentivize buyers. By Q1 2025, secondary transactions surged to a premium of 106% of net asset value (NAV).
  • The Rise of "Quasi-Public" Giants: Massive late-stage private companies—such as SpaceX, Anthropic, Anduril, and Databricks—now trade on secondary desks daily, effectively operating as public companies in terms of volume and interest without the associated regulatory disclosure burdens.

## The Debate on Staying Private vs. Going Public

  • The Danger of Private Market "Sycophancy": Both Gavin Baker (Atreides) and Brad Gerstner (Altimeter) argue that staying private for too long harms executive decision-making.
      • Lack of Clean Feedback: Private board members and investors often act as "sycophants," telling founders what they want to hear to protect their allocations for future rounds.
      • Public Market Accountability: Public markets provide immediate, rigorous, and unsentimental price discovery and pressure testing that forces management to execute cleanly.
  • The Mark Zuckerberg HTML5 Lesson: Gavin Baker highlighted Facebook's historical 2010–2012 delay in building mobile native apps (relying too long on HTML5). Zuckerberg later reflected that had Facebook been public during that internal debate, rigorous questioning from public equity analysts would have forced him to correct the mistake years faster.
  • The Necessity of Liquidity for Talent: With companies remaining private for decades (e.g., SpaceX for 24 years), employees frequently find themselves paper-rich but cash-poor. Structured liquidity programs are essential to allow early, hard-working employees to buy homes and support families without forcing the entire company into an premature IPO.

## Democratization and the Forge-Schwab Alliance

  • The Schwab Partnership: Kelly Rodriques (CEO of Forge) detailed Forge’s landmark deal with Charles Schwab, which integrates Forge's private market trading infrastructure with Schwab’s 46 million retail accounts and $12 trillion in assets under management.
  • Regulated Retail Access: Private market access is rapidly evolving from high-fee, unregulated Special Purpose Vehicles (SPVs) toward regulated, diversified fund structures.
      • Interval and Closed-End Funds: New fund products allow unaccredited retail investors to buy diversified private market baskets (featuring companies like SpaceX) with minimum investments as low as $500.
      • Accreditation Changes: Anticipated changes to SEC rules are expected to introduce a "sophisticated investor" test, replacing arbitrary wealth thresholds and further opening access to the private markets.
  • Systematizing Price Discovery: Historically, secondary trading operated like a "ticket broker" grey market with fragmented bids. Forge is building an exchange-like infrastructure to provide clean, standardized price discovery for late-stage private equities.

## Active Portfolio Management and Venture Capital Exits

  • DPI Imperialism: With LPs aggressively demanding Distributed to Paid-In Capital (DPI), venture capitalists can no longer afford to hold late-stage positions indefinitely. Brad Gerstner emphasized that Altimeter actively sells 30% to 40% slices of high-performing private positions at 4x or 5x valuations to return capital to LPs.
  • Crossover and Mutual Fund Constraints: Large asset managers (like Fidelity, Wellington, and Baillie Gifford) are capped by SEC rules from holding more than 15% of their mutual funds in illiquid private assets (with most self-imposing stricter limits of 3% to 7%).
      • Unlocking Dry Powder: When major private holdings finally go public and lockups expire, these assets move out of the restricted "private" bucket. This structural shift will unlock hundreds of billions of dollars in fresh, institutional late-stage demand.
  • Active Public-Style Discipline: VCs must transition from a pure "buy and hold" mindset to active portfolio management, determining daily whether they should buy, hold, or sell, regardless of a company's private status.

## Top Late-Stage Private Targets of Interest

The panel highlighted several high-conviction private companies currently driving secondary interest:

  • Revolut: Recommended by Chamath Palihapitiya as a premier European neo-bank built on a next-generation software stack. The company is actively unbundling incumbent banks, expanding rapidly in the US, and showing strong profitability across 14 lines of business.
  • Zipline: A highly successful autonomous drone delivery network. The company achieved operational scale by delivering critical medical supplies in Africa—reducing maternal mortality by 90% to 95% in key regions—and is now successfully scaling commercial operations in the US.
  • Aria & DriveNets: Highlighted by Gavin Baker as critical infrastructure plays. As AI data centers grow more specialized, these companies are rewriting the networking layer to synchronize disaggregated chips and coordinate prefill/decode processing workloads.
  • Sierra: Co-founded by Bret Taylor and highlighted by Brad Gerstner. The company focuses on building sophisticated, agent-native customer service, sales, and marketing platforms.
  • Neural Robotics: Highlighted by Kelly Rodriques as a German-based, AI-powered logistics robotics company that is quietly generating $100 million in revenue outside the Silicon Valley echo chamber.

TL;DR

AI is transforming healthcare from an art into a computational information science, driving massive efficiencies across diagnostics, clinical trials, and patient care. To capture this value, innovators and investors must navigate a complex regulatory landscape, bypass fragmented legacy data oligopolies, and prioritize immediate, high-utility business models over purely theoretical science.

Redefining Medicine as an Information Science

  • The Healthcare Data Paradox: Despite the U.S. spending 20% of its GDP on healthcare and generating one-third of the world's data—including complex medical records, lab results, imaging, and omics data—patient outcomes have not improved proportionally over the last 10 to 20 years.
  • Key Acceleration Drivers: Morgan Cheatham, M.D., highlights three core pillars driving the modern healthcare investment thesis:
      • Computation: Transitioning medicine into a highly structured information science rather than a subjective clinical art.
      • Precision Medicine: Moving away from standard "one-size-fits-all" blockbuster drugs toward tailor-made therapeutics designed for specific patient mutations.
      • Prevention: Prioritizing tools that keep patients healthy rather than simply treating them once they fall ill.
  • Reducing Administrative Friction: AI is not replacing doctors or nurses; instead, it is absorbing back-office friction and non-clinical tasks, allowing providers to focus directly on patient care.

Investment Strategies and Viable Business Models

  • Avoiding "Science Experiments": Investors are shifting away from long-term, speculative biotechnology bets toward companies with clear, immediate paths to revenue.
      • Direct-to-Clinician Sales: Companies like Open Evidence bypass slow, low-margin B2B hospital procurement processes by selling AI-driven literature syntheses directly to doctors at the point of care.
      • AI-Enabled Prescriptions: Portfolio companies like Doctronic are legally executing automated medication refills and telemedicine routing in states like Utah, capturing direct transactional revenue.
  • Where Value Accrues: On the services and technology side, value is generated directly inside the exam room where the patient-physician relationship is established. In biotech, physical therapeutic assets remain the ultimate currency, as major biopharma companies continue to focus on acquiring physical drugs rather than software platforms alone.

Navigating Regulatory Windows and Data Oligopolies

  • A Pivotal Regulatory Window: Bradley Tusk notes that while broad public and legislative sentiment toward AI has grown increasingly restrictive—such as New York considering a data center moratorium and federal executive orders targeting frontier models—the FDA and CMS remain highly innovative and permissive regarding healthcare AI.
      • Urgency for Startups: Companies requiring regulatory approvals should act rapidly, as shifting presidential administrations could pivot policies or roll back current clearances.
  • Overcoming the "EMR" Oligopoly: Legacy Electronic Medical Record (EMR) systems like Epic remain highly fragmented and siloed, causing clinical bottlenecks and lost patient records.
  • The Rise of Consumer Health Records: With the explosion of consumer wearables, personal genomic sequencing (now costing under $1,000), and accessible LLMs like Claude and OpenAI, consumers are increasingly building their own longitudinal health trackers, bypassing institutional data bottlenecks.
  • Balancing Privacy and Progress: To achieve rapid drug breakthroughs and diagnostic systems, society may need to tolerate lower privacy thresholds. Many patient communities willingly share their health profiles to accelerate cures for rare diseases.

The Next Frontier: Diagnostics, Trials, and AI Prescriptions

  • Computational Diagnostics: AI's value lies in detecting patterns invisible to human clinicians.
      • *Example:* Researchers are using routine mammography data to detect calcifications in arteries, identifying early-stage cardiovascular disease in women long before traditional symptoms manifest.
  • Real-Time Clinical Trials: Traditional clinical trials suffer from severe data lags, often taking months to aggregate and clean data from international sites. Dr. Shalabh Gupta highlights the transition toward "digital twins" and real-time data analytics, which allows companies to monitor trial metrics instantly and terminate non-viable trials early to save capital.
  • The 3-Year Vision: Panelists predict a future dominated by automated biometric-to-prescription systems, localized healthcare policies that integrate diagnostics with treatment, and the ubiquitous societal adoption of highly effective preventative therapies like GLP-1s.
