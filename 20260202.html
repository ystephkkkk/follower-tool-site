TL;DR

Populism is driving a new economic phase marked by higher inflation and robust nominal growth, favoring hard assets and specific sectors like industrials. While the public market is expensive due to Fed backstops, significant risks are accumulating in opaque private markets, and new investment frontiers are emerging in AI and prediction markets.

The Economic Landscape & Populist Drivers

  • Strong 2026 Economic Outlook: The economy is projected to be strong, making a recession unlikely.
      • Catalysts: A "One Big Beautiful Bill" is expected to inject approximately $150 billion into the system through tax refunds and stimulus.
      • Supply-Side Boosts: Provisions for increased capital spending allow immediate write-offs for building new plants.
      • National Events: The 250th anniversary of the country and the World Cup in the US will further stimulate economic activity.
      • Forecast: Earnings growth is anticipated at 14-15%, and GDP at 3% (plus 2.5% inflation) yields 5.5% nominal growth.
      • Sustainability Question: Long-term sustainability beyond 2026-2027 will depend on the success of these capital investments.
  • Populism's Deep Roots: Populism has dominated the American political scene for the last decade, stemming from public dissatisfaction with social and economic experiments that haven't improved lives.
      • Trade & Jobs: "Free trade" with countries fixing their currency (e.g., China) benefited big businesses but not average people, who prioritize jobs over cheaper goods.
      • Immigration: Uncontrolled immigration is a significant concern for both economic (jobs) and cultural reasons.
      • Environmental Policy: The environmental movement, particularly in Europe, "got out of hand" with unrealistic mandates (e.g., auto manufacturers backtracking on EV plans); fossil fuels remain essential for global energy access.
  • Tariffs as Negotiation Tactics: President Trump's tariff threats (e.g., 200% on French wine) are viewed as part of a continuous "negotiation process."
      • Greenland & Rare Earths: Greenland's rare earths are highlighted for their national security importance, especially for AI and economic growth, which the US could potentially acquire without purchase.
  • Investment Implications of Populism:
      • Higher Inflation: Expect higher inflation due to increased government spending aimed at public appeasement.
      • Sector Focus: Industrials, materials, and hard assets (gold, farmland) are expected to outperform as hedges against inflation.

The Fed, Public Markets, and Investor Behavior

  • Expensive Public Markets: The market is currently "expensive" by historical standards, with valuations (P/E, P/B, P/CF) in the "top couple of percent" of all-time readings.
      • AI Justification: While AI is a "game-changing technology" that offers some justification, other factors contributing to high valuations are considered "not so great."
  • Negative Consequences of Quantitative Easing (QE): An inflated Federal Reserve balance sheet and the Fed's consistent willingness to "backstop problems" (financial repression) are key drivers of current market expense.
      • Financial Engineering: QE encourages companies to prioritize stock buybacks over real capital investments, as low interest rates make issuing bonds for buybacks more attractive than building plants.
      • Distorted Capital Formation: Funds flow into companies that might not otherwise be viable, creating issues like private equity firms holding unsellable assets.
      • Enabling Fiscal Irresponsibility: The Fed's role in monetizing debt allows politicians to "spend whatever they want," with a notable "lack of sophistication" in Washington regarding the national debt.
      • Moral Hazard: Constant backstopping (e.g., Silicon Valley Bank) reduces market discipline, fostering a belief among investors that the Fed will intervene in a crisis.
  • Institutional Investor Sentiment: Institutional investors are "cautiously optimistic" and "fully invested," particularly in growth stocks, despite acknowledging underlying risks.
  • Rise of Retail & Meme Stock Power: The influence of retail investors has dramatically increased due to fewer publicly traded companies, online/commission-free brokerages, and rapid information sharing.
      • Consequence: This environment can make investing "akin to gambling," often sidelining long-term investment strategies.

Unseen Risks in Private Markets

  • The Illusion of Low Volatility: Institutions (e.g., pension funds, endowments) are attracted to private equity (PE) by its reported low volatility, largely because assets are "marked once a month" by the PE firms themselves ("marked to private equity").
      • Underlying Reality: Despite the lack of daily price fluctuations, these highly leveraged private investments inherently carry "much higher" volatility, which simply remains unobserved.
  • Drivers of PE Investment:
      • Unrealistic Return Targets: State pension plans, facing balanced budget requirements, often mandate "7%" returns when realistic projections might be "5%", pushing them into PE to bridge the gap and avoid benefit cuts or tax increases.
      • Uninformed Wealthy Investors: Many affluent investors are unaware of the "extraordinary" fees associated with private equity.
  • Current Challenges:
      • Inter-firm Transactions: A significant portion (60-65%) of all PE transactions are between PE firms themselves, raising questions about genuine value creation.
      • Stagnant Exits: Roughly "30,000" companies acquired during periods of near-zero interest rates (2021-2022) are now "stuck" due to current higher rates and lower valuations. The IPO market, a traditional exit route, remains "still pretty poor."
      • Cash Flow Issues: This lack of liquidity is causing "cash flow issues," leading to pension funds seeking to offload investments in secondary markets, typically "at a discount," indicating a "mispricing" of these assets.
      • Critique: Private equity often "smacks of financial engineering" more than it genuinely "fixes" companies.

Key Sector Opportunities & Emerging Trends

  • Bullish on Financials: The firm is "quite bullish" on the financials sector, anticipating benefits from expected Fed easing and potential deregulation under a Trump administration.
      • Regulatory Impact: Previous bank regulations inadvertently spurred the growth of unregulated "private credit markets."
      • AI's Role: AI is expected to "really help banks just to reduce cost."
      • Bank M&A Needed: A wave of M&A among regional banks is critical, as escalating regulatory and technology costs disproportionately burden smaller institutions, favoring large players like JP Morgan (whose deposit share grew from 7% to 13% pre- to post-crisis).
      • Cyber Threats: Cyber threats are an "increasingly significant issue," with expensive cyber insurance forcing some banks to self-insure, further benefiting larger entities.
  • AI as a Fundamental Shift: AI is viewed as a "real thing," not a bubble, with "the sky's the limit" for its applications.
      • Arms Race Dynamics: An "arms race" is underway among major public companies, driven by incentives to invest heavily, where CEOs face greater risk for *not* participating than for investing and potentially losing money.
      • Skepticism on LLM Scaling: Some experts suggest that large language model (LLM) scaling may have "reached a dead end."
  • Housing Market Insights: While affordability is a major issue—wages have not kept pace with the "level of prices" since 2000—there are "signs of life" in housing statistics.
      • Mortgage Rates: Mortgage rates have declined "about 100 basis points" (now around 6% plus), though supply issues persist.
  • Jason Trenard's Investment Focus:
      • Defense & Space: Invests in defense stocks (aerospace and defense), particularly interested in the "space race idea" (e.g., anticipated SpaceX IPO).
      • Hard Assets: Holds gold stocks (miners, GDXJ).
  • Prediction Markets Convergence: A growing trend (e.g., PolyMarket) where individuals can bet on various outcomes (financial, political).
      • Market Implications: These markets could become deep enough for hedge funds to use for risk offsetting.
      • Regulation Urgency: These markets are "ripe for insider trading" due to a lack of regulation, necessitating oversight.

The current macro environment offers a "sweet spot" for financial assets, driven by significant stimulus and a capital investment super cycle that's poised to drive strong M&A and IPO activity. Both Goldman Sachs and Andreessen Horowitz are strategically adapting to this landscape, with Goldman focusing on scale and funding diversification, while a16z is growing its venture influence and actively shaping crucial policy for emerging technologies like AI and crypto.

  • "Sweet Spot" for Financial Assets: The U.S. macro picture is highly favorable for financial and investable assets.
      • Fiscal Stimulus: Significant and continuously increasing, with recent legislation adding to an already stimulative environment.
      • Monetary Stimulus: In a rate-cutting cycle, with a few more cuts anticipated.
      • Capital Investment Super Cycle: Unprecedented levels of investment; last year, the four largest companies contributed 1% to GDP growth with $400 billion in spending.
      • Deregulatory Wind-back: A shift from a massive regulatory surge, further stimulating the economy.
      • Economic Resilience: The economy is hard to slow down due to this "cocktail of stimulus" and enormous financial leverage, despite average Americans feeling stress from 25-30% higher prices.
  • Market Dynamics:
      • AI Productivity Gains: The market is "pulling forward" expectations for AI-driven productivity gains over the next 1-4 years.
      • Geopolitical Fragility: Moving to a multipolar world, with higher geopolitical risks and increased volatility due to social media.
  • M&A and IPO Expectations:
      • Regulatory Shift: The previous "no" to M&A questions from regulators is now "maybe," even for very significant deals, due to a changing regulatory environment.
