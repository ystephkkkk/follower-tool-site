TL;DR

The next frontier in AI is achieving "sample efficiency" through World Models, which allow agents to learn from synthetic, "imagined" data and predict future states—a process that mirrors the human brain’s evolution but remains hindered by the massive action-space explosion in robotics and self-driving.

The Problem of Sample Efficiency

  • Defining the Gap: One of the most significant hurdles in AI is "intelligence per sample"—the rate at which a model acquires skill relative to new data. While humans can learn a task in a few tries, frontier models require tens of thousands of data points.
  • Inductive Bias: Humans benefit from a massive inductive bias (genetics and K-12 education), whereas models must often compress the entire internet to achieve comparable generalization.
  • The Perfect Model: A "perfect" world model requires zero new samples because it can predict every outcome.
      • Newtonian Physics: This serves as a perfect, differentiable world model. NASA can intercept an asteroid years in advance without collecting new samples because the transition function ($F=MA$) is deterministic and known.
  • Human Simulation: A 1967 study showed that humans who "imagined" practicing basketball layups improved by 23%, nearly matching the 24% improvement of those who physically practiced. This suggests the human neo-cortex evolved specifically for high-fidelity world modeling and "test-time" planning.

Control Theory and the Action Space Problem

  • Deterministic vs. Stochastic: In a drone flight (Newtonian physics), we use Model Predictive Control (MPC) and convex optimization to find an optimal path. This is "deterministic differentiable control."
  • Non-Differentiable Environments: When an adversary (or another driver) is introduced, the environment becomes stochastic and non-differentiable. You cannot "back-prop" through another person's brain, necessitating Reinforcement Learning (RL) to estimate value and policy.
  • The Action Space Explosion: The difficulty of a task is defined by its action-space cardinality ($A^*$).
      • Chess: Very small action space (~8-10 moves per turn); highly tractable.
      • Go: Larger grid ($19 \times 19$), but still tractable with 361 possible actions.
      • Self-Driving: Even a simplified model of steering and braking creates an action space of ~365,000 possibilities.
      • General Robotics: A simple 6-axis arm with 16 degrees of freedom creates an astronomical action space (estimated at $10^{16}$).

Why AlphaGo Cannot Scale to Robotics

  • Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): AlphaGo uses MCTS for "test-time planning," running ~800 simulations per move to explore potential board states.
  • Computational Cost: In Go, this requires ~24,000 model invocations per step. Scaling this to a "million-action" space like robotics would require 60 million+ invocations for a single action, which is computationally impossible for real-time tasks.
  • Real-Time Latency: While a Go player can wait 60 seconds for the computer to "think," a self-driving car must act in milliseconds. If the model takes a minute to decide, the car has already crashed.
  • The Embodiment Gap: Data collected on a Tesla Model X cannot easily be used for a Model 3 because the weights, aerodynamics, and physics differ. This "cross-embodiment gap" makes general robotics data harder to aggregate than LLM text data.

Modern Solutions: Dreamer and Video Diffusion

  • Dreamer Series (Danijar Hafner): A breakthrough approach where a model is trained on a world model first, then a policy is trained entirely on "hallucinated" or synthetic rollouts. Dreamer V4 was notably the first model to successfully mine diamonds in Minecraft using this method.
  • Video Generation as World Models: Researchers are now using video diffusion (Sora-style) and flow matching to build world models from "unlabeled" video (like YouTube).
      • Action Conditioning: By adding a small amount of "tele-op" data (human-controlled robot data) to these video models, companies like Wayve (which raised $1.5B for its "Gaia" model) can simulate driving scenarios.
  • JEPA (Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture): Proposed by Yann LeCun, JEPA predicts transitions in "latent space" rather than pixel space. This avoids the massive computational cost of generating every pixel in a video, focusing instead on the underlying meaning of the scene.

Remaining Hurdles for AGI

  • Physics-Informed Failures: Standard neural networks often fail at "interpolation" of physics. Without proper "data mixing," a model might magically turn a car away from a house because it has never seen a crash in its training data.
  • The Necessity of Sleep: Francois notes that no intelligent species exists that does not sleep. Sleeping may be the biological version of "offline training," where the brain consolidates experiences into the world model via hippocampal "short-wave ripples."
  • Tactile Feedback: Human skin (the epidermis) provides massive streams of data regarding friction, temperature, and shear force. Current robots lack this feedback; if a human’s hands are numbed, they often cannot even tie their shoes, highlighting how crucial tactile data is for fine motor control.

The 400x Leverage and the New Physics of Work

Garry Tan (President, Y Combinator) argues that the fundamental "physics" of business has changed, enabling small teams to operate at a scale previously requiring thousands of employees.

  • Massive Productivity Gains: Tan reports a 400x increase in his own coding output by shifting from writing manual logic to managing AI agents. Even with a "verbosity penalty," the floor for this leverage is 8x to 80x.
  • The "Wiring" Advantage: High-performance founders use the same models (Claude, GPT-4) as everyone else; the differentiator is not the weights of the model, but how the founder "wires the work."
  • Unprecedented Revenue per Head: Tan highlights YC companies achieving massive scale with skeleton crews:
      • Emergence (S24): Scaled from launch to nine figures in ARR in eight months; reached $15M ARR with only 15 people.
      • Retail (W24): Reached $60M ARR with only 40 people.
  • AI as Workforce: The fastest-growing startups do not treat AI as "autocomplete"; they treat it as a workforce of agents executing encoded skills.

Encoding the Organization into "Skill Files"

Tan proposes a framework where core organizational components are converted into code and markdown files, allowing for a "management layer" over AI agents.

  • Roles as Skill Files: A "skill file" is a markdown document describing one specific capability or job clearly enough for an agent to execute.
  • Org Charts as Resolver Tables: Tasks are directed to the correct agent via a "resolver table," which acts as the routing logic for the entire organization.
  • Internal Process as Filing Rules: Organizational compliance and procedures are encoded as rules that agents must follow.
  • Performance Reviews as Trigger E-vals: Automated tests (evaluations) determine if an agent's output meets the required standard, replacing manual performance oversight.

The "Company Brain" and Context Engineering

Because agents can process approximately one million tokens (the equivalent of three Harry Potter books) compared to the human "working memory" of roughly seven items, the primary challenge of modern engineering is context management.

  • The Librarian vs. The Library: A "Company Brain" acts as both a repository of every meeting, email, and decision, and a "librarian" that decides which "three books" of context should be loaded into an agent’s head at any given time.
  • Project GBrain: Tan is open-sourcing GBrain, a retrieval layer (built on Postgres) that functions as a "second brain" for the organization, ensuring agents act with the full weight of the company's historical knowledge.
  • Memory Hygiene: A brain without curation becomes a "garbage dump." Successful AI-native firms treat their internal knowledge base like production infrastructure, focusing on provenance, contradiction checks, and pruning stale facts.

The Discipline of Execution: "Skillify It"

To build a compounding organization, Tan advocates for a strict discipline regarding how work is performed and saved.

  • Eliminating One-Off Work: Founders should never perform a task once without capturing the process. If you have to ask an AI (or a human) for the same result twice, the system has failed.
  • The "Skillify" Command: Once an AI agent successfully completes a task to a founder's satisfaction, the process should be immediately "skillified"—turned into a reusable markdown skill file that enters the company’s permanent library.
  • Organizational Amnesia: Companies that do not capture learning into a central "brain" wake up every morning with amnesia; companies that do get smarter every day as their skill library compounds.
