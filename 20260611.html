How to navigate a crisis: lessons from a chief investment officer

World Economic Forum(1.1M)·33:49·177 views·2026-06-11

TL;DR

Successfully navigating financial crises requires investors to decouple emotional reactions from rational analysis, maintain flexible rather than rigid portfolios, and prioritize long-term relative wealth preservation over the statistically flawed pursuit of perfect market timing.

Endogenous vs. Exogenous Shocks: Defining Market Crises

  • Exogenous Shocks: Events originating outside the economic system, such as geopolitical conflicts or the COVID-19 pandemic.
      • Unpredictability: While external shocks cannot be predicted, their primary danger often lies in their *endogenous* policy consequences. For example, during the 2020 pandemic, governments implemented massive combined fiscal and monetary stimulus. Because the duration of this support was too long and the size was too large, it led to severe, unexpected inflation.
  • Endogenous Shocks: Crises generated within the financial system itself, such as the 1987 market crash or the 2008 Great Financial Crisis.
      • Interconnected Balance Sheets: Because financial assets and liabilities are deeply interconnected, investors can better analyze how these crises propagate. This allows them to identify when a market "wildfire" is reaching its climax and what policy interventions are required to contain it.
  • Geopolitical Resilience (The Iran-West Conflict): In managing the economic fallout of the modern Iran conflict, market responses have remained mild compared to the 1973–1974 oil shocks.
      • Structural Safeguards: Modern economies are less petroleum-intensive than in the 1970s. Additionally, global oil markets began this crisis with ample inventories and structural oversupply.
      • Deleveraged Balance Sheets: Unlike the pre-2008 era, the private sector (households and corporates) in the West and Japan has deleveraged over the last decade, making the system vastly more resilient to sudden infrastructure shocks.

Strategic Portfolio Management and Risk Calibration

  • Relative Wealth Preservation: The core mission of private wealth management is helping ultra-high-net-worth clients "stay rich" relative to their peers.
      • The Benchmark standard: Clients who generate wealth via entrepreneurship or real estate must measure their assets against the MSCI World Index. If a portfolio lags this index by a significant margin, the investor is losing relative purchasing power.
  • The Necessity of Volatility: A reasonable degree of volatility is not only normal but warranted in a healthy portfolio.
      • Robustness Through Flexibility: Similar to physical structures, an investment portfolio that is flexible can absorb shocks far better than one that is completely rigid.
  • Dynamic Risk Adjustments: Rather than attempting to predict the exact path of a crisis, managers must gradually add or reduce risk, retaining the flexibility to reverse decisions quickly.
      • The 2008 Recovery Case Study: During the climax of the 2008 GFC in late March and early April of 2009, Julius Baer normalized its risk load. By tolerating a temporary low double-digit drawdown, they acquired severely undervalued assets and generated a swift, high-performing recovery.

Psychological Pitfalls and Behavioral Bias

  • The Rational vs. Emotional Brain: Citing the work of Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman, Bonzon notes that the human brain is hardwired for Stone Age survival. During market panics, emotional responses naturally override rational thought, creating a massive advantage for investors who can remain disciplined and analytical.
  • The Anchoring Bias: Investors frequently fall into the trap of "anchoring" to a specific historical cash value, such as the liquidity amount realized during an IPO or an M&A transaction. This fixation clouds objective assessment of current market conditions.
  • The Curse of Experience: Long-term market experience can sometimes act as a barrier to accurate analysis if an investor tries to force new events into old scripts.
      • The Tech Stock Misconception: Many veteran investors falsely dismissed the rise of the "Magnificent Seven" tech stocks as a simple rerun of the 1999–2000 dot-com bubble, missing distinct structural differences.
  • The Market Timing Fallacy: Emotional selling during market drawdowns is highly inefficient because it requires two flawless decisions: knowing exactly when to exit and when to reinvest. Bonzon advises everyday investors to focus on personal education, strict discipline, and long-term asset allocation rather than speculative trading.
