TL;DR

BBVA has integrated generative AI across its global operations of 120,000 employees through "The Eight" framework—comprising six specialized functional AI robots and two technical pillars. By combining a business-sponsored, top-down strategy with active bottom-up experimentation, the bank is actively scaling AI from isolated pilot projects into an industrialized, agent-driven operating layer.

"The Eight" Framework: Six Robots and Two Pillars

BBVA's AI strategy is anchored by six specialized "robots" representing high-impact business areas, supported by two foundational pillars:

  • Robot 1 (Retail Business): Creating conversational, multimodal digital channels on smartphones to reinvent how retail customers interact with banking services.
  • Robot 2 (Advisory & Commercial Banking): Empowering bankers in corporate, private, and enterprise banking to increase customer face-time.
      • The goal is to shift customer advisory time from the historical average of 20%–25% up to 30%–35%+.
  • Robot 3 (Risk Management): Providing risk analysts with advanced AI tools to accelerate credit underwriting and improve customer onboarding experiences.
  • Robot 4 (Process & Back-Office Operations): Automating document extraction and classification for heavily administrative processes, such as mortgages, insurance, and consumer finance paperwork.
  • Robot 5 (Software Development): Integrating OpenAI’s Codex across developer teams to clear project backlogs and optimize software engineering capacity.
  • Robot 6 (Connected Robot / General Adoption): Distributing ChatGPT Enterprise licenses to all 120,000 global employees to build general-purpose agents for scheduling, email, and HR tools.
  • Two Core Pillars: Becoming a "data-rich" company to make data consumable by autonomous agents, and building a centralized orchestration infrastructure to manage and continuously improve the resulting network of agents.

Operating Model & Adoption Mechanics

To transition from theoretical AI strategy to bank-wide utility, BBVA relies on active change management and structural peer pressure:

  • Business-Led Sponsorship: The AI agenda is not run in isolation by the technology department. Each "robot" is co-led and funded directly by business unit heads (e.g., the Head of Corporate and Investment Banking co-sponsors Robot 2).
  • Executive Adoption Dashboards: The bank sends monthly adoption metrics directly to the executive committee, including the Chairman and CEO.
      • These dashboards track personal tool usage, benchmarking leaders against their peers to foster healthy executive competition and drive top-down adoption.
  • Bottom-Up Innovation ("Planting Grass"): By distributing licenses broadly, BBVA allows employees to find organic use cases before formalizing them into enterprise paths.
      • This has resulted in more than 100 employee-built GPTs that are actively used by thousands of employees, driving 70% to 80% time savings on specific workflows.
  • Aha Moments and Stage-Gated Funding: Instead of long-term speculative budgets, BBVA funds AI projects iteratively based on tangible "aha moments" delivered every two to three months.

Ecosystem Partnerships & Industrializing the Future

As the technology rapidly shifts, BBVA is focusing on agility over fixed legacy architectures:

  • Mitigating Obsolescence via OpenAI: Partnering closely with OpenAI has allowed BBVA to quickly abandon obsolete technical paths. The bank has made several "180-degree flips" in its technical direction based on direct guidance from OpenAI, avoiding stranded assets.
  • Industrializing Agentic Capabilities: BBVA is transitioning from building bespoke individual agents to building a standardized, industrial-scale playbook.
      • This shift involves automating the governance, security, evaluation, and API connectivity required to deploy thousands of autonomous agents safely.

ECB Governing Council Press Conference - 11 June 2026

TL;DR

The ECB Governing Council unanimously raised its three key interest rates by 25 basis points in response to persistent inflationary pressures driven by the Middle East conflict, maintaining a highly data-dependent, meeting-by-meeting policy approach without pre-committing to a specific rate path.

Monetary Policy Action and Inflation Outlook

  • Interest Rate Decision: The ECB raised its three key interest rates by 25 basis points. The decision was unanimous and robust across all analyzed economic scenarios, including adverse, severe, and a newly introduced "milder" scenario.
  • Revised Inflation Projections: Staff upwardly revised the baseline inflation outlook, primarily driven by a higher path for energy prices:
      • Headline Inflation: Expected to average 3.0% in 2026, 2.3% in 2027, and 2.0% in 2028.
      • Core Inflation (ex-energy/food): Projected to average 2.5% in 2026 and 2027, before easing to 2.2% in 2028.
  • Current Price Pressures: Inflation rose to 3.2% in May (up from 3.0% in April). Energy inflation ticked up to 10.9% in April, while services inflation increased significantly from 3.0% to 3.5%.
  • Inflation Expectations: Shorter-term inflation expectations remain elevated and well above pre-war levels. However, longer-term expectations remain anchored around the 2.0% target.
  • Policy Characterization: ECB President Christine Lagarde rejected the label of an "insurance" or "preemptive" rate hike, clarifying that it is a direct, sensible monetary policy response to an enduring energy shock.

Economic Growth and Activity

  • Revised GDP Projections: Growth baseline projections were revised downward for 2026 and 2027, reflecting the drag of energy costs and geopolitical uncertainty on confidence and real incomes:
      • Growth Outlook: Baseline GDP growth is projected at 0.8% in 2026, 1.2% in 2027, and 1.5% in 2028.
  • Labor Market Resilience: The labor market remains historically tight, with the unemployment rate at 6.3% in April. While job creation continues, labor demand has cooled, and firms expect the labor market to soften ahead.
  • Sectoral Divergence:
      • Services: Showing signs of a slowdown as the war in the Middle East weighs on consumer confidence.
      • Manufacturing: Holding up due to firms building safety stocks to handle supply chain pressures and increased public defense spending.
  • Domestic Demand: Solid household balance sheets and positive wage growth mean consumption is expected to remain the primary driver of growth in the medium term.

Macroeconomic Risks and Financial Stability

  • Downside Growth Risks: Growth risks are tilted to the downside, primarily due to the war in the Middle East. Prolonged energy disruptions, the closure of shipping routes, tighter credit conditions, and Russia's ongoing war in Ukraine remain significant threats.
  • Upside Inflation Risks: Inflation risks are heavily tilted to the upside, tied to prolonged high energy prices, broader wage-price pass-through, trade fragmentation, and extreme weather events driving up food prices.
  • Banking Sector Health: Euro area banks remain highly resilient with strong capital, solid liquidity ratios, and robust profitability.
  • Financial Stability Vulnerabilities: The primary risks to financial stability include sudden asset price corrections—amplified by the non-bank financial sector—and deteriorating asset quality in energy and trade-sensitive industries.

Fiscal Guidelines, Structural Reforms, and Emerging Tech Risks

  • Fiscal Policy Stance: The ECB urged member states to maintain sound public finances. Fiscal support measures addressing the energy shock must be temporary, targeted, and tailored to preserve fiscal sustainability.
  • Structural Reforms: There is an urgent need to fast-track the savings and investment union, enhance single-market integration, and accelerate the transition away from fossil fuels to boost productivity and strategic autonomy.
  • The Digital Euro: Swift adoption of the regulation on the digital euro is deemed essential to enhance Europe's payment innovations and competitiveness.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) Risks: The ECB is actively monitoring systemic and cybersecurity risks posed by rapid AI developments. The central bank is taking defensive patching measures to prevent hacking and is working with the Single Supervisory Mechanism (SSM) to ensure commercial banks safeguard themselves against AI-driven intrusions.

TL;DR

Former CFTC and SEC Chairman Gary Gensler is filing an amicus brief in the Sixth Circuit arguing that Congress never intended for the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) to regulate sports betting under the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act. Gensler contends that sports betting and prediction markets should remain under state jurisdiction, as the CFTC lacks the resources, staff, and legislative mandate to police them.

Legislative Intent of Dodd-Frank and the Amicus Brief

  • Sixth Circuit Filing: Gensler is filing an amicus brief to explain the original context of the 2010 Dodd-Frank Act.
      • He notes that Dodd-Frank was passed during a financial crisis to address credit default swaps and interest rate swaps, not to nationalize sports betting regulation.
  • Lack of Congressional Mandate: During his 54 congressional testimonies as CFTC Chairman, no lawmakers from either party suggested the agency should regulate sports betting.
      • Harry Reid's Position: Gensler asserts that former Senate Majority Leader Harry Reid (D-NV) would have "thrown [him] out of his office" if there had been any attempt to strip states of their betting regulatory powers.

The Case for State-Level Regulation

  • Bipartisan State Coalition: A group of 39 states—representing red, blue, and purple states—recently filed a joint amicus brief in the same Sixth Circuit case to protect state-level regulation.
  • Resource Advantage: States possess the revenue, law enforcement, and regulatory infrastructure to manage sports betting, whereas the CFTC has shrunk and lacks the budget.
  • Local Policy Autonomy: States are better positioned to tailor laws to their local communities.
      • Currently, 11 states prohibit sports betting entirely.
      • States can set customized age limits (e.g., 21 instead of 18) and address local public health concerns like gambling addiction and the "dopamine rush" associated with youth betting.

The CFTC’s Regulatory Limits and Recent Actions

  • Historical Scope: The CFTC was established in the 1970s to oversee agricultural, energy, and financial derivatives, not millions of daily retail sports wagers.
  • Staffing Constraints: The CFTC is a small agency of only about 400 employees and has recently undergone further downsizing.
  • Reversing Past Precedents: The CFTC is currently proposing a 200+ page rule change that Gensler argues attempts to reverse a unanimous 2011 CFTC ruling.
      • The 2011 rule prohibited contracts on assassination, war, terrorism, gaming, and unlawful acts.
      • Preemptive Lawsuits: The CFTC has taken the unusual step of preemptively filing lawsuits against various states (such as Arizona and New York) to assert federal jurisdiction over prediction markets.

Prediction Markets and Insider Trading Vulnerabilities

  • High Sports Betting Volume: Between 70% and 80% of activity on major prediction platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi is sports betting, putting them in direct competition with state-regulated entities like FanDuel and DraftKings.
  • Insider Trading Risks: Ensuring market integrity is exceptionally difficult on prediction platforms compared to traditional stock markets.
      • Unlike corporate equities with established fiduciary duties, prediction markets are highly vulnerable to insiders (e.g., corporate employees or political consultants) trading on non-public, proprietary information.
  • Government Leaks: Gensler warns that government insiders could leak information from the White House Situation Room or Capitol Hill to trade on geopolitical or legislative contracts.

TL;DR

Successfully navigating market crises requires distinguishing between unpredictable exogenous shocks and systemic endogenous failures, maintaining rigorous rational discipline over emotional behavioral biases, and retaining the portfolio flexibility to pivot quickly when initial assumptions prove wrong.

The Anatomy of Market Shocks: Exogenous vs. Endogenous

  • Exogenous Shocks: External, unpredictable events (e.g., the COVID-19 pandemic or sudden geopolitical conflicts) that strike outside the economic system.
      • Secondary Consequences: While the initial shock is unpredictable, it can trigger highly impactful endogenous policy responses. For example, the massive, prolonged fiscal-monetary stimulus implemented during COVID-19 ultimately drove global inflation.
  • Endogenous Crises: Structural failures originating within the financial system itself, such as the 2008 Great Financial Crisis (GFC).
      • Predictability & Propagation: These crises are analytically easier to manage because the interconnections between balance sheets, propagation mechanisms, and the conditions required to contain the system-wide "wildfire" are better understood.

Case Studies in Crisis Management: 2008 vs. Geopolitical Conflicts

  • The 2008 Great Financial Crisis: Yves Bonzon highlights 2008 as the most "enjoyable" crisis of his career to navigate due to its endogenous nature.
      • Mitigating Drawdowns: Understanding the flaws in subprime mortgage securitization allowed Julius Baer to limit portfolio drawdowns during the panic phase and normalize risk rapidly at the market's tipping point in late March/early April 2009, capturing significant upside.
      • Internal Information Silos: A key systemic surprise was that major investment banks hoarded "pseudo-AAA" toxic assets on their own balance sheets. This occurred because manufacturing departments and treasury operations departments functioned in silos and failed to communicate.
  • The Middle East / Iran Conflict: Market reaction to recent geopolitical tensions has been mild, characterized by contained equity risk premia and muted volatility.
      • Structural Buffers: Unlike the 1973–1974 oil crisis, the global energy sector is currently buffered by high oil inventories, structural oversupply, and a lower global GDP oil intensity.
      • Systemic Resilience: The Western and Japanese private sectors (corporates and households) have significantly deleveraged since 2008. Consequently, the current system is far more resilient to external energy and geopolitical shocks.

Wealth Management Philosophy & Risk Dynamics

  • The Goal of Relative Wealth: In private wealth management, the ultimate mandate is to keep clients rich in relative terms.
      • MSCI World Benchmark: For ultra-high-net-worth individuals, lagging behind major indices like the MSCI World represents a real loss of purchasing power and relative financial standing compared to their peers.
  • The Value of Volatility: A reasonable degree of portfolio volatility is healthy and necessary to compound long-term returns.
      • Flexibility as Robustness: Portfolios must remain structurally flexible. In physics and finance, flexible structures are far more robust than completely rigid ones.
  • Agility Over Prediction: Success does not require a perfect crystal ball. Portfolio managers must possess the operational agility to add or reduce risk incrementally and reverse decisions quickly if they are proven wrong.

Overcoming Cognitive and Behavioral Biases

  • The Emotional Brain vs. Rational Brain: Drawing on the work of Nobel laureate Daniel Kahneman, Bonzon notes that human brains are hardwired for Stone Age survival, causing emotional fight-or-flight responses during market drawdowns. The primary role of a CIO is to enforce rational discipline when panic sets in.
  • The "Double-Decision" Trap: Emotional investors who panic-sell during market drawdowns often forget that market timing requires two perfect decisions: knowing when to exit, and knowing exactly when to re-enter. Mathematically, very few investors can execute both correctly.
  • The Anchoring Bias: Following a liquidity event (such as an IPO or M&A transaction), investors often become counterproductively obsessed with the specific cash figure crystallized, distorting future risk-taking decisions.
  • The Curse of Experience: Long-tenured investors must guard against falsely projecting past historical templates onto new situations. For example, treating today’s "Magnificent Seven" tech leaders as a simple rerun of the 1999–2000 dot-com bubble overlooks fundamental structural differences.

TL;DR

Meesho became India's dominant Android shopping app by relentlessly focusing on mass-market consumers and pivoting its business model through five distinct iterations—transitioning from a localized directory to a WhatsApp social commerce tool, and finally to a direct-to-consumer e-commerce giant processing 2.5 billion annual orders.

Meesho's Scale and Market Dominance

  • Massive Transaction Volumes: Meesho currently processes 2.5 billion orders per year, with its user base averaging 10 purchases annually.
  • Unrivaled User Base: The platform has 250 million unique annual buyers (growing at over 30% year-over-year) and close to 1 million active sellers.
  • Market Share: Out of India's estimated 350 to 400 million annual online shoppers, more than half purchase on Meesho.
  • App Store Leadership: Since launching its direct-to-consumer app on July 5, 2021, Meesho has held the #1 position in the shopping section of India's Android Play Store every single day.

The Journey of Iterations: From FashNear to Meesho Supply

  • The Core Thesis: Co-founders Vidit Aatrey and Sanjeev Barnwal launched the company in 2015 to bring e-commerce to "mass India" outside of major tech hubs like Bangalore, where online shopping was virtually non-existent.
  • Version 1 (FashNear): A localized directory to help physical fashion shops sell to nearby consumers.
    • *The Failure:* The team shut it down in three months after realizing they had only spoken to sellers and ignored the consumer experience, which ended up offering the worst of both physical malls and online shopping.
  • Version 2 (Meesho - "Meri Shop"): Developed during their YC Summer 2016 batch, this software helped physical shopkeepers manage inventory and collect payments over WhatsApp groups.
    • *The Monetization Bottleneck:* While hundreds of thousands of shops used it, local Indian businesses were unwilling to pay for software.
  • Version 3 (Meesho Supply / Product-Market Fit): The founders noticed "power users" who were online-native drop-shippers (mostly homemakers looking for zero-upfront-cost businesses). They launched a separate app to connect these resellers with wholesale suppliers.
    • *Organic Explosion:* With zero spend on marketing due to extreme capital constraints, Meesho Supply doubled in transaction volume every single month for 10 consecutive months, prompting the founders to kill the original app and rebrand.

The High-Stakes D2C Pivot in 2021

  • The Paradigm Shift: By 2020, cheap mobile data (driven by Reliance Jio) and the COVID-19 pandemic forced mass-market Indian consumers to learn how to shop online directly, making the data-saving, WhatsApp-mediated model obsolete.
  • The Hard Decision: Despite having 10 million active entrepreneurs running WhatsApp groups on their platform, Vidit and his team realized they had to bypass these intermediaries and go direct-to-consumer (D2C).
  • The Boardroom Risk: Investors were hesitant and wanted a slow experiment, but Vidit committed fully, knowing a half-hearted pivot would alienate sellers without winning over consumers.
  • The Result: Meesho launched its consumer app in July 2021. Within five months, it scaled from 10 million users to 100 million Monthly Active Users (MAUs). Competitors who failed to pivot quickly went out of business or were acquired.

The Next Frontier: AI-Driven "Invisible" Commerce

  • Targeting the Next 750 Million: To expand from 250 million users to 1 billion, Meesho must onboard rural demographics who struggle with digital literacy, app navigation, and written search.
  • Voice-First Shopping with "Vani": Meesho recently launched a voice AI agent named *Vani* designed to eliminate the need to read, type, or click buttons.
    • *The Workflow:* Consumers navigate the platform entirely through voice prompts and sliding images, speaking their address and OTP verbally to complete orders.
  • The Strategy: The company prioritizes long-term consumer accessibility over legacy software patterns. Vidit predicts that within a few years, e-commerce may not even require a traditional app interface, but rather a ubiquitous, conversational voice agent.
