TL;DR

Despite prolonged conflict in the Middle East and shifting interest rate expectations, the ETF market remains resilient due to robust market structure; however, investors are increasingly shifting toward energy, hedged equity, and active management to navigate potential "midterm election year" volatility.

Market Resilience and Geopolitical Shifts

  • Unexpected Market Stability: Christian Magoon noted that despite Middle East conflict, credit concerns, and a hawkish Fed, a bear market has not materialized.
      • Resilience Factors: Strong company earnings and the U.S. position as a net exporter of oil and natural gas have buffered the global economy from price shocks.
  • Psychological Shifts: Investors are moving away from the "transient" view of geopolitical risk.
      • Sector Rotation: Leadership is shifting from 2025’s technology winners toward energy and basic materials.
      • Contrarian Indicators: Institutional ownership of energy recently hit multi-year lows just before the sector became a top performer, suggesting "reverse sentiment" is a strong buy signal.

ETF Market Structure and Liquidity Performance

  • Proven Stress Testing: Jamie Harrison highlighted that ETFs have successfully navigated the pandemic, tariff wars, and current geopolitical shocks without structural failure.
      • Arbitrage Mechanism: The primary market mechanism incentivizing market makers remains robust, ensuring liquidity even during wartime shocks.
  • Friction Points: While the overall market is healthy, some "dislocations" are appearing in specific areas.
      • International and Emerging Markets: These sectors are seeing wider spreads and increased risk premiums as the cost for market makers to acquire underlying positions rises.
  • Capacity Concerns: Harrison warned that with over 800 products launched recently, market makers do not have "endless balance sheets" and may become more selective during extreme sell-offs.

Strategic Thematics: Hedging and "Buying the Dip"

  • Hedged Equity Demand: Investors are gravitating toward products that couple equity exposure with option income (e.g., Amplify’s IDVO and NDIV) to mute volatility while maintaining a ~6–10% distribution rate.
  • Cybersecurity Opportunities: Magoon views the recent pullback in cybersecurity ETFs as a buying opportunity, arguing that AI will drive M&A and increased demand rather than disrupting the sector's utility.
  • Crypto Stagnation: Cryptocurrency remains "rangebound" due to the "Two L’s": lack of Liquidity and pending Legislation.

Identified "Red Flags" in Complex Products

  • Liquidity Mismatches: Both speakers cautioned against ETFs holding Private Credit, noting a potential disconnect between the daily trading pace of an ETF and the illiquidity of the underlying assets.
  • Equity-Linked Notes (ELNs): Magoon flagged ETFs using ELNs for income as a significant risk during major drawdowns due to underlying credit risk and potential redemption contagion.
  • Due Diligence: Harrison advised investors to stress-test portfolios for 15–20% drawdowns, specifically looking at collateral constraints and whether underlying constituents will continue to trade.

Forward Outlook: Elections and Active Management

  • Midterm Election Volatility: Historically, midterm years see an average drawdown of 17%, followed by a 12-month forward return of roughly 31%.
  • Prediction Markets: New ETF filings are exploring "prediction market" data and binary outcomes related to presidential election results.
  • The Rise of Active Management: Harrison noted that 86% of new products in 2025 were actively managed, a trend expected to dominate through 2026 as the wrapper evolves beyond "cheap passive beta."
