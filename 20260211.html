TL;DR

Microsoft is navigating a fundamental shift where AI-written code commoditizes software, forcing a transition from "growing the pie" to a predatory battle for industry adjacencies. To survive, Microsoft is prioritizing its own high-margin AI agents and R&D over third-party Azure demand, signaling a strategic pivot toward "Work IQ" and integrated utility.

The Software Survival Crisis

  • The End of the SaaS Golden Age: The previous decade of SaaS focused on identifying a business function and building a siloed app. AI-written code collapses this model by lowering the cost of code to near-zero, enabling every software company to attack their neighbors' territory.
  • Commoditization Risks: While businesses are unlikely to write all their own software, the abundance of AI-generated code means software companies must justify their existence through support, compliance, and integrations rather than just the code itself.
  • A Zero-Sum Market: Companies are increasingly cutting software spend to fund their own AI token usage. This shifts the software industry from a period of expansion to a "fight for the pie," where the primary survivors will be those who can leverage AI to rapidly absorb adjacent business functions.

The Azure Miss and Capacity Prioritization

  • Financial Impact: Microsoft experienced its biggest stock plunge since 2020, losing $357 billion in value in a single session—second only to Nvidia’s rout following the DeepSeek launch.
  • Strategic Resource Allocation: The reported "miss" in Azure growth (which fell 1% below expectations) was a deliberate choice. CFO Amy Hood confirmed that Microsoft prioritized GPU capacity for internal R&D and first-party apps like M365 Copilot and GitHub Copilot over renting that capacity to Azure customers.
  • High-Margin Preference: CEO Satya Nadella defended this "portfolio approach," noting that Microsoft earns better margins and higher Lifetime Value (LTV) from its own productivity applications than from pure cloud infrastructure.
  • Infrastructure Stress: Capital expenditures reached a record $37.5 billion in the most recent quarter, a 66% year-over-year increase, highlighting the extreme costs of maintaining the AI lead.

Work IQ and the Future of Agents

  • The Identity Advantage: Microsoft’s "Work IQ" strategy leverages its ownership of identity (Active Directory) to build agents that understand a worker’s role, communications, and history.
  • The Per-Seat Licensing Risk: Microsoft’s current business model relies heavily on per-seat licensing. As AI agents begin to perform the work of humans, the number of "seats" may dwindle, threatening the company's core revenue stream.
  • Adjacency as Defense: To counter the shrinking per-seat market, Microsoft is aggressively incorporating more business functions into its suite. The goal is to make its agents more capable and the software surface area so large that other software makers are forced to interface with Microsoft’s ecosystem.

The Rise of "Token Foundries"

  • The Integrated Fab Problem: Azure currently operates like an "Integrated Device Manufacturer" (e.g., Intel), where it builds the infrastructure but prioritizes its own products during shortages.
  • Customer Conflict: Azure customers (including OpenAI, which represents 45% of Azure’s Remaining Performance Obligations) are effectively "last in line" behind Microsoft's internal needs.
  • Market Opportunity: This creates a strategic opening for "pure play" token foundries—like Oracle or specialized "neoclouds"—that do not compete with their customers and can offer guaranteed capacity, similar to the TSMC model in semiconductors.

One way to track the AI era, which began with the November 2022 launch of ChatGPT, is to observe which Big Tech company was considered most threatened at any given time. Initially, many were concerned about Google and the potential disruption of Search. Early last year, the focus shifted to Apple as an upgraded Siri struggled to launch. By the fall, Meta was in the crosshairs as the company relaunched its AI efforts following difficulties with Llama. Now, the spotlight has turned to Microsoft. This is a full-circle moment, considering the company was initially viewed as the biggest winner of the ChatGPT era due to its partnership with OpenAI. In early 2023, Microsoft seemed exceptionally well-placed. It possessed a cloud service selling GPUs and served as the exclusive provider for OpenAI. While that infrastructure was expensive, it appeared Microsoft was investing in the foundational architecture of a new epoch. At the time, Bing was compared to the Mac on the eve of the iPhone—a small player with everything to gain by disrupting the status quo. Adding GPT functionality to productivity apps was expected to mirror the success of GitHub Copilot, transforming Microsoft from a poster child of disruption victims into a company reaching new heights because of it. However, much of that early optimism now seems shortsighted. While OpenAI remains Azure’s largest customer, the fact that it represents 45% of Azure’s Remaining Performance Obligations is now viewed by the market as a liability. Bing’s growth prospects seemed to stall after Microsoft tempered the initial, more aggressive "Sydney" AI experience. Furthermore, while all Microsoft products now feature a Copilot, their effectiveness remains unclear as competitors like Claude and OpenAI move into the professional productivity space. Microsoft 365 Copilot has 15 million paying customers, but that is a small fraction of the total user base. Moreover, the rise of AI agents raises fundamental questions about the long-term viability of the per-seat licensing model that sustains Microsoft’s business. These factors, combined with Azure growth falling slightly short of expectations, led to one of the worst days in stock market history for the company. Microsoft shares recently suffered a 10% plunge, wiping out $357 billion in value—the second-largest single-session loss ever. This selloff reflects heightened investor skepticism regarding whether the hundreds of billions spent on AI will eventually pay off. While capital expenditures rose 66% to a record $37.5 billion, growth in the Azure cloud unit has slowed. This raises the question of whether the big tech foundations of the future are beginning to crack. Regarding AI-written code, it can be difficult to separate the hype of "vibe coding" from reality, but the trajectory is clear. Even skeptical software engineers admit that AI helps them write features or applications that would have previously been impossible or too time-consuming. AI-written code provides a nearly perfect match of probabilistic inputs and deterministic outputs; the code must run, and it can be tested. While the role of the software architect remains vital, it seems inevitable that the majority of software will eventually be written by AI. This leads to a bearish question: why pay for software when you can ask an AI to write a custom application? There are reasons to be skeptical of the total commoditization of software. Most companies focus on their core competencies, which rarely include maintaining software. Writing an initial app is only the beginning; there is maintenance, security, and constant updates. Furthermore, software companies provide more than just code—they offer support, compliance, and integrations. Consequently, software companies may be the biggest beneficiaries of AI coding, as they will be able to produce more code more efficiently. However, the history of the internet offers a warning. When distribution costs went to zero, it initially seemed like an opportunity for publishers, but it ultimately destroyed their collective economic power by creating an overwhelming abundance of content. If the cost of code trends toward zero, the SaaS ecosystem could be upended. For the last decade, SaaS companies grew by identifying business functions and building siloed apps. In an era where businesses are cutting spending to fund their own AI initiatives, the growth story for these companies is in question. The next decade will not be about growing the pie, but fighting for it, with model makers acting as the arms dealers. As this battle unfolds, there will be a shift toward agents. Microsoft is positioning its "Work IQ" as a horizontal layer that leverages data across Microsoft 365 to create stateful agents for organizations. This utilizes Microsoft’s control over identity and Active Directory, which has long been the lynchpin of its enterprise strategy. The problem is that if AI reduces the number of human workers, a per-seat licensing model becomes less valuable. This creates an urgency for Microsoft to incorporate more business functions directly into its software to justify higher prices and compel other software makers to interface with its agents. This pressure explains Microsoft’s recent Azure performance. The company clarified that demand still exceeds supply, but it chose to prioritize its own first-party AI services—like Microsoft 365 Copilot and GitHub Copilot—along with internal R&D, over selling capacity to Azure customers. CEO Satya Nadella described this as a "portfolio approach," focusing on the higher lifetime value and margins of productivity applications compared to renting out raw capacity. While this was a strategic decision to protect their long-term position, it resulted in missing the growth rates Wall Street expected. This capacity allocation highlights a broader issue: the downside of chip shortages. If Microsoft cannot meet demand today, the problem could intensify as AI needs expand. Furthermore, Azure customers may grow frustrated by a provider that prioritizes its own products over their needs. This creates an opening for "pure play" token foundries or neoclouds that do not compete with their customers. Despite these challenges, Microsoft’s sheer size and its strategic focus on internal compute suggest it is positioned to be a survivor in this new landscape, even if the road ahead requires unprecedented levels of infrastructure investment.

TL;DR

Capital Group maintains its status as a "gold standard" in investment management by utilizing a unique multi-manager system, prioritizing an eight-year incentive horizon to eliminate short-termism, and operating under a 94-year-old employee-owned structure that ensures intergenerational stability.

The "Capital System" Investment Model

  • The Multi-Manager Approach: Launched in 1958 to eliminate "key person risk," the system divides portfolios among multiple managers and analysts rather than a single individual.
      • The Principal Investment Officer (PIO): Acts as the "conductor of the orchestra," ensuring accountability while allowing various managers to express their highest-conviction ideas.
      • Analysts as Investors: Unlike many firms where analysts merely rate stocks, Capital Group analysts manage real client assets. This "skin in the game" forces them to understand portfolio risk and diversification.
  • Style Consistency: The firm prioritizes managers who avoid "style creep." Gitlin emphasizes that even in bull markets, managers must stick to their defined styles (growth, value, etc.) so that the overall portfolio risk remains intended and balanced.
  • Low Attrition: The average tenure for portfolio managers exceeds 20 years, with investment professional attrition in the low single digits.

Long-Termism and Compensation Structure

  • 8-Year Incentive Horizon: The primary driver of quantitative compensation is an investment professional's rolling 8-year performance record, followed by 5-year and 3-year windows.
      • Reducing Volatility: By making the 1-year number the smallest component of compensation, the firm discourages "hedge fund" behavior and reactive, knee-jerk decision-making.
  • The "Time" Edge: Gitlin argues that the firm’s competitive advantage is simply *time*—the ability to stay with a thesis and go against the grain without fear of quarterly reprisal.
  • Client Alignment: This structure mirrors the goals of their 20 million+ family clients who are saving for retirement decades in the future, rather than focusing on the 24-hour news cycle.

Strategic Evolution: AI, Passive Markets, and Private Equity

  • Active vs. Passive Dynamics: Gitlin notes that as passive management reaches ~50% of the market, benchmarks have become dangerously concentrated. This creates a massive opportunity for active managers to provide true diversification by looking at the "53,000+ public companies" outside the top few.
  • AI and Proprietary Data: Capital Group is leveraging AI to mine 94 years of digitized proprietary research.
      • Investor Insights: AI tools help current investors analyze their past decisions in similar market cycles to identify recurring mistakes or successes.
      • Efficiency: Automated tools like "Prep Me" help advisors synthesize client notes and history for more productive interactions.
  • Hybrid Private Markets: Rather than building or buying a private equity arm, Capital Group partnered with KKR.
      • Avoiding "Practice": Gitlin stated they were unwilling to "practice" on client money while learning the private space; partnering allows them to combine KKR’s 50 years of expertise with Capital’s public market scale.

Culture and Ownership Structure

  • "Rented" Ownership: The firm is 100% employee-owned, but Gitlin describes it as "rented." Owners must sell their shares back to the firm upon retirement.
      • Anti-Concentration: No single individual owns more than approximately 1% of the firm, ensuring that the incentive is to pass a healthy business to the next generation rather than monetize for personal gain.
  • The Hiring "Veto": The recruitment process is intentionally slow, lasting 6 to 12 months. This ensures cultural fit and helps candidates determine if they are truly ready for a career-long commitment.
  • In-Person Collaboration: The firm maintains a four-day in-office week, citing that face-to-face verbal communication is superior to virtual collaboration for deep investment debate.

Our daily life relies on the function of a large number of different molecules, including DNA, RNA, proteins, lipids, and sugars. Structural biology unravels the mechanisms underlying these functions. To illustrate this, consider proteins, which have a very simple chemical structure: they are linear arrays of building blocks with a defined start and end. However, knowing the chemical structure alone is insufficient for understanding their wide range of functions. For example, a common protein structure known as the antiparallel beta sheet or hairpin structure has a strength comparable to steel. In contrast, the alpha helix offers no resistance when pulled, which is why it is found in hair. If ligaments in the knee were composed of alpha helices instead of more rigid structures, it would be a disaster. In drug development, understanding the three-dimensional structure is vital. While a chemical sequence might suggest targeting sequential residues, the 3D structure reveals crossovers that create recognition points far apart in the chemical sequence. These points form the receptor positions where a drug can bind. Today, these structures are determined through techniques like Cryo-EM and X-ray crystallography, which involve freezing proteins in liquid nitrogen to observe them with high precision. Another technique, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, allows researchers to work in solutions resembling body fluids. However, NMR is currently limited to relatively small proteins and suffers from low sensitivity. A major boundary in the field is that most structures are determined from isolated, purified proteins rather than within living systems. The next few decades will see structural biology move toward studying macromolecules *in vivo*. While cryo-electron tomography and NMR spectroscopy of living cells currently provide limited information, there is significant potential in combining NMR spectroscopy with magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). This would merge structural and interaction data with geographical information, marking the future of structural biology through the development of methods that improve both cryo-EM tomography and magnetic resonance techniques. Photoredox catalysis represents another disruptive shift, specifically in how photons can be used for chemistry and biology. This field emerged from the idea of taking knowledge from inorganic chemistry—specifically how to power the planet with solar energy—and exporting it into organic chemistry. Since the initial papers in 2008, the field has grown to produce over 1,900 papers annually. This interest stems from the development of new organic reactions that power the creation of novel drug molecules. For instance, a Rho kinase inhibitor used in neuroscience features a bond that could not be easily made without this technology. Today, industrial infrastructure allows these chemical reactions to be performed on a massive scale using visible light. Beyond chemistry, photons and catalysis are being used to gain insights into biology, specifically biological interaction networks known as interactomes. Most biological molecules, such as proteins, interact dynamically with other proteins, metabolites, and signaling molecules. Many of these interactions remain unknown. By using proximity labeling—placing a photocatalyst on a protein of interest and shining light on it—researchers can map a protein's "footprints" to see everything it interacts with. This has practical applications in medicine, such as mapping the biological differences between patients who are susceptible or resistant to the breast cancer drug Herceptin. This research has already led to the development of a small molecule drug that can "switch on" Herceptin for previously resistant cells. Furthermore, this technology is being used to identify new receptors that allow small peptides to cross the blood-brain barrier and to study the assembly and disassembly of proteins associated with ALS. In the realm of physics, the ten years since LIGO first detected a gravitational wave have seen massive improvements in detection capabilities. LIGO now detects waves from colliding black holes several times a week, a feat achieved through squeezed vacuum technology, also known as squeezed light. This technology was the result of a 50-year struggle. It began in the early 1970s with Rainer Weiss’s invention of the gravity wave detector and Vladimir Braginsky’s warning that gravitational waves might be hidden by the mirrors' quantum fluctuations—a tiny shaking that can never be stopped. Yakov Zeldovich further contributed the concept of vacuum fluctuations, speculating that the Big Bang itself was produced by such fluctuations. In 1981, Carlton Caves realized that vacuum fluctuations of light entering an interferometer could control the mirrors' shaking. By squeezing these fluctuations—reducing one aspect while increasing another—humans could manipulate the vacuum to prevent quantum shaking from hiding gravitational waves. Jeff Kimble later invented the technology for this squeezing using laser-pumped nonlinear crystals. After decades of refinement by research groups worldwide, a mature form of squeezing was implemented in LIGO in 2019, leading to a 50% increase in detections. However, the future of such long-term, government-funded university research is at risk due to political turmoil, necessitating strong international collaboration to ensure no single nation holds an exclusive advantage over such powerful disruptive technologies. This approach has become one of the most active topics in chemistry and materials science, with thousands of papers published and over a million citations. Single-atom catalysts allow for an atomic-scale understanding of heterogeneous catalysis. By providing clear structure-activity and spectrum-structure data, this field is now poised to leverage artificial intelligence for the rational design of new catalysts. Researchers are currently establishing large databases to facilitate this transition. In terms of commercialization, single-atom catalysts are promising for green hydrogen production, carbon dioxide conversion, and nitrogen activation. Because every atom is utilized, the atom efficiency is extremely high, making these low-cost, high-efficiency catalysts essential for achieving carbon neutrality in the future.

The Boardroom Dilemma: Fragmented Responsibility

In most organizations, "Responsible AI" is a fractured concept, with different departments owning only small pieces of the whole, leading to a "soup of contradictory descriptions" for the CEO.

  • The Silo Effect:
      • Legal: Focuses solely on regulatory compliance.
      • Risk: Prioritizes assessment and mitigation.
      • Technical/Data: Focuses on robustness and minimizing "hallucinations."
      • Ethics: Concerned with human dignity and values.
      • Sustainability: Evaluates the environmental impact.
  • The "Green Dashboard" Trap: Even when every department’s specific metric is "green," the system can still fail. For example, a bank might inadvertently discriminate against a customer even if the data, legal, and business dashboards all report success.

Trust vs. Responsibility: A Crucial Distinction

Karine Ysebrant de Lendonck argues that "trust" is an insufficient metric for high-stakes AI deployment because it is passive and relational.

  • Trust as a Feeling: Trust is based on past history and expectations (e.g., "I trust the sun will rise"). It is a passive state that offers no recourse when a system fails.
  • Responsibility as a Commitment: Unlike trust, responsibility is an active moral commitment. It involves "owning the future outcome even if we don't know what that outcome will be."
  • The Failure of Trust: If a self-driving car hits a pedestrian, telling the family "we trusted the algorithm" is an empty, meaningless statement. Responsibility requires someone to stand for the decision before it is made.

The Shift to Agentic AI

AI is no longer just a recommendation tool; it has become "agentic," meaning it executes long chains of actions autonomously, making the traditional "human-in-the-loop" model obsolete.

  • From Moments to Trajectories: In the past, a human could review an AI recommendation (a "moment" of decision). Today, agentic AI manages entire "trajectories"—such as rerouting supply chains, canceling orders, and paying fees—at a speed humans cannot cognitively follow.
  • The End of the Loop: Because machines perform hundreds of decisions a minute, humans can no longer stay "on the loop" for every step. Companies must adapt to governing the *trajectory* rather than the individual decision.

Three Drifts of Misplaced Responsibility

When companies operate in the "grey zone" between humans and machines, they risk three primary types of failure:

  • Fragmentation: Different teams look at isolated dashboards, allowing harm (like discrimination) to occur while everyone believes they are succeeding.
  • Metrics Replacing Judgment: Algorithms optimized for specific metrics can lack context.
      • *Example:* A social media algorithm designed to block 99% of violent content might inadvertently suppress historical news or war reporting because it lacks human judgment.
  • Shift to the Aftermath: A dangerous "we'll fix it later" mindset. While software can be "patched," real-world agentic AI cannot "reboot" a patient who received the wrong medication or a pedestrian hit by an autonomous vehicle.

The Framework: The "Responsibility License"

To avoid the "Triangle of Strategic Failure," leadership must treat responsibility as a mandate for governing permission rather than just controlling models.

  • Grant: Upstream definition of boundaries. Clearly state what the AI is and is not allowed to do before deployment.
  • Guard: Ongoing animation of the conditions required to stay responsible while the AI is active.
  • Pull (The Kill Switch): The absolute necessity of a "revocable" permission. A specific human leader must have the documented mandate to pause or stop a system immediately when questions arise.
