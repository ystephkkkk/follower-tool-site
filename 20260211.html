TL;DR

Microsoft is navigating a fundamental shift where AI-written code commoditizes software, forcing a transition from "growing the pie" to a predatory battle for industry adjacencies. To survive, Microsoft is prioritizing its own high-margin AI agents and R&D over third-party Azure demand, signaling a strategic pivot toward "Work IQ" and integrated utility.

The Software Survival Crisis

  • The End of the SaaS Golden Age: The previous decade of SaaS focused on identifying a business function and building a siloed app. AI-written code collapses this model by lowering the cost of code to near-zero, enabling every software company to attack their neighbors' territory.
  • Commoditization Risks: While businesses are unlikely to write all their own software, the abundance of AI-generated code means software companies must justify their existence through support, compliance, and integrations rather than just the code itself.
  • A Zero-Sum Market: Companies are increasingly cutting software spend to fund their own AI token usage. This shifts the software industry from a period of expansion to a "fight for the pie," where the primary survivors will be those who can leverage AI to rapidly absorb adjacent business functions.

The Azure Miss and Capacity Prioritization

  • Financial Impact: Microsoft experienced its biggest stock plunge since 2020, losing $357 billion in value in a single session—second only to Nvidia’s rout following the DeepSeek launch.
  • Strategic Resource Allocation: The reported "miss" in Azure growth (which fell 1% below expectations) was a deliberate choice. CFO Amy Hood confirmed that Microsoft prioritized GPU capacity for internal R&D and first-party apps like M365 Copilot and GitHub Copilot over renting that capacity to Azure customers.
  • High-Margin Preference: CEO Satya Nadella defended this "portfolio approach," noting that Microsoft earns better margins and higher Lifetime Value (LTV) from its own productivity applications than from pure cloud infrastructure.
  • Infrastructure Stress: Capital expenditures reached a record $37.5 billion in the most recent quarter, a 66% year-over-year increase, highlighting the extreme costs of maintaining the AI lead.

Work IQ and the Future of Agents

  • The Identity Advantage: Microsoft’s "Work IQ" strategy leverages its ownership of identity (Active Directory) to build agents that understand a worker’s role, communications, and history.
  • The Per-Seat Licensing Risk: Microsoft’s current business model relies heavily on per-seat licensing. As AI agents begin to perform the work of humans, the number of "seats" may dwindle, threatening the company's core revenue stream.
  • Adjacency as Defense: To counter the shrinking per-seat market, Microsoft is aggressively incorporating more business functions into its suite. The goal is to make its agents more capable and the software surface area so large that other software makers are forced to interface with Microsoft’s ecosystem.

The Rise of "Token Foundries"

  • The Integrated Fab Problem: Azure currently operates like an "Integrated Device Manufacturer" (e.g., Intel), where it builds the infrastructure but prioritizes its own products during shortages.
  • Customer Conflict: Azure customers (including OpenAI, which represents 45% of Azure’s Remaining Performance Obligations) are effectively "last in line" behind Microsoft's internal needs.
  • Market Opportunity: This creates a strategic opening for "pure play" token foundries—like Oracle or specialized "neoclouds"—that do not compete with their customers and can offer guaranteed capacity, similar to the TSMC model in semiconductors.

Microsoft and Software Survival was published on Tuesday, February 3, 2026. One way to track the AI era, which began with the November 2022 launch of ChatGPT, is to observe which Big Tech company was considered most threatened at any given time. Initially, many were concerned about Google and the potential disruption of Search. Early last year, the focus shifted to Apple as an upgraded Siri struggled to launch. By the fall, Meta was in the crosshairs as the company relaunched its AI efforts following difficulties with Llama. Now, the spotlight has turned to Microsoft. This is a full-circle moment, considering the company was initially viewed as the biggest winner of the ChatGPT era due to its partnership with OpenAI. In early 2023, Microsoft seemed exceptionally well-placed. It possessed a cloud service selling GPUs and served as the exclusive provider for OpenAI. While that infrastructure was expensive, it appeared Microsoft was investing in the foundational architecture of a new epoch. At the time, Bing was compared to the Mac on the eve of the iPhone—a small player with everything to gain by disrupting the status quo. Adding GPT functionality to productivity apps was expected to mirror the success of GitHub Copilot, transforming Microsoft from a poster child of disruption victims into a company reaching new heights because of it. However, much of that early optimism now seems shortsighted. While OpenAI remains Azure’s largest customer, the fact that it represents 45% of Azure’s Remaining Performance Obligations is now viewed by the market as a liability. Bing’s growth prospects seemed to stall after Microsoft tempered the initial, more aggressive "Sydney" AI experience. Furthermore, while all Microsoft products now feature a Copilot, their effectiveness remains unclear as competitors like Claude and OpenAI move into the professional productivity space. Microsoft 365 Copilot has 15 million paying customers, but that is a small fraction of the total user base. Moreover, the rise of AI agents raises fundamental questions about the long-term viability of the per-seat licensing model that sustains Microsoft’s business. These factors, combined with Azure growth falling slightly short of expectations, led to one of the worst days in stock market history for the company. Microsoft shares recently suffered a 10% plunge, wiping out $357 billion in value—the second-largest single-session loss ever. This selloff reflects heightened investor skepticism regarding whether the hundreds of billions spent on AI will eventually pay off. While capital expenditures rose 66% to a record $37.5 billion, growth in the Azure cloud unit has slowed. This raises the question of whether the big tech foundations of the future are beginning to crack. Regarding AI-written code, it can be difficult to separate the hype of "vibe coding" from reality, but the trajectory is clear. Even skeptical software engineers admit that AI helps them write features or applications that would have previously been impossible or too time-consuming. AI-written code provides a nearly perfect match of probabilistic inputs and deterministic outputs; the code must run, and it can be tested. While the role of the software architect remains vital, it seems inevitable that the majority of software will eventually be written by AI. This leads to a bearish question: why pay for software when you can ask an AI to write a custom application? There are reasons to be skeptical of the total commoditization of software. Most companies focus on their core competencies, which rarely include maintaining software. Writing an initial app is only the beginning; there is maintenance, security, and constant updates. Furthermore, software companies provide more than just code—they offer support, compliance, and integrations. Consequently, software companies may be the biggest beneficiaries of AI coding, as they will be able to produce more code more efficiently. However, the history of the internet offers a warning. When distribution costs went to zero, it initially seemed like an opportunity for publishers, but it ultimately destroyed their collective economic power by creating an overwhelming abundance of content. If the cost of code trends toward zero, the SaaS ecosystem could be upended. For the last decade, SaaS companies grew by identifying business functions and building siloed apps. In an era where businesses are cutting spending to fund their own AI initiatives, the growth story for these companies is in question. The next decade will not be about growing the pie, but fighting for it, with model makers acting as the arms dealers. As this battle unfolds, there will be a shift toward agents. Microsoft is positioning its "Work IQ" as a horizontal layer that leverages data across Microsoft 365 to create stateful agents for organizations. This utilizes Microsoft’s control over identity and Active Directory, which has long been the lynchpin of its enterprise strategy. The problem is that if AI reduces the number of human workers, a per-seat licensing model becomes less valuable. This creates an urgency for Microsoft to incorporate more business functions directly into its software to justify higher prices and compel other software makers to interface with its agents. This pressure explains Microsoft’s recent Azure performance. The company clarified that demand still exceeds supply, but it chose to prioritize its own first-party AI services—like Microsoft 365 Copilot and GitHub Copilot—along with internal R&D, over selling capacity to Azure customers. CEO Satya Nadella described this as a "portfolio approach," focusing on the higher lifetime value and margins of productivity applications compared to renting out raw capacity. While this was a strategic decision to protect their long-term position, it resulted in missing the growth rates Wall Street expected. This capacity allocation highlights a broader issue: the downside of chip shortages. If Microsoft cannot meet demand today, the problem could intensify as AI needs expand. Furthermore, Azure customers may grow frustrated by a provider that prioritizes its own products over their needs. This creates an opening for "pure play" token foundries or neoclouds that do not compete with their customers. Despite these challenges, Microsoft’s sheer size and its strategic focus on internal compute suggest it is positioned to be a survivor in this new landscape, even if the road ahead requires unprecedented levels of infrastructure investment.
