TL;DR

Vice President JD Vance swore in Colin McDonald as Assistant Attorney General to lead a newly established Department of Justice (DOJ) division dedicated to a "zero-tolerance" approach toward taxpayer fraud, aiming to restore social trust and fiscal deterrence.

The Anti-Fraud Task Force and Mission

  • Strategic Context: The administration identifies a "fraud crisis" where trillions in taxpayer dollars are at risk due to a breakdown in social trust and government oversight.
      • Leadership: The effort is spearheaded by a dedicated Anti-Fraud Task Force led by Vice President JD Vance.
      • Policy Shift: Speakers criticized previous administrations and "blue states" for allowing billions to reach fraudsters, both domestic and foreign.
  • Preserving the Safety Net: The mission is framed as essential to protecting the social safety net for those who have a legal right to participate in and benefit from these programs.

Establishment of a New DOJ Division

  • New Organizational Structure: JD Vance announced the creation of a specific division within the DOJ focused holistically on eliminating fraud against taxpayers.
      • Departmental Support: The office was established with the cooperation of Attorney General Pam Bondi and Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche.
  • Appointment of Colin McDonald: McDonald, a career prosecutor from California, was selected to lead the division as Assistant Attorney General.
      • Professional Background: McDonald previously prosecuted cases involving fraud, political corruption, and organized crime, and served under the Deputy Attorney General for the past year.

Enforcement Priorities and Deterrence

  • Zero-Tolerance Policy: A primary shift in strategy involves investigating all fraud regardless of the dollar amount.
      • Ending Thresholds: Vance noted that the DOJ previously "turned a blind eye" to fraud under certain dollar amounts; the new division will prosecute both high-level and low-level offenses.
  • Restoring Deterrence: The division’s goal is to ensure potential fraudsters fear the consequences of their actions, specifically the threat of federal prosecution and jail time.
      • Application Scrutiny: McDonald emphasized a particular focus on identifying and prosecuting false applications for government funds.

Legislative and Political Context

  • Senate Confirmation: McDonald was confirmed by the U.S. Senate, receiving unanimous Republican support but limited support from Democrats.
  • Executive Leadership: The briefing highlighted that this specific focus on taxpayer fraud is a cornerstone of the President's broader directive to improve government efficiency and accountability.

The Normalization of Privacy Exploitation

  • Personal Context: Prince Harry links his lifelong experience with media intrusion to the broader digital privacy crisis, noting he has spent seven years in litigation against three UK media organizations for systemic, unlawful invasions of privacy dating back to the early 2000s.
  • Commercialization of Extraction: He argues that the exploitation of personal data has moved beyond the media into a "normalized" commercial tactic used by tech giants to fuel social media platforms and AI innovations.
  • Structural Surrender: Harry challenges the current definition of digital consent.
      • When participation in modern life requires agreeing to non-negotiable terms, consent becomes a "formality, not a safeguard."
      • Companies often conflate "repeated use" of a platform with "trust," ignoring the fact that users often have no realistic choice but to opt in.

The Human Cost and Algorithmic Harm

  • Impact on Youth: Through his work in philanthropy and business, Harry highlights "harrowing stories" of technology leading to irreversible harms, including eating disorders, drug overdoses, bullying, and suicide.
  • The "Blame Shifting" Narrative: He rejects the industry claim that tech-related harms are the result of "parenting failure" or a "lack of discipline," asserting instead that systems are intentionally designed to capture and hold attention through precise behavioral prediction.
  • Case Study in Accountability:
      • Legal Precedents: He cites recent successful lawsuits against Meta and YouTube as evidence that platforms are built to exploit rather than protect.
      • Access Barriers: He highlights the plight of parents who have had to wait over five years to gain access to a deceased child’s phone due to the platforms’ own restrictive privacy rules, which protected the data but shielded the harmful content that led to the child's death.

Reframing Innovation and Regulation

  • Engineering Trust: Harry disputes the tech industry’s claim that "guardrails" stifle innovation.
      • He compares the tech sector to aviation, medicine, and finance, industries that only became scalable and transformative because safety and trust were engineered into their core systems.
  • Safety by Design: He contrasts "Privacy by Design" with recent industry slogans like "Privacy by Experimentation," which he labels as "slick slogans with a sinister undertone."
  • Upstream Accountability: He calls for companies to stop leaning on "downstream complexities" (the difficulty of managing content) to avoid "upstream accountability" (the responsibility for how the system was designed).

The AI Inflection Point and Global Leadership

  • A Rare Opportunity: As AI begins to reshape society, Harry argues we have a unique chance to reverse the "build first, govern later" approach of the last 20 years.
      • Proactive Architecture: Accountability and safety must be embedded into the architecture of AI from the start, rather than retrofitting them after harm has occurred.
  • Global Influence: Speaking in Washington D.C., he notes that American tech standards define the digital environment globally. He calls for:
      • Data Sovereignty: Choosing user control over data surveillance.
      • Human-Centered Leadership: A move toward "Moral Leadership" and "EQ" in tech to ensure innovation benefits the public rather than just consolidating wealth.

TL;DR

The Supreme Court heard arguments on whether the 14th Amendment’s Citizenship Clause permits the executive branch to deny birthright citizenship to children of undocumented immigrants and temporary visitors based on a "domicile" and "allegiance" requirement. The case hinges on whether the phrase "subject to the jurisdiction thereof" refers to simple obedience to U.S. laws (*jus soli*) or a deeper political allegiance and lawful residency (*jus sanguinis*).

The Core Legal Dispute: Jurisdiction and Domicile

  • The Government’s "Domicile" Theory: General Sauer argued that the 14th Amendment presupposes a legal domicile—lawful presence combined with the intent to remain—to establish the requisite "allegiance" for citizenship.
      • Allegiance Requirement: The government contends that "jurisdiction" in the 1860s meant more than being subject to police power; it required a total political allegiance, which illegal aliens and temporary "sojourners" cannot legally establish.
      • Exclusion of Classes: Under this theory, children of birth tourists and those in the country illegally would not be citizens because their parents lack the legal capacity to form a domicile.
  • The "Universal Rule" Counter-Argument: Ms. Wang, representing the respondents, argued that the 14th Amendment codified the English common law rule of *jus soli*, where birth on the soil confers citizenship regardless of parental status, with very narrow exceptions.
      • Limited Exceptions: These exceptions traditionally only include children of foreign diplomats, invading enemy armies, and (historically) tribal Indians, all of whom were viewed as being "under a fiction of extraterritoriality."

Interpretation of Precedent: *Wong Kim Ark* (1898)

  • The Role of Domicile: A central point of contention was the 1898 case *United States v. Wong Kim Ark*.
      • General Sauer's View: Pointed out that the term "domicile" appears over 20 times in the *Wong Kim Ark* opinion, suggesting the Court's ruling was limited specifically to the children of *domiciled* aliens.
      • Ms. Wang's View: Argued that while domicile was a stipulated fact in that case, it was not the *holding*. She asserted the controlling rule of decision was the common law, which ignores domicile in favor of the location of birth.
  • Judicial Skepticism: Justice Kagan and Justice Sotomayor questioned why the Court should adopt a "revisionist" history that contradicts a century of understanding. Justice Kagan noted that most of the government’s brief focused on temporary visitors, while the immediate policy target is undocumented persons.

Originalist Context and the 1866 Civil Rights Act

  • Textual Shifts: Justice Kavanaugh and Justice Alito explored the differences between the Civil Rights Act of 1866 (which used the phrase "not subject to any foreign power") and the 14th Amendment ("subject to the jurisdiction thereof").
      • Foreign Power Clause: Justice Alito suggested that a child born to foreign nationals remains "subject to a foreign power" (such as potential military conscription in their parents' home country), which might exclude them under the 1866 Act's logic.
  • The Intent of the Framers: General Sauer cited Senator Lyman Trumbull, a primary framer, who stated that jurisdiction meant "not owing allegiance to anybody else." Conversely, Ms. Wang cited contemporaneous treatises and members of Congress who believed the amendment was intended to be a broad, bright-line rule.

Practical Consequences and Policy Implications

  • Birth Tourism and "Pull Factors": General Sauer argued that unrestricted birthright citizenship has created a "sprawling industry" of birth tourism (specifically mentioning hotspots in China and Russia) and acts as a magnet for illegal immigration.
  • Implementation Challenges: Justice Jackson expressed concern over the "logistics of the delivery room," asking how hospitals would determine a parent's subjective "intent to stay" or immigration status at the moment of birth.
      • Government Response: General Sauer claimed the process would be "transparent" for most, using existing DHS databases to verify status, similar to how Social Security numbers are currently generated.
  • The Risk of Statelessness: Justice Sotomayor and Ms. Wang warned that the government's theory could lead to "thousands of American babies" losing citizenship and could potentially be applied retroactively, despite the government’s request for prospective-only relief.

      • Development-Heavy Cuts: Reductions were concentrated in engineering, product, and design (EPD), where AI gains are highest.
      • Protected Areas: Compliance, regulatory, and sales teams saw minimal cuts due to the high risk and nuanced nature of those roles.

Internal Infrastructure: The Agentic Operating System

  • Goose (Agent Harness): A model-agnostic substrate developed by Block that allows the company to swap between 120+ different models (OpenAI, Anthropic, open-source) depending on the task. It serves as the foundation for both internal tools and customer-facing products.
  • Builder Bot: An internal autonomous tool that builds features and merges Pull Requests (PRs).
      • Efficiency: Builder Bot frequently completes 85% to 100% of a feature’s code, with humans acting as "final-mile" editors.
  • G2 (Agentic OS): An internal-only system that allows any employee to automate deterministic workflows.
  • Shift in Workflow: Engineering has moved from a linear PR-review-merge cycle to a parallelized system where a single developer might oversee 14 different agents building PRs simultaneously.

Organizational Redesign: From Hierarchies to Squads

  • The "Small Squad" Model: Block moved from a functional hierarchy (e.g., 14-person teams with eight server engineers and four client engineers) to "squads" of one to six people.
  • Flattening the Org: On the development side, Block cut management layers by 50% to 60%, resulting in only two or three layers between the executive team and the "builders."
  • The Demise of Meetings: To facilitate rapid building, Block reduced internal meetings by 70% to 80%, allowing teams to focus on output over coordination.
  • Role Fluidity: In the new structure, the lines between roles are blurring. Most designers and Product Managers (PMs) at Block are now expected to ship PRs directly to GitHub.

Product Innovation: Generative UI and Proactive Intelligence

  • From Static to Generative UI: Jennings predicts the end of rigid app interfaces. Instead of every user seeing the same layout, "Generative UI" creates custom interfaces, charts, and visualizations on the fly based on individual user data.
  • Money Bot (Cash App): Built on the Goose harness, this serves as a "CFO in your pocket" for users, proactively suggesting financial moves and generating custom data visualizations that do not exist in the hardcoded source.
  • Manager Bot (Square): Allows merchants to build custom business applications via natural language. For example, a restaurant owner can ask the bot to create a custom employee scheduling app that integrates with WhatsApp.
  • QA Challenges: Moving to non-deterministic, generative outputs for tens of millions of users creates significant Quality Assurance (QA) hurdles that the company is currently solving through "proactive intelligence" filters.

New Moats in the AI Era

  • The "Vibecoding" Risk: While simple apps can now be "vibecoded" (built entirely by AI) in a week, Jennings argues that distribution, network effects, and hardware remain defensive moats.
  • Deep Context as a Moat: The ultimate defense is a company’s unique understanding of complex signals—in Block’s case, the interplay between buyers and sellers.
  • Iteration Speed: Defensibility now depends on how many times a company can run the "feedback loop" between their proprietary data and their AI builders. Companies that can iterate thousands of times a day via agents will outpace those relying on human-speed development.
