TL;DR

AI has unlocked an 8x increase in code shipping velocity at Anthropic, shifting the engineering bottleneck from writing code to verifying it. To navigate this rapid evolution, engineering organizations must transition to asynchronous "just-in-time" planning, foster high-agency cultures, and actively combat the "loneliness epidemic" of solo agent-driven development.

The 8x Developer and the Verification Bottleneck

  • The Post-Coding Era: Coding is no longer the primary bottleneck in software creation. The widespread adoption of AI tools has lifted the ceiling of developer capability, shifting the focus from execution to ambition.
      • The 8x Velocity Jump: Anthropic engineers ship an average of eight times more code per quarter today than they did during the 2021–2025 baseline.
      • The Verification Shift: Because the volume of code output has scaled exponentially, human reviews have become a major bottleneck. Engineering has shifted from a writing discipline to a "trust but verify" validation discipline.
  • Automated Testing and Evals: To keep pace with 8x code velocity, teams must build automated frameworks of "what good looks like" directly into repositories.
      • AI-Driven TDD: Test-Driven Development (TDD) has been revitalized by AI. Developers can instruct Claude Code to write failing tests first, then write the code and verify the pass, eliminating the traditional developer "tax" of manual test creation.
  • Asynchronous Workflows and Routines: Work is transitioning from synchronous prompting to automated, asynchronous agent fleets.
      • Manager Cron Jobs: Leaders can use "Routines" (automated agents run on schedule, like a cron job) to monitor Slack feedback channels overnight, analyze trends, and present fully formed PRs/summaries for review by morning.
      • Context-Switching Cost: Running multiple concurrent agents increases the cognitive load of context-switching, requiring leaders to explicitly block out focus time to review asynchronous outputs.

Restructuring the Team: "Dreamers," Systems Experts, and Player-Coaches

  • Two Critical Hiring Profiles: Fung identifies two main archetypes required for modern, AI-pilled engineering teams:
      • Creative Builders with Product Sense: The "dreamers" who possess the ambition to conceptualize products end-to-end, iterate rapidly based on user feedback, and polish user experiences.
      • Deep Systems Experts: High-level specialists who understand the lower layers of the stack and can perform the highly technical verification that models might miss.
  • The Player-Coach Manager Model: Fung mandates that new managers at Anthropic start as Individual Contributors (ICs) before taking on people-management responsibilities.
      • Onboarding with AI: New managers can use Claude as an "onboarding buddy" to ask questions about the codebase, run automated tests, and gain the confidence to ship PRs without distracting human teammates.
      • Maintaining Technical Touch: Even at scale, leaders must continue writing lightweight code or executing manual testing to stay close to the flow of the product and retain operational empathy.
  • Role Blurring: Traditional boundaries between engineering, product management, design, and data science are dissolving.
      • PMs Shipping Code: With AI assistance, PMs can actively check in code and ship features when engineering bandwidth is constrained.
      • The Data Science Shift: Data scientists increasingly transition from running primary analyses to auditing and correcting AI-generated data science work submitted by non-technical team members.

Combating Loneliness, Fear, and the AI Divide

  • The Loneliness of Agent-Driven Engineering: A hidden cost of the AI transition is the isolation that occurs when engineers spend their days interacting primarily with AI agents rather than teammates.
      • Pairwise Programming Lunches: To rebuild connection, Anthropic teams host pairwise programming lunches—similar to the concept of "parallel play" in childhood—where engineers work side-by-side to watch and learn from each other's distinct AI workflows.
      • Team Hackathons: Regular team-wide hackathons are leveraged to foster real-time human collaboration and creative friction.
  • Addressing the AI Resistance: The gap is widening between engineers who embrace AI and those who fight it out of frustration or underlying fear.
      • The Growth Mindset: Success in the current landscape requires a willingness to abandon the old skills that previously made an engineer successful.
      • Focusing on Control: To counter the fear of displacement, leaders should encourage anxious engineers to identify what remains within their immediate control and take one active step toward building proficiency.
  • Bridging the Equitable AI Divide: Tech-forward leaders have a responsibility to democratize AI literacy outside of the software bubble, particularly for small businesses and less tech-literate communities.
      • Claude for Small Business: Anthropic developed a simplified small-business toggle inside Cowork to help non-technical users automate back-office operations like invoicing, document organization, and localized competitor price analyses.

Modern Product Management: "JIT" Planning and "Bad vs. Sad" Quality

  • Just-In-Time (JIT) Planning: Traditional 6-month roadmap documents are too slow for the current pace of AI development. Anthropic has shifted to JIT monthly planning.
      • Weightless Roadmaps: Plans are kept on lightweight spreadsheets, focusing strictly on top monthly priorities, with weekly check-ins to verify if the priorities are still relevant.
      • Permission to Kill Process: Teams must possess explicit permission to ruthlessly kill processes (like bloated planning cycles) that no longer serve the immediate needs of the product.
  • The "Bad" vs. "Sad" Quality Framework: To manage the qualitative user experience across fast-moving product surfaces, teams categorize bugs into a simple mental model:
      • "Bad" Errors: Severe, irrecoverable system failures (e.g., CLI crashes, data loss).
      • "Sad" Errors: Annoying but recoverable user-experience friction points (e.g., UI flickering). Stacking up too many "sads" eventually degrades the product to "bad."
      • Decentralized Thresholds: Teams are given high agency to define what constitutes a "bad" or "sad" experience for their specific feature set.
  • Extreme Dogfooding over Dashboard Metrics: Metrics must never replace first-hand product intuition.
      • The Scopes of Anecdotal Evidence: While data dashboards are useful, product leaders must actively use and break their own tools (e.g., experiencing live scams on Facebook Marketplace or slow LTE loading screens in Chile) to unearth growth blocks that macro data obscures.

Overview of AMNC 2026: "Innovating at Scale"

  • Event Context: The World Economic Forum’s 17th Annual Meeting of the New Champions (AMNC26) is hosted in Dalian, China, at its futuristic, custom-built congress center, alternating annually with the city of Tianjin.
  • The "New Champions" Concept:
      • Future Orientation: The summit serves as a hub for experimentation, highlighting the next frontiers of science, technology, and economic growth.
      • Focus Sectors: A strong cohort of physical AI, robotics, and biomanufacturing "unicorns" from China and broader Asia are featured.
  • Diverse Global Representation:
      • Political Leaders: Over 1,700 leaders are in attendance, including cabinet ministers and heads of state from Bangladesh, Mongolia, Guinea, Montenegro, and the Prime Minister of South Korea.
      • Theme Objectives: The core theme of "Innovating at Scale" addresses how to transition from exponential technological advancement to practical deployment that impacts the real economy, industry, and society.

Navigating a Contested Geopolitical Landscape

  • US-China Dynamics: Despite ongoing geoeconomic friction, the US and China remain deeply interconnected. The summit features dedicated sessions on navigating this relationship, featuring prominent scholars such as Graham Allison.
  • The Middle East and the Strait of Hormuz:
      • Economic Pressures: Leaders are analyzing the economic fallout of the Strait of Hormuz crisis, particularly regarding inflated energy prices and global inflationary pressures.
      • Long-Term Strategy: Discussions aim to address the structural "end game" for the Persian Gulf and how global businesses must adapt to changing trade realities in the region.

The Energy Transition: Short-Term Vulnerability vs. Long-Term Security

  • The Short-Term Fossil Fuel Pivot:
      • Infrastructure Limits: Energy shocks often force Southeast and South Asian markets to temporarily increase reliance on coal, oil, and gas due to lack of immediate alternative infrastructure.
      • Insulated Markets: China (due to decades of strategic energy planning and rapid EV electrification) and Brazil (due to domestic biofuels production) have remained relatively insulated from recent energy price spikes.
  • The Risk of a Future Supply Glut:
      • Diversification Impacts: Global energy majors are rapidly diversifying oil and gas extraction outside of the Middle East, with major investments in Africa, North America, and South America (including Argentina, Brazil, and Venezuela).
      • Price Suppression: If the Strait of Hormuz stabilizes while these new global supplies come online, the world could see a massive oil and gas surplus in 2 to 5 years, driving prices down and severely undermining the economic incentive to transition to cleaner alternatives.
  • Capital Realignment and Long-Term Clean Energy Acceleration:
      • Shift in Investment Risk: High interest rates and policy uncertainty are currently driving capital toward lower-risk, shorter-payback projects (like domestic grids and LNG) while stalling high-cost, less-mature technologies like offshore wind and clean hydrogen.
      • The Security Driver: Despite short-term setbacks, energy-importing nations are actively moving away from fossil fuels to bolster national security. Modeling indicates that long-term, national security concerns will serve as a primary driver for domestic renewables and nuclear energy deployment.
