TL;DR

Cowboy Space Corporation, led by Robinhood co-founder Baiju Bhatt, is developing vertically integrated, space-based data centers to solve Earth's AI energy crisis by deploying orbital computing hubs that utilize unfiltered solar power. By bypassing terrestrial infrastructure delays, the company aims to launch its first proprietary rocket-turned-satellite by the end of 2028, shifting AI compute capacity scaling timelines from years to weeks.

***

The Transition from Fintech to Frontier Tech

  • Childhood NASA Influence:
    • Bhatt grew up around the NASA Langley Research Center in Virginia, where his father worked as a physicist. Experiencing the sheer scale of NASA's wind tunnels and machinery left a lasting impression of awe, but also a sense of stagnation regarding the post-Apollo space era.
  • The Capitalism Throughline:
    • Democratizing Finance to Democratizing Space: Just as Robinhood aimed to shift generational viewpoints by putting the power of financial capitalism into consumers' pockets, Bhatt wants to expand the "commercial envelope" of space.
    • Moving Past Government Constraints: Historically, the space industry has operated "by the government, for the government, at government prices." Bhatt believes injecting commercial capitalism into the sector will inspire top-tier engineering talent to choose careers in aerospace over traditional corporate paths.

Cowboy Space Corporation’s Orbital Architecture

  • The Core Mission:
    • Solving the AI Energy Crisis: Cowboy Space Corporation (formerly known as Aetherflux) is focused on providing the massive energy required to power next-generation artificial intelligence from orbit.
  • Rocket-to-Satellite Integration:
    • The Rocket is the Data Center: Rather than launching discrete, containerized satellites inside a rocket's fairing, Cowboy Space utilizes a highly differentiated architecture where the rocket itself becomes the satellite and the data center.
    • Unfolding Radiators: Once in orbit, the rocket’s fairing is designed to unfold, transforming into a massive thermal radiator to cool the internal AI chipsets.
    • Vertical Integration: The company is building its own launch vehicles, rockets, and orbital computing payloads in-house to maintain complete control over the stack.

Overcoming Space Environment Challenges

  • Mitigating Hardware Failures:
    • No Orbital Technicians: Because physical repairs are economically unviable in space, the company is collaborating with chip manufacturers to source native, space-rated hardware designed to survive harsh radiation and thermal environments.
    • Over-Provisioning Strategy: Rather than swapping out failed GPUs, the architecture relies on over-provisioning—installing excess compute capacity upfront based on highly researched hardware failure rates.
    • Rigorous Ground Testing: Payloads undergo intense environmental shock, vibration, and thermal vacuum chamber (TVAC) testing before launch.
  • Latency Expectations:
    • Low Earth Orbit (LEO) Altitudes: The constellation is planned for LEO at an altitude of approximately 800 to 1,000 kilometers.
    • Acceptable Delay: Latency is projected to be around tens to 100 milliseconds, which Bhatt notes is highly serviceable for AI inference tasks and various orbital workloads.
    • Ground Uplinks: High-speed data links will connect directly to receivers placed adjacent to, or on top of, existing terrestrial data centers.
  • The Orbital Advantage:
    • Abundant Power: Satellites capture pure, unfiltered solar energy before it enters Earth's atmosphere, avoiding interruptions from weather, cloud cover, and day-night cycles.
    • Rapid Scalability: Building a new data center on Earth takes upwards of five years due to grid capacity and land acquisition. Once operational, Cowboy Space aims to scale net-new orbital compute capacity in a matter of weeks.

Road to 2028: Rocket Engine Production

  • Ambitious Timeline:
    • Target Launch: The company has set a target to launch its first fully in-house developed rocket by the end of 2028.
  • Prioritizing Propulsion:
    • Focus on Engines: The immediate priority for the company is the development and production of its proprietary rocket engines.
    • Seattle Expansion: Cowboy Space is actively building out its engine development team and establishing production facilities in the Seattle, Washington area.

TL;DR

The U.S. defense sector is undergoing a massive shift as $66 billion in venture capital disrupts an over-consolidated, slow-moving military-industrial complex, while a parallel nuclear renaissance is driving overlooked growth for specialty suppliers.

The Great Defense Shake-Up: Silicon Valley vs. Legacy Primes

  • The Atrophied Legacy Base: The U.S. defense industry consolidated from over 60 prime contractors in the 1980s to just five today, following the Pentagon's famous 1993 "Last Supper" meeting.
      • Cost-Plus Inefficiencies: These five primes capture 50% of the Department of Defense's procurement budget but operate on slow "cost-plus" models that prioritize complex, ultra-expensive "exquisite" platforms rather than speed and volume.
      • Munitions Depletion: Recent conflicts have exposed extreme supply chain vulnerabilities. The U.S. is currently firing $4 million Patriot interceptor missiles to take out cheap $20,000 Iranian-designed Shahed drones, and restarting atrophied supply chains (like Stinger missiles) takes years.
  • The Silicon Valley Influx: Between 2020 and 2024, venture capital and private equity funneled $66 billion into defense tech upstarts.
      • Commercial-First Mindset: Companies like the privately held Anduril Industries (valued at $14 billion) build tactical hardware and autonomous aircraft on their own dime before pitching to the Pentagon, bypassing slow government R&D cycles.
      • The Pentagon Shift: Under leadership beneath Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth, including business figures like Emil Michael and Stephen Feinberg, the Pentagon is actively pushing for a faster, commercial-style acquisition process.

The Sum-of-the-Parts Case to Break Up Lockheed Martin

  • The F-35 Drag: The F-35 fighter jet represents 30% of Lockheed Martin’s (LMT) revenue. While it is the world's premier fighter, it costs $80 million+ per plane and $100,000 per hour to operate. It took over 20 years to build a global inventory of just 1,100 aircraft.
  • The Threat of Drone Mission Creep: By the 2030s, cheap autonomous drones will threaten the F-35's market share. For short-distance defense scenarios (e.g., the 117-mile strait between China and Taiwan), cheap, expendable drone swarms are far more practical than manned stealth jets.
  • Unlocking 50% to 100% Value: Analyst Peter Arment argues that breaking Lockheed Martin into three standalone public entities would unlock massive shareholder value:
      • Missiles & Missile Defense Co: Would trade at a premium multiple due to skyrocketing global demand for air defense.
      • Space Co: Consisting of highly classified, high-multiple national security satellite portfolios.
      • RemainCo (Aeronautics & Sikorsky): A slow-decaying, high-dividend cash cow suited for value investors.
  • Northrop Grumman's Superior Portfolio: Among the major primes, Northrop Grumman (NOC) holds the most durable portfolio, with long-term contracts for the B-21 stealth bomber, the Minuteman nuclear ballistic missile replacement, and a third of the F-35's internal systems.

Non-Prime Public Stocks and Drone Disruptors

  • AeroVironment (AVAV): The pure-play drone manufacturer behind the "Switchblade" loitering munition ($150,000 per shot). AVAV recently merged with BlueHalo, doubling its size and adding directed-energy (laser) weapons like the LOCUST system, which can fry a drone's electronics from 2 kilometers away in 2.5 seconds.
  • BWX Technologies (BWXT): Beyond nuclear propulsion, BWXT is a major player in "Group 3" tactical drones (sub-$100,000 units with a 1,500-mile range). These provide crucial "standoff capability," allowing forces to launch precision strikes from a safe distance without risking high-value aircraft.
  • Curtiss-Wright (CW): A highly successful private equity roll-up of critical component manufacturers. It functions as a pure-play supplier for missile defense, space launch, and aerospace, trading at a premium valuation of roughly 40x EBITDA.

The Nuclear Renaissance Powering Specialty Suppliers

  • Power Grid Pressure: Artificial intelligence, data centers, and the European energy crisis have triggered a major nuclear energy revival. Over 50% of the 94 commercial nuclear reactors in the U.S. are currently applying for 20-year lifetime extensions (pushing operational lifespans from 60 to 80 years).
  • Curtiss-Wright’s Near-Monopoly: The company produces the massive, high-margin reactor coolant pumps ($30 million each) required for Westinghouse’s modern AP1000 nuclear plants.
      • The Revenue Opportunity: With 14 AP1000 plants in development globally (each requiring four pumps), Curtiss-Wright has a highly profitable $1.7 billion pipeline locked in, yielding 25% to 30% operating margins.
  • BWX Technologies’ Navy Moat: BWXT holds a functional monopoly on manufacturing the nuclear reactors used to power the U.S. Navy's submarines and aircraft carriers. The company is also poised to benefit from commercial Small Modular Reactors (SMRs) funded by tech hyperscalers seeking clean, off-grid power for data centers.

Commercial Aerospace and the Diluted Recovery of Boeing

  • The Aerospace Fleet Duopoly: Global air traffic correlates strongly with GDP (historically growing at ~5%), meaning the global fleet of commercial aircraft doubles every 15 years.
  • Boeing's "Bean-Counter" Legacy: Following its 1997 merger with McDonnell Douglas, Boeing’s management prioritized cost-cutting. Rather than spending 5 years and billions of dollars developing a clean-sheet plane to counter Airbus, they opted to put larger engines on the legacy 737 frame, resulting in the flawed 737 Max software and subsequent crashes.
  • Diluted Free Cash Flow: Boeing was forced to issue massive amounts of equity to shore up its balance sheet during the crisis. While it was once projected to reach $40 in free cash flow per share, dilution has capped that potential to the low $20s. However, because there are no expensive new airplane development cycles on the horizon, Boeing remains a strong multi-year cash-generation story with a target price of $300 to $400.
