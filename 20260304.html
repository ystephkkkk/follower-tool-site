TL;DR

Temasek is rebalancing its \$340 billion portfolio toward a 50/50 split between liquid and illiquid assets to maintain agility amid frequent macro shocks, prioritizing AI adoption and global equities while navigating high interest rates and geopolitical volatility.

Portfolio Transformation and Global Diversification

  • Shift from Singapore-Centric to Global: Under a 20-year transformation, Temasek has reduced its exposure to Singaporean assets from 97% to 41% of its Net Asset Value (NAV).
      • Current Composition: The portfolio is balanced across Singapore companies (41%), global investments (30%), and third-party funds/partnerships (23%).
  • Equities Dominance: Temasek remains primarily an equities house, with 97% of its portfolio invested in public or private equity.
  • Geographic Expansion: Capital is now distributed across the US, Europe, and Asia to achieve a balance between developed and emerging markets.

Liquidity Strategy and Market Agility

  • The 50/50 Liquidity Target: After shifting heavily toward private markets in the last decade (reaching 55% illiquid), the fund is now targeting a 50/50 split between liquid and illiquid assets.
  • The "Liquidity Premium": In a high-interest-rate environment where the time to monetization for private assets has stretched beyond a decade, CEO Dilhan Pillay Sandrasegara argues there is a premium in being liquid to pivot strategies during shocks.
  • Responding to Volatility: Increased liquidity allows the fund to react to frequent "catalysts" such as AI disruption, oil shocks, and geopolitical conflicts.
      • Example: While some holdings like airlines and power generation are sensitive to oil prices, the broader portfolio is structured to be relatively inoculated from energy price spikes.

Technology and AI Investment Thesis

  • Adoption vs. Disruption: Temasek looks for "mission-critical" companies that use AI to accentuate their business models rather than those merely trying to survive disruption.
  • Key Sectors for AI:
      • Healthcare: Strong focus on AI’s impact on drug discovery and development within pharma and biotech.
      • Software: Identifying companies that are "mission-critical" and can integrate AI to protect their moats.
      • Convergence: The fund sees significant opportunity in the intersection of AI and climate tech adoption to drive the green transition.
  • The "Mag 7" and Deep Tech: The fund views certain large-cap tech innovators as long-term "compounders" that can be held for 10+ years in public markets.

Risk Management and Private Credit

  • Private Credit as a Signal: While private credit is only 2% of the portfolio (~$7B USD), it serves as a critical "market signal" for potential stress in the equity layers of capital structures.
      • Growth Cap: Private credit may grow to 5% of the portfolio, but the firm does not intend to change its "equities DNA."
  • Geopolitical Sanguinity: Pillay notes that markets currently appear relatively sanguine regarding geopolitical risks (e.g., Middle East conflict), with minimal movement in the S&P 500 despite significant global tensions.
  • Currency Exposure: 41% of the portfolio is directly exposed to the US Dollar.
      • Hedging Strategy: Because the cost of hedging has risen to 2.5%–2.6%, the fund is relying more on "natural hedges"—investing in assets that outpace expected dollar depreciation—rather than tactical currency hedging.

TL;DR

Vinod Khosla predicts that by 2040, AI and robotics will usher in an era of extreme abundance where the necessity for human labor is eliminated, requiring a radical restructuring of global tax policies and a shift from economic efficiency to social equity.

The 2040 Vision: From Survival to Abundance

  • The Obsolescence of Labor: Khosla predicts that within 15 years, the need for work as a means of survival will disappear.
      • Job Market Shift: He asserts that a five-year-old today will likely never look for a traditional job, as AI will be capable of performing 80% of current human roles by 2030.
      • Free Expertise: Access to specialized knowledge—from oncology to legal advice—will become globally free and ubiquitous.
      • End of "Servitude": Careers involving repetitive physical labor (e.g., assembly lines, farm work) will be replaced by robotics costing only a few hundred dollars a month.
  • Redefining Human Purpose: Society will move from a "survival-first" mindset to one centered on "following your passion."
      • AI is expected to free humans to focus on creative endeavors, caregiving for children and the elderly, and personal fulfillment.

Economic Disruption and Structural Policy Reform

  • The Deflationary Economy: Khosla describes a future where the cost of living drops precipitously.
      • Declining Prices: Goods and services—including education, healthcare (excluding interventional surgery), and energy—will become near-free to produce and consume.
      • GDP Limitations: Traditional GDP will become an ineffective measure of growth because it does not account for the massive value created by declining prices in a competitive economy.
  • Proposed Tax Reforms: To prevent massive wealth disparity, Khosla advocates for shifting the tax burden from labor to capital.
      • Income Tax Threshold: He proposes a policy where no individual making under $100,000 per year pays income tax.
      • Capital Gains Realignment: To fund this, he suggests eliminating the concept of capital gains and taxing all investment returns as ordinary income.
      • Wealth Funds: Nations may need to adopt models similar to Norway’s oil fund to redistribute the wealth generated by AI and robotics.

Geopolitics and the "Techno-Economic War"

  • The Race with China: Khosla characterizes the AI competition between the U.S. and China as a "techno-economic war" that will determine global socioeconomic influence for decades.
      • Strategic Necessity: He argues that whoever wins the AI race will dictate which political system—democracy or centralized authoritarianism—prevails globally.
      • OpenAI’s Role: Khosla’s $50 million investment in OpenAI (his largest in 40 years) was driven by the conviction that the West needed a viable alternative to state-backed AI efforts in China.
  • Competitive Advantages: While the lead in AI models shifts frequently, Khosla believes the long-term winners will be determined by who possesses the most "compute" and who places the largest long-term research bets.

Enterprise Implementation and AI Agents

  • Prioritizing Use Cases: Jason Girzadas (CEO, Deloitte US) emphasizes that CEOs should focus on high-impact, "un-glamorous" areas ripe for automation, such as finance, expense management, and working capital.
  • The "AI Intern" Phase: Khosla suggests an interim period where every professional will manage four "AI interns," training them to leverage their own expertise before the agents become fully autonomous.
  • Accountability and KPIs: Girzadas notes that successful AI integration requires:
      • Intentionality across specific functional areas.
      • Mainstream business planning with dedicated budgets.
      • Real accountability for business outcomes through specific KPIs.

TL;DR

KKR Co-CEO Scott Nuttall anticipates a "K-shaped" divergence in the private equity industry, where firms practicing disciplined "linear deployment" will thrive while those who over-leveraged during the bull market face consolidation. Despite global volatility, KKR remains aggressive with $118 billion in "dry powder," viewing market corrections as high-value entry points for long-term returns.

The "K-Shaped" Industry Divergence

  • Industry Maturity: The alternative asset management industry has surged from $3 trillion in 2010 to roughly $15 trillion in 2025. Nuttall notes that the "benign environment" of low rates and high multiples is over, revealing which firms made sound decisions versus those who "confused a bull market with brains."
  • Consolidation and Survival: Nuttall predicts a shake-out where some firms will disappear or be forced into "apology funds" (significantly smaller subsequent fundraisings).
      • Strategic Winners: Success will be defined by those who avoided over-deployment in 2021 and maintained global diversification.
      • M&A Trends: While mega-mergers between two giant alternative firms are unlikely due to LP overlap and cultural friction, Nuttall expects more acquisitions by traditional players (like BlackRock) looking to enter the alternatives space.

Investment Discipline and "Linear Deployment"

  • The 20% Rule: KKR avoids the mistake of over-deploying during peak years (a lesson learned from 2006–2007). The firm focuses on "linear deployment," aiming to invest roughly 20% of a fund per year over a five-year cycle.
  • Portfolio Construction: Success in the current environment relies on three pillars:
      • Through-cycle investing (maintaining pace regardless of macro noise).
      • Geographic diversity (over half of KKR’s professionals are outside the U.S.).
      • Strict portfolio construction to weather higher inflation and shifting interest rates.

Market Volatility as an Opportunity

  • Capitalizing on "Dry Powder": KKR currently holds $118 billion in dry powder. Nuttall views market volatility and cheaper valuations as "a good day" because the firm's capital is locked in and cannot be pulled away.
  • Equity vs. Credit Markets:
      • Emotional vs. Sober: Nuttall describes the equity market as an "emotional friend" prone to panic, while the credit market is the "sober-minded friend."
      • Credit Resiliency: Despite "PTSD" from the 2008 financial crisis, Nuttall argues that private credit (direct lending) is only about 1% of the $150 trillion fixed income market and currently lacks the systemic leverage or duration mismatches that would trigger a crisis.
  • Valuation Rebalancing: Nuttall views current tight credit spreads and high equity valuations as potentially needing a "healthy rebalancing" or "taking air out of the balloon."

Strategic Growth: The Arctos Acquisition

  • Expansion into Sports and Secondaries: KKR’s acquisition of Arctos was driven by a desire to enter the sports franchise asset class (including real estate and media rights) and to finally build a dedicated secondaries business.
  • Acquisition Criteria: KKR remains selective with M&A, requiring targets to offer:
      • A new asset class or capability.
      • Cultural alignment with the existing team.
      • "Permanency of capital" rather than run-off assets.

The Ratepayer Protection Pledge: Five Core Commitments

President Trump joined leaders from Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Oracle, and OpenAI to sign a pledge designed to decouple AI growth from consumer energy price hikes.

  • Energy Self-Sufficiency: Tech companies commit to providing or paying for 100% of the power generation required for their AI projects, largely by building their own dedicated power stations.
  • Infrastructure Cost Coverage: Industry leaders will cover all costs associated with upgrading existing power delivery infrastructure necessitated by their energy demands.
  • Independent Rate Structures: Companies will negotiate separate utility rate structures, ensuring they pay for new capacity even if they do not use the full output, preventing costs from shifting to residential consumers.
  • Community & Workforce Investment: Projects will prioritize hiring and training American workers, including electricians, engineers, HVAC technicians, and welders.
  • Grid Resilience: Data centers will contribute backup power to local grids during times of peak need or emergencies.

Energy Infrastructure and Permitting Reform

To meet the projection that energy demand will triple by 2035, the administration is shifting toward a model of private energy production and drastically accelerated regulation.

  • Fast-Track Permitting: President Trump pledged to reduce energy infrastructure approval times from decades to "two, three, or four weeks" through the efforts of the OSTP and EPA.
  • The "Build Your Own" Model: The administration’s strategy moves away from relying on the aging public grid, instead encouraging tech "geniuses" to act as energy companies.
  • Overcapacity Benefits: Tech companies are expected to build overcapacity for their data centers, with the excess electricity sold back into the public grid at low prices to drive down overall costs.

Industry Commitments and Local Impact

Specific industry leaders detailed their current investments and the practical application of the pledge across various states.

Economic and Geopolitical Strategy

The Structural Evolution of Capital Markets

  • Bank Retrenchment: The shift toward private credit is a permanent evolution of Western capital markets. Post-financial crisis regulations have shrunk bank balance sheets, leaving a void that only private pools of fiduciary capital can fill.
  • Government Over-Leverage: As governments have leveraged themselves heavily, they no longer have the capacity to fund the necessary growth of the global economy, making private capital a critical pillar for the next 30 to 50 years.
  • Funding Massive CAPEX: Massive capital expenditure cycles—specifically in AI technology build-outs and underlying infrastructure—require sophisticated private structuring to attract foreign direct investment and provide necessary debt alongside equity.

Market Risks and Pockets of Vulnerability

  • The "Software Vintage" Concern: Loans made to the software sector during the 2021–2022 period are under scrutiny. These were often highly priced, and some business models are now vulnerable to disintermediation by AI.
  • Small-Cap Fragility: Smaller U.S. companies are facing a "triple threat" of rising interest rates, labor dislocations, and the complexities of navigating tariffs, which may lead to poor performance in specific credit pockets.
  • Default vs. Loss: Kapnick emphasizes that while the industry is returning to a "normal" default rate of 3.5% to 4%, defaults do not necessarily equal losses. The differentiator for managers will be their ability to manage work-outs and protect principal.
  • Leverage Shift: Unlike the 2008 crisis, systemic leverage has largely moved to governments, though Kapnick echoes Jamie Dimon’s warnings about the need for extreme discipline in current lending practices.

The Strategic Value of Scale (BlackRock & HPS)

  • The "Origination" Shortage: In a crowded market, the scarcest resource is the ability to find and originate high-quality assets. The BlackRock/HPS integration (creating a $400 billion platform) aims to solve this by providing a global "footprint" where every major borrower wants to engage.
  • Platform Simplification: By bundling high-yield, private credit, and specialized solutions into one "boutique" within BlackRock, the firm can offer LPs a tailored risk-return profile that smaller, niche players cannot match.
  • Operational Depth: Large-scale firms have the capital and talent to maintain internal teams that can "take over the keys" of a company if a loan fails—a capability that differentiates top-tier fiduciaries from smaller firms that may be forced to realize losses.

Future Technological Disruption

  • Tokenization and AI: Kapnick expects technological evolution to reshape financial structures that have been in place since the 1930s. Tokenization, as championed by Larry Fink and Rob Goldstein, will likely change how assets are handled and traded.
  • Productivity as a Hedge: Despite concerns over labor dislocation caused by AI, Kapnick remains "cautiously optimistic" that the U.S. economy will grow its way through the transition via significant productivity improvements.
  • Portfolio Construction: The next five years will favor managers with a "wide funnel" (mixing sponsor and non-sponsor lending) over those focused solely on narrow niches like middle-market sponsor lending.

President Trump and leading tech executives signed the "Ratepayer Protection Pledge," an agreement where major AI companies commit to fully funding their own energy infrastructure to ensure massive data center power demands do not increase electricity costs for American households.

The Ratepayer Protection Pledge: 5 Core Commitments

To prevent AI data centers from driving up utility bills, the administration and "Big Tech" leaders established five specific mandates:

  • Energy Self-Sufficiency: Tech companies will provide or pay for all power generation and electricity needed for their AI projects, essentially building their own power plants to avoid straining the existing grid.
  • Infrastructure Funding: Companies are responsible for the costs of upgrading existing power delivery infrastructure required for their energy demands.
  • Separate Rate Structures: Utilities will negotiate distinct rate structures for these companies so that new capacity investments are clearly assigned to the corporations, not residential consumers.
  • Community Investment: Firms will provide workforce development and high-skilled jobs (electricians, engineers, HVAC technicians) in the local communities where they operate.
  • Grid Resilience: Companies will use their private infrastructure to contribute backup power to local grids during times of peak need or emergencies.

Energy Deregulation and AI Dominance

The President and Energy Secretary emphasized a radical shift in how energy projects are approved to maintain a competitive edge over China.

  • Fast-Track Permitting: The administration aims to reduce permit approval times from decades to just two to four weeks.
      • Natural Gas and Coal: Targeted for two-week approvals.
      • Nuclear Power: Targeted for three-week approvals.
  • The "Build Your Own" Model: President Trump noted that AI demand is expected to triple by 2035. By allowing companies to build private power plants (Natural Gas, Clean Coal, or Nuclear), the administration seeks "energy dominance" without public expense.
  • Bureaucratic Reform: Energy Secretary Chris Wright and the FERC (Federal Energy Regulatory Commission) are tasked with running the department "like a business," treating industry leaders as partners rather than adversaries.

Industry Commitments & Technical Initiatives

CEOs and Presidents from major tech firms detailed their specific contributions under the pledge:

  • Google (Ruth Porat): Committed to adding 7,800 megawatts of net new energy in Texas and acquiring Intersect Power to build data centers adjacent to power generation. They also pledged to train 100,000 electrical workers.

  • xAI (Gwynne Shotwell): Developing 1.2 gigawatts of power for supercomputers and building state-of-the-art water recycling plants to protect local aquifers. Shotwell also proposed "orbital data centers" powered by space-based solar to move demand off-earth.

  • AI RAN (Radio Access Network): AI is now pervasive across the infrastructure, moving from static rules to intelligent orchestration.

Infrastructure and the "Edge AI Factory"

  • Shift to Specialized Hardware: The success of players like Nvidia is increasingly tied to networking (InfiniBand) and interconnects rather than just GPUs.
      • The market is moving toward "specialty compute" that feeds inference engines and quasi-training at the far edge.
  • Distributed AI Factories: The future network architecture consists of mini AI factories at the edge connected to centralized "mega-factories."
  • The O-RAN Catalyst: AT&T’s $14 billion commitment to Ericsson for Open RAN (O-RAN) signifies a massive investment in retrofitting legacy infrastructure to become software-defined.
      • Sarbjeet Johal notes that the "RIC" (RAN Intelligence Controller) remains too proprietary; for true innovation, it must be opened to the broader VC and startup community.

Telco Business Models and Monetization

  • Silicon Valley Velocity vs. Telco "Glaciers": Historically, telcos have moved slowly due to heavy regulation and risk-management cultures. To survive the AI era, they must adopt the "break the rules" mindset of the tech sector.
  • Identity-Based Commerce: Telcos are exploring ways to monetize through APIs that map a user’s SIM identity to their commerce identity, enabling "one-button" purchases without credit card entries.
  • Fiber-First Strategy: Referencing AT&T’s approach, Johal highlights that fiber is the foundational strength from which all other services—edge computing, commerce, and AI—must hang.

Geopolitics and Sovereign AI

  • Sovereignty as a Value Proposition: Beyond simple data residency, "Sovereign AI" allows nations to tailor AI services to specific cultural and economic needs (e.g., India’s rural population vs. China’s urbanized economy).
  • The Proximity Factor: High-level AI innovation remains concentrated in Silicon Valley due to the density of know-how. Johal argues that global telcos must establish a significant physical presence in these tech hubs to remain competitive.
  • The Scale Problem: Because most telcos operate locally or regionally, they often lack the scale required to compete with global hyperscalers. Innovation thrives on scale, which allows for the division of labor and massive R&D.
