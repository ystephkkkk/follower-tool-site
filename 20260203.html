TL;DR

President Trump signed the Consolidated Appropriations Act to immediately reopen the federal government and fund operations through the fiscal year, highlighting its role in cutting wasteful spending, bolstering national security, and implementing fiscally conservative reforms.

Bill Signing and Key Contributors

  • Act Signed: President Trump signed the Consolidated Appropriations Act, which reopens the federal government and funds the majority of operations for the remainder of the fiscal year.
  • Purpose: The President described the bill as a "great victory for the American people," presenting it as a fiscally responsible package that cuts wasteful spending while supporting critical programs.
  • Acknowledgements: The President thanked numerous individuals for their work on the bill, including:
      • Speaker Mike Johnson: Praised for doing an "incredible job."
      • Representatives/Senators: Tom Cole, Ronnie Jackson, Buddy Carter, Stephanie Bice, Ken Calvert, John Carter, David Joyce, Steve Womack (with condolences for his late wife), Robert Aderholt, Susan Collins, Cindy Hyde-Smith, Bill Hagerty, Lindsey Graham, Mario Diaz-Balart, Katie Britt, John Barrasso, Shelley Moore Capito.
      • Staff: Hal Duncan (Associate Director of Legislative Affairs), Susan Rosser-Averson, Betsy McDonald.

Policy Achievements and Fiscal Impact

  • Ending "Woke Programming": The bill officially ends taxpayer subsidies for "radical far-left woke programming" on NPR and PBS.
  • Foreign Aid Reduction: Slashes nearly $10 billion in "wasteful foreign aid spending" and replaces USAID with a new "America First Opportunity Fund" to provide assistance serving American interests.
  • IRS Program Cuts: Cuts funding for "abusive and weaponized IRS programs," with a Republican Senator noting $10 billion was stripped from the IRS in the bill. The President also referenced his own experience as a "victim" of alleged IRS harassment and leaks.
  • Military Funding & Shipbuilding: Fully funds the military, includes a pay raise for service members, and makes "historic investments" in the American shipbuilding industry.
      • Steel Production Milestone: The U.S. produced more steel than Japan for the first time in over 30 years, signifying a resurgence in the industry.
  • Deportation & Border Security: Continues funding for deportation flights removing "dangerous illegal aliens." The President claimed the border is secure with "nobody coming across" illegally for nine months, though this conflicted with his later statement about 25 million people entering.
  • Melania Trump Initiative: The bill includes a "Melania Trump Foster Youth to Independence Initiative" within the Department of Housing and Urban Development, aimed at supporting foster youth transitioning out of the system.

Economic and Safety Outcomes

  • Economic Growth: The President cited a 5.6% GDP, claiming it could have been 7% without a previous 42-day shutdown, and that this bill prevents further economic loss.
  • Healthcare Reforms: Includes significant healthcare reform, EBM reform, and efforts to reduce drug prices through a "most favored nations" policy, aiming for prices "tied with the lowest price anywhere in the world."
      • Expanded Access: Extends telehealth services and addresses maternal mortality, with a focus on rural healthcare.
  • Crime Reduction:
      • Washington D.C.: Murders are reportedly down 80-97%, making it a "very safe city."
      • Memphis: Crime is down 77%.
      • New Orleans: Crime is down 68% in three to four weeks.
      • Minnesota: Crime is down due to federal removal of "thousands of criminals."
      • National Claim: Crime is at its lowest since 1900 nationally, despite what the President called an "open border stupid policy."
  • Taxpayer Benefits: Anticipates tax return increases of at least $1,000, no tax on tips or social security, and an interest deduction for loans used to purchase American-made cars.

Broader Policy Debates and Controversies

  • Election Integrity: The President advocated for federal government intervention in elections, suggesting "nationalizing elections" if states demonstrate "horrible corruption" or "rigged, crooked elections" in areas like Detroit, Pennsylvania, Philadelphia, and Atlanta. He also emphasized the need for voter ID.
  • Immigration Policy (Sanctuary Cities): Senator Lindsey Graham vehemently rejected Senator Schumer's suggestion of requiring search warrants for ICE, arguing it would hinder deportations. Graham also called for ending "insane policy of sanctuary city" and plans to bring a vote to the floor.
  • International Engagements:
      • Iran: Negotiations are ongoing, with Iran reportedly seeking a deal.
      • Colombia: A productive meeting with President Gustavo Petro focused on counter-narcotic efforts and sanctions.
      • Venezuela: The U.S. is "getting along very well with the leadership" and will work with them against guerrilla and terrorist groups.
  • Epstein Files: The President addressed the resignation of Peter Mandelson over Epstein links, claiming "Wolf" (a reporter) and Epstein conspired against him, and framing the Epstein issue as a "Democrat problem."
  • Hillary Clinton Deposition: Expressed mixed feelings about Hillary Clinton's impending deposition related to Epstein, comparing it to his own past legal challenges.
  • Savannah Guthrie's Mother: Expressed concern over her missing mother and offered federal assistance.
