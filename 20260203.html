Executive Briefing: Midi Health Achieves Unicorn Status & Rapid Expansion

TL;DR

Midi Health has achieved a valuation of over $1 billion, becoming the first "menopause unicorn," following a successful $100 million funding round aimed at accelerating its rapid expansion and addressing critical gaps in women's holistic healthcare.

Unicorn Status & Funding Achievement

  • Valuation Milestone: Midi Health has officially reached a valuation of over $1 billion, making it the first "menopause unicorn."
  • Successful Funding Round: The company closed a $100 million funding round.
      • Lead Investor: Goodwater Capital.
      • New Investors: Foresite Capital and Serena Ventures.
      • Existing Investor Participation: All prior investors also participated in the round.
  • Fundraising Context: This was an opportunistic round, driven by Midi's rapid growth rather than a pre-planned fundraising cycle.
      • Evolving Landscape: Contrasts sharply with earlier difficulties in securing initial capital, reflecting a growing market recognition of women's health as a significant economic opportunity.

Hyper-Growth and Expanding Care Aperture

  • Exponential Patient Growth: Midi has more than doubled its patient base, now treating over 25,000 women per week, up from ~12,000 per week less than a year ago.
  • Nationwide Reach: Provides care and has insurance coverage in all 50 states.
      • High Demand: Current demand is so significant that wait times can exceed one month in some states.
  • Holistic Care Evolution: While initially focused on perimenopause and menopause, Midi's scope has broadened significantly to holistic women's health.
      • Primary Care Role: Increasingly acts as a primary care provider for many women due to systemic care gaps, addressing issues beyond initial symptoms (e.g., pre-diabetes, connecting to cancer care, migraines, PCOS).
      • Age Range Expansion: Now serves women from their 20s to 80s, driven by patient needs and broader health concerns.
  • Efficiency of Virtual Care: Virtual appointments save patients approximately 3.5 hours per visit compared to traditional in-person care, offering convenient 30-minute online consultations.

Strategic Initiatives and Future Directions

  • Economic Impact of Unmet Needs: Untreated menopause symptoms contribute an estimated $25 billion annually in medical expenses and lost work productivity.
      • Cost Savings Focus: Midi aims to save the healthcare system significant costs by providing appropriate, early intervention and reducing the need for specialist care or unnecessary procedures.
      • Addressing SSRI Overuse: Research will examine the potential to reduce inappropriate antidepressant use, as 20% of women in their target age range are on SSRIs.
  • New Research Institute: Launching to leverage data from its 300,000+ patients to better understand cost savings, patient benefits, and personalize care (e.g., drug targeting based on genetics and health history).
  • Leveraging AI for Quality: Investing in AI technologies to enhance care efficiency, ensure up-to-date treatment protocols, and monitor quality.
  • New Care Lines: Plans to launch new services addressing metabolic health, weight management, musculoskeletal health, and long-term wellness.

Operational Scaling and Leadership for the Future

  • Maintaining High Standards: Acknowledges the challenge of sustaining high-quality care amidst rapid growth.
      • Technology & Training: Investing in AI for efficient provider training and quality monitoring, alongside significant resources for hands-on clinician training.
  • Key Executive Hire: Appointed Jason Wheeler, former executive at Tesla and Google, as Chief Financial Officer.
      • Strategic Rationale: Wheeler was brought out of retirement to help build an "efficient, strong operation" that can scale effectively.
  • Scalability Confidence: Early growth phases felt challenging, but with established infrastructure, Midi is now confident in scaling more efficiently to serve millions of women.
  • Long-Term Vision: The core goal remains to bridge the enormous women's healthcare gap, ensuring access to high-quality care for women across all demographics, from rural factory workers to urban CEOs.

Executive Briefing: Ken Griffin at WSJ Invest Live

TL;DR Citadel CEO Ken Griffin outlined his concerns over US fiscal policy, government overreach in business, and eroding public ethics, while defending the dollar's long-term strength and explaining Citadel's strategic shift to Florida due to quality of life issues.

Macroeconomic Outlook & US Fiscal Health

  • Dollar Primacy:
      • The US dollar has "lost some of its luster" over the past 12 months but remains one of the world's great safe harbors.
      • US geopolitical strength (e.g., "Mission Impossible"-like military mission in Venezuela) underpins confidence.
      • America's capital markets are "breathtakingly" strong, allowing companies like Anthropic to raise $10 billion in days, a stark contrast to Europe.
      • A strong reserve currency reduces capital costs, lowers interest rates, and enhances living standards.
  • National Debt & Fiscal Discipline:
      • National debt now exceeds $38 trillion, a critical concern.
      • The US is "late in an economic cycle" yet still running a significant deficit, which should be closer to break-even.
      • Warns that deferring decisions on fiscal discipline will lead to "far more pain 20 years down the road," citing the uncertain future of Social Security for younger generations.
      • Advocates for increased fiscal discipline in both spending and revenue generation, calling for bipartisan agreement akin to the "Simpson-Bowles" committee.
  • Inflation Concerns:
      • A 3% inflation rate is "grating to tens of millions of American households" and was a key issue in past elections.
      • Credits the administration for shifting the dialogue to "affordability" and taking steps like appointing a credible Fed chairman (Kevin Walsh) and rolling back some tariffs.

Business Environment & Government Overreach

  • Impact of Regulatory Uncertainty:
      • Frequent changes to "rules of the road" by Washington (e.g., tariffs) make long-term investment decisions (3, 5, 10, 20+ year horizons) extremely difficult.
      • Unpredictability can lead businesses to make "no decision" at all, hindering capital investment and manufacturing growth.
  • Critique of Government as VC (Crony Capitalism):
      • Acknowledges government's important role in consumer protection and preventing externalities (like pollution).
      • However, criticizes current government involvement in corporate America as "distinctly different," particularly government investments in companies (e.g., "green energy companies" that ended in bankruptcy).
      • States the US has a robust venture capital community and "doesn't need the US government to pretend to be venture capitalists."
      • Distaste for Favoritism: Most CEOs find government engagement that "tastes of favoritism" incredibly distasteful, preferring to compete on merit rather than regulatory favors.
  • Silencing the Corporate Voice:
      • The "woke movement" and social media backlash have created "fear and apprehension" among CEOs, causing them to withdraw from public commentary.
      • Argues that the voices of corporate leaders, who create jobs, are "very important" in Washington and public discourse, rather than being "completely extinguished."
  • Ethics in Public Service:
      • Expressed deep concern over "missteps" in the administration leading to personal enrichment and conflicts of interest (e.g., $500 million donation to a Trump crypto vehicle, Supreme Court issues).
      • States he is not optimistic about a return to strong ethics without continued scrutiny from the press, which serves as a "very important check."

Personal Insights & Societal Challenges

  • Investment Philosophy:
      • Portfolio Purpose: Investors must focus on the purpose of their portfolio based on age and objectives (e.g., 20s: equities; 70s: inflation-protected assets like TIPS or commercial real estate).
      • Personal Investment: As the largest investor in Citadel's hedge fund, his capital is deployed across alternative strategies with "dozens of the world's best investors."
  • Citadel's Move to Florida:
      • Pandemic Response: Initial move to Palm Beach during the pandemic was strategic due to science suggesting lower respiratory virus transmissibility in hot, humid environments. Florida government was "110% behind" them in converting the Four Seasons into a trading floor.
      • Quality of Life: A senior partner's question ("Why did we come back?") prompted a re-evaluation, concluding that Florida offered "safer streets, better schools, the weather's certainly better."
      • Chicago Concerns: Chicago was "gripped in violence," schools were "failing," and recruiting talent was hard due to negative headlines.
      • Florida's Vibrancy: Miami is now Citadel's third-largest office globally, thriving with "positive vibrancy and energy." His art collection also moved to Palm Beach.
  • AI's Real Impact:
      • Does not believe AI will be a major election issue this cycle.
      • Suggests AI is often used as an "excuse" for companies to trim workforces, but objectively, "very few businesses are actually seeing productivity gains that come anywhere close to the headline of job losses."
  • Public Service Ambitions:
      • Has a "deep interest in public policy issues" and studied economics and government at Harvard.
      • Believes that "at a future point in my life, I will be involved in public service."
      • Currently, he can effect change through philanthropic efforts (e.g., Operation Warp Speed, internet access for Chicago children) and by influencing policymakers through existing ties.

Peter Muller, founder of PDT Partners, established one of the most successful quantitative trading firms by cultivating a unique, collaborative culture and disciplined growth over sheer scale. Pete Muller: A Quantitative Finance Maverick

  • Early Life & Unconventional Path:

      • Princeton graduate in mathematics, also a jazz musician.

  • Discovery of Quant Finance:
      • Found his way to BARRA (1985-1992), an early quantitative finance firm, where he discovered his passion for mathematical modeling in finance.
      • Mentored by Richard Bernold and Arjun Devicha.
  • Audacious Pitch & PDT's Founding:
      • In 1992, rejected an analyst role at Morgan Stanley and instead successfully pitched the radical idea of starting a proprietary, quantitative trading desk (Process Driven Trading - PDT) with Morgan Stanley's capital.
      • Conviction stemmed from his experience playing poker; Morgan Stanley gave him two years to prove the concept.

The Genesis and Evolution of PDT Partners

  • Early Success and Growth:
      • PDT launched in 1993, quickly becoming Morgan Stanley's most profitable group, reportedly averaging 20% annual returns.
      • The flagship fund has never experienced a down year since its inception in 1993.
      • Key early team members included Ken Nickerson (Princeton colleague), Kim Elcessor (MIT, MBA, EE), and Frank McQuio (tech).
  • Burnout and Sabbatical:
      • Despite extraordinary success, Muller experienced severe burnout around 1999, his mind overloaded with formulas, hindering his musical pursuits.
      • He took a multi-year phased sabbatical (initially planned for seven years), traveling (e.g., Bhutan), focusing on music (released albums), creating New York Times crossword puzzles, and competing in the World Series of Poker.
  • Return and Independence:
      • Returned to PDT when the firm faced challenges during his phased absence, eventually resuming leadership.
      • A critical decision was to significantly increase hiring of smart, collaborative people, shifting from a cautious, secretive culture. Partner Tar Shaw was instrumental in building the research group.
      • In 2012, post-Volcker Rule, PDT amicably spun out from Morgan Stanley as an independent firm, retaining all 80 employees and transferring its trading book seamlessly.

Culture as a Competitive Edge

  • Core Philosophy:
      • PDT's goal is to be the best place to work in quantitative finance, prioritizing quality over sheer scale.
      • The firm currently manages assets in the mid-teens of billions, with a focus on maximizing dollar profit per employee.
      • PDT charges "3 and 50," typically referring to a 3% management fee and 50% performance fee, and boasts a low 3.5% turnover rate.
  • Hiring and Values:
      • Recruits the smartest individuals, but insists they be collaborative and kind.
      • Maintains an exceptionally warm, nurturing, and trust-based work environment.
      • New hires often express surprise that the supportive culture, which seemed "too good to be true" during interviews, is genuinely maintained.
  • Rewarding Collaboration:
      • Compensation structures reward collaborative behavior, not just individual P&L.
      • A strict "no jerks" policy is enforced; high performers with poor interpersonal skills will not last.
      • The firm fosters a culture where competition is directed towards beating the market, not colleagues, and internal sharing of ideas is encouraged without fear of credit loss.
  • Supportive Environment:
      • The firm operates with a "more like a family" ethos than a "sports team," bending over backward to help struggling employees find suitable roles, even if it's considered "inefficient."

Navigating Risk and Scaling for Quality

  • The 2007 Quant Crisis:
      • In August 2007, PDT faced the "quant crisis," where aggressive leveraging by other firms led to widespread deleveraging and a liquidity crunch, even as the broader market remained flat.
      • PDT made the difficult decision to cut two-thirds of its trading book, but managed to make back the year's returns on the remaining one-third.
