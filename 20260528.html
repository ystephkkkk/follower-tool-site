TL;DR

The ongoing closure of the Strait of Hormuz has triggered one of the most severe energy and trade crises in modern capitalism, with World Economic Forum economists warning that a prolonged shutdown into the second half of 2026 will inflict an economic shock equivalent to nearly 84% of the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic.

The Scale of the Hormuz Chokepoint Crisis

The Strait of Hormuz—the vital artery for global fossil fuel trade—has been effectively closed since February 28, 2026, causing unprecedented disruptions to international commerce.

  • Collapse in Maritime Traffic:
    • Daily Transit Deficit: In normal times, approximately 120 commercial vessels crossed the strait daily. This volume has collapsed to a mere handful (fewer than 10 per day on average, with many days seeing zero transits).
    • Severe Energy Gap: Prior to the crisis, the strait carried 25% of global seaborne oil trade and 20% of global liquefied natural gas (LNG) exports. Currently, approximately 6 million barrels of oil per day are missing from the global market in Q2 2026.
  • Persistent Geopolitical and Physical Barriers:
    • Fragile Ceasefires: Ceasefires brokered between the US and Iran (April 8) and Israel and Lebanon (April 16) have reduced outright hostilities but failed to restore safe passage.
    • Physical Hazards: Iran has deployed sea mines in the strait. According to a US Department of Defense report to Congress, fully de-mining the channel could take up to six months.
    • Sanctions and Blockades: Shipowners face severe legal and financial risks. Iranian authorities demand transit-related payments that violate Western sanctions, while the US blockade under Central Command (Centcom) had already diverted 89 commercial vessels and disabled four as of May 19.

Economic Fallout and the "COVID Benchmark"

According to the World Economic Forum's May 2026 *Chief Economists Outlook*, a survey of global public and private sector economists reveals a sharp deterioration in macroeconomic confidence compared to January 2026.

  • Plunging Growth Outlook:
    • Pessimistic Consensus: 89% of chief economists now expect global economic growth to weaken over the coming year, a massive surge from the 53% who expected weakening in January.
    • Severe Weakening: More than 20% of economists expect global growth to be "severely weakened," while only 8% expect any economic strengthening (down from 20% in January).
  • The Pandemic Benchmark: Economists rated the relative economic severity of the Hormuz crisis against the COVID-19 pandemic (set at a benchmark of 100):
    • 2025 Tariff Turmoil: Scored 40 on the scale.
    • Hormuz Reopening by June 2026: Projected to score 72.
    • Hormuz Closure Extending into H2 2026: Projected to score 83.5, meaning the prolonged shutdown would inflict nearly 84% of the economic damage of the pandemic.
  • Systemic Inflationary Pressure:
    • Core Inflation Risks: Over 90% of chief economists expect increased inflation. High energy prices are quickly bleeding into core inflation, driving up the costs of manufacturing, chemicals, and transportation.
    • Monetary Policy Pivot: The anticipated stabilization and reduction of central bank interest rates are now off the table. Rates are expected to remain higher for longer, eroding household disposable income and stalling business investments.

Supply Chain Paralysis and the Lack of Alternatives

Unlike other major shipping chokepoints, the geography of the Persian Gulf offers no realistic maritime alternatives to the Strait of Hormuz.

  • No Rerouting Options:
    • Comparison to Bab-el-Mandeb: Since early 2024, Houthi attacks have forced container ships to bypass the Red Sea and circumnavigate Africa. While this added 20 days to voyages, the industry adapted. Hormuz, by contrast, has no physical maritime bypass.
    • Logistical Inefficiencies: Shipping firms are utilizing "land bridges" by unloading goods at ports outside the Gulf (such as Jeddah in Saudi Arabia, Salalah in Oman, or Khor Fakkan in the UAE) and trucking them overland. However, a single 20,000-container vessel requires 20,000 individual trucks, making this approach highly inefficient and impossible to scale.
  • Severe Trade Contraction:
    • Import Slump: In March 2026, containerized imports into the Middle East contracted by 40% year-on-year, starving major regional hubs like Dubai’s Jebel Ali and Qatar's Doha of fast-moving consumer goods and automotive parts.
    • Export Shock: Regional containerized exports contracted by 46% year-on-year in March. Critical global supply chains are being starved of primary plastics, aluminum, chemicals, fertilizers, and helium (a critical component for artificial intelligence hardware).

The Broader Global Geopolitical Picture

The Hormuz crisis occurs during a highly volatile transition phase for the global economic order, where supply chains have shifted from economic efficiency tools to instruments of foreign and security policy.

  • Compounding Global Chokepoints:
    • Panama Canal Vulnerability: Climate change and El Niño patterns threaten the Panama Canal with low water levels (similar to 2023), presenting compounding risks if multiple straits experience simultaneous disruptions.
  • Trade Policy Volatility:
    • US Tariff Shifts: A temporary 10% tariff on all US imports (following a Supreme Court ruling) is set to expire in July 2026, leaving businesses highly uncertain about incoming sector-specific replacements.
    • USMCA Negotiations: The upcoming renegotiation of the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement reveals diverging strategies, with Mexico aligning closely with US trade policies while Canada takes a more confrontational stance.
    • Contradictory Trade Signals: While Western capitals are tightening protectionist measures against strong Chinese exports, historic and previously stalled bilateral trade deals—such as EU-Mercosur and EU-India—have unexpectedly been signed.
  • Three Competing Economic Forces: Economists are closely watching the balance between the negative economic drag of the Hormuz energy shock, the offsetting growth fueled by the AI investment boom in the US and East Asia (Korea and Taiwan), and the baseline stabilization provided by massive fiscal deficits (6% of GDP in the US and nearly 3% in the Eurozone).

The Evolution of Third Point's Investment Philosophy

  • Event-Driven Roots: Dan Loeb’s foundational framework was shaped by credit investing and Joel Greenblatt’s classic book, *You Can Be a Stock Market Genius*.

      • Specialized Teams: He structured his investment team around industry specialists rather than generalists.
