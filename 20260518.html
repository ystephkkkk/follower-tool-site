TL;DR

Effective leadership in legacy organizations requires reconciling a deep respect for institutional history with an aggressive, risk-tolerant push toward simplification and future-readiness.

The Challenge of Institutional Inertia

  • The "Legacy Cancer": Ajay Banga highlights the danger of the "we tried that 28 years ago" mentality. He argues that past failures are often irrelevant because variables like market prices and technology have shifted significantly.
  • Complexity Traps: Karin Rådström noted the inefficiency of having 18 people involved in "very simple topics," emphasizing that organizational bloat often masks a lack of decision-making clarity.
  • The Disruptor’s Blind Spot: Jesper Brodin reflects on a realization that IKEA had stopped listening to customers, noting that being a historic disruptor can lead to complacency when new disruption (like mobile commerce) arrives.
  • The "One-Third" Rule for Culture Change: Banga categorizes staff into three groups:
      • The Supporters: One-third who want the leader to succeed.
      • The Resisters: One-third who actively oppose change.
      • The Fence-Sitters: One-third who are waiting to see what happens.
      • Strategy: A leader’s job is to win over the middle third to create a two-thirds winning majority; the resisters are rarely winnable.

Balancing Future Innovation with Historical Legacy

  • Non-Linear Progress: Brodin argues that the future and the past should not be viewed as a linear trade-off. Leaders must "love the past" but fight to ensure it doesn't stifle the "curiosity of the future."
  • Institutionalized Risk-Taking:
      • The "Go Banana" Card: To combat the fear of making mistakes at IKEA, Brodin issued thousands of cards that essentially co-signed potential failures in advance to encourage entrepreneurship.
      • American vs. European Mindsets: Rådström observes a difference in risk-taking between the US and Europe. She advocates for cross-pollinating "American optimism" with "German diligence" to balance boldness with execution.

Operational Excellence and People Management

  • Banga’s Three Pillars for Management:

1. Done is better than perfect: Aiming for speed over absolute perfection.

2. Fail faster and harder: Embracing the risk of failure as a learning tool.

3. Question everything always: Constant interrogation of "how things are done."

  • The Five-Minute Rule: Banga utilizes a simplified four-box feedback system for employees focused on:
      • Positive attributes.
      • Areas for improvement.
      • Three core traits: Urgency, thoughtful risk-taking, and accountability.
      • Long-term career pathing.
      • *Insight:* If a manager cannot fill this out in five minutes, they do not know their employee well enough.
  • "Gardening" the Organization: Brodin compares organizations to plants—they grow and get more complicated naturally. Leaders must constantly "garden" (prune) the bureaucracy to avoid the need for massive, painful restructurings later.

The Dual Nature of AI

  • Business Transformation: Rådström identifies self-driving trucks as a major AI opportunity that could reduce customer costs by 25-30%, requiring more decentralized decision-making as expertise moves away from the boardroom.
  • Societal Risks: Brodin expresses concern that while AI offers efficiency, it carries risks to human connection. He compares it to the "addiction" of social media, which promised connection but delivered loneliness.
  • The Data Divide: Banga warns that emerging markets may be left behind in the Large Language Model (LLM) race due to a lack of computing power, electricity, and data infrastructure.
  • National Security: Banga predicts that "citizen data" will soon be viewed as a high-stakes national security asset, similar to how energy policy is viewed today.

TL;DR

Public safety is undergoing a paradigm shift toward a data-driven "ecosystem" where autonomous drones, AI-powered sensor networks, and officer wellness analytics are replacing traditional, reactive policing models to solve staffing shortages and improve de-escalation.

The Shift to Autonomous First Response

The integration of drones and sensors is transforming how departments handle high-risk situations by providing immediate, low-cost situational awareness.

  • Drone-as-First-Responder (DFR): Rahul Sidhu (Flock Safety) notes that drones act as flying robots capable of automating tasks like chasing Amber Alert vehicles or pursuing shooters detected by gunshot sensors.
      • Sustainability: Replacing traditional helicopters with drones is more cost-effective; maintaining 24/7 aerial coverage with helicopters is financially impossible for most departments.
  • De-escalation via Intelligence: David Ulevitch cites an example where a drone identified a "man with a shotgun" as actually being a "janitor with a broom," preventing a potentially violent police confrontation through superior situational awareness.
  • Layered Sensor Networks: Flock Safety utilizes a combination of license plate readers (LPR), gunshot detection, and drone dispatch to create a proactive safety net.

AI and Officer Wellness

Colonel Jeffrey Glover (Arizona DPS) highlights a shift toward using technology to support the "human" element of policing, specifically focusing on mental health and performance optimization.

  • Brain Scan Diagnostics: Arizona DPS utilizes Vytania (Heal the Heroes) to conduct brain scans as "temperature checks" for officers to assess mental readiness.
  • Body-Worn Camera Analytics: The department uses Truleo to run analytics on body-cam footage.
      • Behavioral Scorecards: The tech tracks how troopers interact with the public and vice versa.
      • Burnout Detection: The system flags signs of officer burnout or high-stress levels, allowing supervisors to intervene before a crisis occurs.
  • Structural Support: Based on tech-driven burnout data, the department has instituted well-checks and sabbaticals at the 15-year and 25-year marks to maintain job satisfaction.

Global Intelligence and Large-Scale Events

As global events increasingly have local repercussions, law enforcement is expanding its intelligence apparatus beyond local borders.

  • Fusion Centers: The Arizona Counter Terrorism Information Center (ACTIC) collaborates with other U.S. fusion centers to share unclassified data on global crime trends.
  • International Partnerships: Colonel Glover is building intelligence-sharing bridges with nations including Mexico (Sonora), the UAE, and Liberia to stay ahead of international criminal trends.
  • Major Event Readiness: Departments are currently utilizing the TRX program to prepare for high-security upcoming events like the FIFA World Cup and the Olympics.

Advice for Public Safety Founders

Building for law enforcement requires a high degree of empathy and a willingness to navigate a culture that is famously resistant to change.

  • The "Dual Hate" Paradox: Rahul Sidhu notes that "Cops hate two things: for things to change and for things to stay the same." Founders must navigate this narrow path by building tools that feel "inevitable."
  • Immersion is Mandatory: To build effective tools, founders must conduct ride-alongs or serve as reserve officers. Understanding the "language" and the feeling of being on the beat is critical for product-market fit.
  • The 10-Year Outlook: Colonel Glover predicts that within a decade, the officer skill set will shift from physical tactics (e.g., "kicking in doors") to more nuanced, investigative work focused on AI-driven fraud and technical video analysis.

TL;DR

The AI hardware market is shifting from a GPU-centric model focused on training to a heterogeneous landscape where "Agentic Inference"—autonomous tasks performed without human oversight—prioritizes massive memory capacity and low cost over raw processing speed.

The State of AI Hardware: GPUs vs. Wafer-Scale

  • The GPU Dominance: Nvidia currently leads because GPUs excel at both training and inference by combining high compute power with High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM) and sophisticated chip-to-chip networking.
      • Training Dynamics: Training requires massive parallelization but is bottlenecked by serial steps where all GPUs must share results, making Nvidia’s networking a "moat."
      • The SpaceX/Anthropic Benchmark: Anthropic’s deal to use SpaceX’s "Colossus 1" (220,000 Nvidia GPUs) illustrates how the same hardware is currently used interchangeably for both training and high-scale inference.
  • Cerebras and the Speed Niche: Cerebras’ WSE-3 chip bypasses traditional size limits by using an entire silicon wafer as a single chip, leading to extreme performance metrics.
      • Bandwidth Advantage: The WSE-3 offers 21 petabytes per second of bandwidth—6,000 times that of an Nvidia H100.
      • The Trade-off: While "blisteringly fast," Cerebras is limited by on-chip memory capacity (44GB SRAM). If a model or context (KV cache) exceeds this, the architecture becomes prohibitively expensive and inefficient.

Defining the "Inference Shift"

  • Answer Inference: This is the current standard (e.g., ChatGPT, voice assistants). It is human-centric and highly sensitive to latency.
      • User Experience: Speed is the primary variable. For "Answer Inference," chips like Cerebras or Groq are ideal because they minimize the time a human waits for a response.
  • Agentic Inference: This represents the next era, where AI agents (like Claude Code) perform complex, multi-step tasks autonomously.
      • Machine-to-Machine: The true power of agents lies in work done without human involvement. If an agent runs a job overnight, the "tokens per second" speed is secondary to the agent's ability to complete the task.
      • Memory over Speed: Agentic workloads require massive context, history, and state. This favors a "memory hierarchy" (DRAM, SSDs, databases) over pure compute speed.

The Economic Unbundling of the GPU

  • Stranded Resources: Current GPU architectures often "strand" resources—wasting HBM during prefill or wasting compute during the serial decode process.
  • The "Good Enough" Architecture: Because agents are latency-tolerant, the market will likely shift toward cheaper, high-capacity memory and "good enough" compute.
      • Cost Efficiency: Hyperscalers may favor simpler, non-GPU-bound systems for agentic workloads to reduce premiums paid for Nvidia’s high-speed networking and HBM.
      • Strategic Advantage for China: China possesses the "good enough" components (older GPUs/CPUs and standard DRAM) required for agentic inference, potentially mitigating their lack of access to bleeding-edge nodes for training.

Future Implications: Space and Moore's Law

  • Space-Based Data Centers: Slower, older nodes are actually better suited for space.
      • Reliability: Physically larger transistors (older nodes) withstand radiation better.
      • Thermals: Lower power consumption reduces the difficulty of dissipating heat in a vacuum.
  • The End of Moore's Law Necessity: While Nvidia’s Jensen Huang argues Moore's Law is dead to promote systems innovation, agentic inference suggests it might not matter at all. If the primary market for AI moves to autonomous agents, the industry can scale by adding more "good enough" compute rather than chasing marginal speed gains on the bleeding edge.

The fundamental driver of the ongoing surge in semiconductor stocks is, of course, AI, particularly the realization that agents are going to need a lot of compute. What Cerebras represents, however, is something broader. While the compute story for AI has been largely about GPUs, particularly from Nvidia, the future is going to look increasingly heterogeneous. The GPU Era: the story of how graphics processing units became the center of AI is a well-trodden one, but in brief: just as drawing pixels on a computer screen was a parallel process, making AI-related calculations was a parallel process. This meant there was a direct connection between the number of processing units and graphics speed. Nvidia enabled this dual-usage by making its graphics processors programmable, and created an entire software ecosystem called CUDA to make this programming accessible. The big difference between graphics and AI has been the size of the problem being solved. Models are a lot bigger than video game textures, which has led to a dramatic expansion in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) per GPU, and dramatic innovations in terms of chip-to-chip networking to allow multiple chips to work together as one addressable system. Nvidia has been the leader in both. The number one use case for GPUs has been training, which stresses the third point in particular. While the calculations within each training step are massively parallel, the steps themselves are serial. Every GPU has to share its results with every other GPU before the next step can begin. This is why a trillion-parameter model needs to fit in the aggregate memory of tens of thousands of GPUs that can communicate as one system. Nvidia dominates both problem spaces, first by securing HBM ahead of the rest of the industry, and second thanks to its investments in networking. Of course, training isn’t the only AI workload. The other is inference. Inference has three main parts. Prefill encodes everything the LLM needs to know into an understandable state. This is highly parallelizable and compute matters. The first part of decode entails reading the KV cache, which stores context, including the output of the prefill step, to make an attention calculation. This is a serial step where bandwidth matters, but the memory requirements are variable and increasingly large. The second part of decode is the feed-forward computation over the model weights. This is also a serial step where bandwidth matters, and the memory requirements are defined by the size of the model. The two decode steps alternate for every layer of the model. They’re interleaved, not in sequence, which is to say that decode is serial and memory bandwidth bound. For every token generated, two distinct memory pools must be read. The KV cache, which stores context and grows with each token, and the model weights themselves must both be read in full to produce a single output token. GPUs handle all three needs: high compute for prefill, abundant HBM for KV cache and model weights, and chip-to-chip networking to pool memory across multiple chips when a single GPU isn’t enough. In other words, what works for training works for inference. Look no further than the deal SpaceX made with Anthropic. From Anthropic’s blog: "We’ve signed an agreement with SpaceX to use all of the compute capacity at their Colossus 1 data center. This gives us access to more than 300 megawatts of new capacity, over 220,000 Nvidia GPUs, within the month. This additional capacity will directly improve capacity for Claude Pro and Claude Max subscribers." SpaceX retains Colossus 2, presumably for both training of future models and inference of existing ones, and can afford to do both in the same data center precisely because xAI’s models aren’t getting much usage. More pertinently to this piece, they can do both in the same data center because both training and inference can be done on GPUs. Indeed, the GPUs Anthropic is contracting for at Colossus 1 were originally used for training as well. The fact that GPUs are so flexible is a big advantage. Understanding Cerebras: Cerebras makes something completely different. While a silicon wafer has a diameter of 300 millimeters, the reticle limit is around 26 millimeters by 33 millimeters. This is the maximum area that a lithography tool can expose on that wafer and the effective size limit for chips. Going beyond that entails linking two separate chips together over a chip-to-chip interposer, which is exactly what Nvidia has done with the B200. Cerebras, on the other hand, has invented a way to lay down wiring across the so-called scribe lines. These scribe lines are the boundary between reticle exposures. By making the entire wafer into a single chip, there is no need for relatively slow chip-to-chip linkages. The net result is a chip with a lot of compute and a lot of SRAM that is blisteringly fast to access. To put it in numbers, the WSE-3, Cerebras’ latest chip, has 44 gigabytes of on-chip SRAM at 21 petabytes per second of bandwidth. An H100 has 80 gigabytes of HBM at 3.35 terabytes per second. In other words, the WSE-3 has just over half the memory of an H100, but 6,000 times the memory bandwidth. The reason to compare the WSE-3 to an H100 is that the H100 is the chip most used for inference, and inference is clearly what Cerebras is most well-suited for. You can use Cerebras chips for training, but the chip-to-chip networking story isn’t very compelling, which is to say that all of that compute and on-chip memory is mostly just sitting around. What is much more interesting is the idea of getting a stream of tokens at dramatically faster speed than you can from a GPU. Note, however, that the limitation in terms of training also potentially applies in terms of inference. As long as everything fits in on-chip memory, Cerebras’ speed is an incredible experience. The moment you need more memory, whether that be for a larger model or a larger KV cache, then Cerebras doesn’t make much sense, particularly given the price. That whole-wafer-as-chip technique means high yields are a massive challenge, which hugely drives up costs. At the same time, I do think there will be a market for Cerebras-style chips. Right now, the company is highlighting the usefulness of speed for coding. Reasoning means a lot of tokens, which means that dramatically scaling up tokens per second equals faster thinking. But I think this is a temporary use case, for reasons I’ll explain in a bit. What does matter is how long humans are waiting for an answer, and as products like AI wearables become more of a thing, the speed of interaction will have a tangible effect on the user experience. This is particularly true for voice, which will be a function of token generation speed. Agentic Inference: I have previously made the case, including in "Agents Over Bubbles," that we have gone through three inflection points in the LLM era. ChatGPT demonstrated the utility of token prediction. o1 introduced the idea of reasoning, where more tokens meant better answers. Opus 4.5 and Claude Code introduced the first usable agents, which could actually accomplish tasks using a combination of reasoning models and a harness that utilized tools and verified work. All of this falls under the banner of inference, but I think it will be increasingly clear that there is a difference between providing an answer, what I will call answer inference, and doing a task, what I will call agentic inference. Cerebras’ target market is answer inference. In the long run, I think the architecture for agentic inference will look a lot different, not just from Cerebras’ approach, but from the GPU approach as well. I mentioned above that fast inference for coding is a temporary use case. Specifically, coding with LLMs requires a human in the loop. It’s the human that defines what is to be coded, checks the work, and commits the pull request. It’s not hard to envision a future, however, where all this is completely handled by machines. This will apply to agentic work broadly. The true power of agents will not be that they do work for humans, but rather that they do work without human involvement at all. This by extension will mean that the likely best approach for solving agentic inference will look a lot different than answer inference. The most important aspect for answer inference is token speed. The most important aspect for agentic inference, however, is memory. Agents need context, state, and history. Some of that will live as active KV cache. Some will live in host memory or SSDs. Much of it will live in databases, logs, embeddings, and object stores. The important point is that agentic inference will be less about GPUs answering a question, and more about the memory hierarchy wrapped around a model. Critically, this articulation of an agentic-specific memory hierarchy implies a necessary trade-off of speed for capacity. Here’s the thing, though. Lower speed isn’t nearly as important a consideration if there isn’t a human in the loop. If an agent is waiting around for a job that is being run overnight, the agent doesn’t know or care about the user experience impact. What is most important is being able to accomplish a task, and if entirely new approaches to memory make that possible, then delays are fine. Meanwhile, if delays are fine, then all of the focus on pure compute power and high-bandwidth memory seems out of place. If latency isn’t the top priority, then slower and cheaper memory, like traditional DRAM, makes a lot more sense. And if the entire system is mostly waiting on memory, then chips don’t need to be as fast as the cutting edge either. This represents a profound shift in future architectures, but it also doesn't mean that current architectures are going away. Training will continue to matter, and Nvidia’s current architecture will continue to dominate. This includes high-speed compute, large amounts of high-bandwidth memory, and high-speed networking. Answer inference will be a meaningful market, albeit a relatively small one, and speed from chips like Cerebras or Groq will be very useful. I explained how Nvidia is deploying Groq’s LPUs here. Agentic inference will gradually unbundle the GPU. The GPU alternates between stranding high-bandwidth memory during the prefill process and stranding compute during the decode process. Future systems will favor increasingly sophisticated memory hierarchies, dominated by high capacity and relatively lower cost memory types, with good enough compute. Indeed, if anything, it will be the speed of CPUs for things like tool use that will matter more than the speed of GPUs. At the same time, these categories won’t be equal in size or importance. Specifically, agentic inference will be the largest market by far, because that is the market that won't be limited by humans or time. Today’s agents are fancy answer inference. In the future, true agentic inference will be work done by computers according to dictates given by other computers. And the market size scales not with humans, but with compute. The Implications of Agentic Inference on Compute: to date, the invocation of scaling with compute has implicitly meant Nvidia bullishness. However, much of Nvidia’s relative advantage to date has been a function of latency. Nvidia chips have fast compute, but keeping that compute busy has required big investments in ever-expanding HBM memory and networking. If latency isn’t the key constraint, however, then Nvidia’s approach seems less worth paying a premium for. Nvidia does recognize this shift. The company launched an inference framework called Dynamo that helps disaggregate different parts of inference, and is shipping products like standalone memory and CPU racks to enable increasingly large KV caches and faster tool use. This is done to better keep their expensive GPUs busy. Ultimately, however, it’s easy to see costs and simplicity being increasingly attractive to hyperscalers for agentic inference that isn't remotely GPU bound. China, meanwhile, for all of its lack of leading-edge compute, has everything it needs for agentic inference: fast enough, but not leading-edge GPUs; fast enough, but not leading-edge CPUs; DRAM; hard drives; and so on. The challenge, of course, is compute for training. It’s also possible that answer inference is more important for national security, at least when it comes to military applications. The other interesting angle is space. Slower chips actually make space data centers more viable for a number of reasons. First, if memory can be offloaded, chips can be made much simpler and run much cooler. Second, older nodes, by virtue of being physically larger, will better withstand space radiation. Third, older nodes require less power, which means there will be less heat to dissipate via radiation. Fourth, not being on the bleeding edge will mean higher reliability, an important consideration given that satellites won’t be repairable. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang regularly says that Moore’s Law is Dead. What he means is that the future of computing speed-ups will be a function of systems innovation, which is exactly what Nvidia has done. Maybe the most profound implication of agents that act without humans in the loop, however, will be that Moore’s Law doesn't matter, and that the way we get more compute is by realizing that the compute we have is already good enough.

TL;DR

The energy sector is currently defined by a "conundrum" where massive geopolitical supply shocks (specifically the Iranian threat to the Strait of Hormuz) haven’t translated into sustained stock rallies due to market belief in a quick resolution and a confusing forward curve. However, rising electricity demand for AI and data centers is creating a structural long-term growth story for natural gas and copper.

The Oil Paradox and Geopolitical Risk

  • The Strait of Hormuz "Black Swan": Bob Brackett notes that Iran’s ability to block the Strait captures 20% of global oil flows and 20% of natural gas. This is a larger supply threat than the Russia-Ukraine war, yet oil prices remain below previous peaks due to "market hope" that the situation will be resolved quickly.
  • The Inventory Drain: Global inventories are being drained at a rate never seen before to compensate for supply shortfalls. This creates a "coiled spring" effect for prices as governments will eventually need to restock strategic reserves.
  • The Forward Curve Confusion: The market is currently in Backwardation (future prices are lower than spot prices).
      • Investors often mistakenly use the forward curve as a price predictor, leading them to fear negative earnings revisions and avoid stocks like ExxonMobil despite strong current cash flows.
  • OPEC Instability: The UAE's exit from the OPEC cartel stems from a desire to grow their prolific onshore resources, which was restricted by quotas. This signaling suggests a weakening of the cartel's long-term ability to control prices.

Natural Gas: The AI and Electricity Growth Story

  • Surging Electricity Demand: After 15 years of flat demand, US electricity needs are growing at 3-4% annually, driven by AI data centers and robotics.
      • Scale: A single one-gigawatt data center requires as much energy as a city of one million people.
  • The Bridge Fuel: While nuclear is 24/7 "green," it has long lead times. Natural gas turbines are the only short-term solution to meet massive AI-driven power needs.
  • Investment Picks: Brackett favors "pure-play" dry gas producers over diversified majors for those looking to play the gas surge.
      • EQT (EQT) and Expand Energy (EXE): These are the largest US gas producers and benefit most directly from rising Henry Hub prices.
  • Negative Pricing in the Permian: In West Texas, natural gas is currently a "waste product" with negative prices because oil drillers have no pipe capacity to move the associated gas, forcing them to pay others to take it away.

Mining: Copper Scarcity vs. Lithium Disruption

  • The Copper Tailwind: Unlike oil (which benefited from fracking technology), there is no new technology to "frack" for copper.
      • Freeport (FCX): Identified as the "copper champion" due to its massive assets in Indonesia and the US.
      • Demand Drivers: EVs and grid infrastructure are "copper-hungry." One EV contains as much copper as an entire house.
  • Lithium’s Technological Shift: Lithium is moving from traditional hard-rock mining to "oilfield brines."
      • The Arkansas/Appalachia Resource: Companies like Exxon and Equinor are exploring extracting lithium from the salty water produced during oil and gas drilling. This "low-cost" chemical process could disrupt traditional lithium miners.

Strategic Midstream and The "Toll-Booth" Model

  • Cheniere Energy (LNG): Described as a "refrigerator" model. They buy US gas, liquefy it, and sell it globally on 20-year "take-or-pay" contracts.
      • Safety: It is a pass-through model that removes commodity price risk, acting as a "toll-booth" for global energy.
      • Valuation: Currently trades at an 8% free cash flow yield, which Brackett views as undervalued compared to other S&P 500 midstream companies.
  • The Majors (Exxon/Chevron): These remain the "gold standard" for project management. Brackett notes their 3% dividends are "untouchable" and were maintained even when oil prices went negative during COVID, unlike European peers (Shell, BP) which cut distributions.
