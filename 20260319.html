TL;DR: Global trade growth is projected to slow significantly from a surprisingly strong 4.6% in 2025 to a modest 1.9% in 2026, as the temporary boosts from AI investment and "front-loading" of imports dissipate amid rising geopolitical risks in the Middle East.

2026 Trade Projections and Economic Outlook

  • The 2026 Forecast: Under the WTO's baseline scenario, world merchandise trade volume growth will slow to 1.9% in 2026, down from 4.6% in 2025. A rebound to 2.6% is anticipated for 2027.
  • Normalization of Trade-to-GDP: In 2025, trade grew 1.6 times faster than global GDP (4.7% vs 2.9%). The 2026 forecast expects this relationship to return to the historical trend where trade growth closely matches GDP growth.
  • Services Sector Stability: Commercial services trade is expected to grow by 4.8% in 2026 (down from 5.3% in 2025) before picking up to 5.1% in 2027.
  • Regional Highlights:
      • Africa: Showed strong 2025 growth due to demand for fossil fuels, green transition minerals (copper, cobalt, lithium), and favorable crop conditions for cocoa and coffee.
      • Middle East: 2025 growth was driven by a rebound in energy exports.
      • Europe: Expected to be the hardest hit by potential energy price spikes due to its status as a major net importer of energy and liquefied natural gas (LNG).

Primary Drivers of the 2025 Surge

  • Import "Front-Loading": A significant portion of 2025's growth was attributed to North American companies accelerating imports early in the year to bypass expected tariff hikes in the United States.
      • This created a one-time boost in trade volume that is not expected to recur in 2026.
  • The AI Investment Boom: Surging investment in AI-enabling goods provided nearly half of the merchandise trade growth in 2025.
      • High Import Intensity: AI-related goods (chips, servers, software) have an import intensity of 70% to 90%, meaning 70–90 cents of every dollar invested goes toward imports. For comparison, construction investment has an import intensity of less than 2%.
      • Geographic Concentration: This boom primarily benefits a small group of specialists: the U.S. (design/software), Chinese Taipei and Korea (semiconductors), the Netherlands and Japan (manufacturing equipment), and China (assembly).

Geopolitical Risks and Downside Scenarios

  • The Middle East Conflict: If regional conflict persists, it could shave 0.5 percentage points off merchandise trade growth (reducing it to 1.4%) and 0.7 percentage points off services growth.
  • Energy and Food Security:
      • Strait of Hormuz: This chokepoint handles 20% of global oil consumption and 30% of global fertilizer shipments.
      • Fertilizer Impact: Disruptions to fertilizer supply threaten global food security by raising costs for farmers and prompting a shift to less productive, "low-input" crops.
  • Tariff Uncertainty: Following a U.S. Supreme Court decision affecting AEEPA tariffs, the U.S. implemented temporary Section 122 balance-of-payments tariffs (10%). Ongoing Section 301 investigations may lead to more permanent, product-specific replacements in 2026.

Resilience of the Multilateral Trading System

  • MFN Tariff Share: The share of global merchandise trade subject to statutory "Most Favored Nation" (MFN) tariffs—a key indicator of the health of the rules-based system—dropped from 80% in 2024 to 72% currently.
  • Systemic Health: Despite this erosion, nearly three-quarters of global trade still occurs under standard WTO MFN terms, suggesting the system remains functional despite significant geopolitical "battering."
  • Ministerial Conference (MC14): Leadership intends to use these figures as a "wake-up call" for members meeting in Cameroon to pursue effective reforms and maintain trade predictability.

TL;DR

The ECB maintained its key interest rates while signaling that the war in the Middle East has introduced significant uncertainty, leading to upward revisions for near-term inflation and downward revisions for economic growth. The Governing Council remains "data-dependent" and "meeting-by-meeting," refusing to pre-commit to a specific rate path while monitoring energy-driven second-round effects.

Monetary Policy and Interest Rate Decision

  • Interest Rates Unchanged: The Governing Council decided to keep the three key ECB interest rates at their current levels to ensure inflation returns to the 2% medium-term target.
  • Meeting-by-Meeting Approach: President Lagarde emphasized that the ECB is not pre-committing to a particular rate path. Decisions will be based on:
      • The evolving inflation outlook and underlying inflation dynamics.
      • The strength of monetary policy transmission.
      • Incoming economic and financial data.
  • Reaction Function: The ECB is paying specific attention to commodity markets, supply bottlenecks, firms' selling price expectations, and the "ECB wage tracker" to anticipate potential second-round effects.

Updated Economic and Inflation Projections

  • Exceptional Data Cut-off: Staff projections incorporated information up to March 11, 2026—an unusually late date—to account for the impact of the conflict in the Middle East.
  • Inflation Outlook (Baseline): Headline inflation is projected to average 2.6% in 2026, 2.0% in 2027, and 2.1% in 2028.
      • Upward Revision: The 2026 figure was revised higher due to the war's impact on energy prices.
      • Core Inflation: Excluding energy and food, inflation is seen at 2.3% (2026), 2.2% (2027), and 2.1% (2028).
  • Economic Growth (Baseline): Growth projections were revised downward to 0.9% in 2026, 1.3% in 2027, and 1.4% in 2028.
      • Growth Drivers: While the war weighs on confidence and real incomes, growth is supported by low unemployment, private sector balance sheets, and increased public spending on defense and infrastructure.
  • Scenario Analysis: Staff produced "Adverse" and "Severe" scenarios. In the severe scenario, oil and gas prices remain high for a longer period, resulting in inflation staying above the baseline beyond the projection horizon.

Risks to the Economic Outlook

  • Downside Risks to Growth: A prolonged war in the Middle East could further erode household income and discourage investment. Additional risks include global trade frictions, volatile trade policies, and Russia's ongoing war in Ukraine.
  • Upside Risks to Inflation: The primary concern is a larger-than-expected upward shift in energy prices.
      • Persistence: Inflation could stay higher if wage growth rises in response to energy costs or if global supply chains face broader disruptions.
  • Financial Conditions: Market conditions have tightened since the last meeting; stock markets have fallen, and short-term interest rates have risen notably.

Financial Stability and Strategic Initiatives

  • Private Credit Concerns: Vice-President de Guindos addressed risks in private credit, citing opacity, lack of transparency in asset valuation, and high leverage.
      • EU Exposure: While the market is growing, European bank exposure to private credit is currently more limited than in the U.S.
  • Digital Euro and Integration: President Lagarde urged the swift adoption of regulations for the digital euro to enhance Europe’s strategic autonomy and innovation in payments.
  • Fiscal Policy Guidance: The ECB advised that any fiscal responses to the energy shock must be temporary, targeted, and tailored to avoid fueling inflationary pressures.

Nvidia’s "Platform Moat" Strategy

  • The Shift to Inference: While Nvidia dominated the "training" era via CUDA lock-in, the "inference" era (running models) is more competitive as hyperscalers build custom silicon.
  • NemoClaw as an Operating System: Nvidia’s launch of NemoClaw—a free, open-source platform for AI agents—is a strategic move to become the "Android of AI."
      • Commoditization: By providing the software layer for free, Nvidia makes it harder for labs like OpenAI or Anthropic to squeeze them on pricing or bypass their hardware.
      • Monetization via Infrastructure: Like Google (Android/Search) or Microsoft (IE/Windows), Nvidia gives away the software to drive demand for the underlying "token factories" (GPUs and data centers).
  • Aligned Incentives: Hugging Face CEO Clément Delangue notes that Nvidia’s success now depends on everyone being able to build AI, not just a few "closed-garden" labs.

The Geopolitical Race for Open-Source Dominance

  • The Rise of China: For the first time, downloads of open-source models from Chinese providers (e.g., DeepSeek, Qwen) have surpassed American providers, even within the U.S.
  • Sovereign AI: Delangue argues that the U.S. needs an "American King of Open Source" to ensure foundation models reflect democratic values and entrepreneurship rather than the biases of foreign competitors.
  • The New American Champion: Nvidia is currently dethroning Meta as the primary American contributor to open source, releasing new models or datasets almost daily.

Enterprise Adoption and the Hybrid Model

  • Fortune 500 Integration: Approximately 30% of Fortune 500 companies currently use Hugging Face for open-source models; Delangue predicts this will become a majority by 2027.
  • The 90/10 Split: Future enterprise workflows are expected to be a mix:
      • Proprietary APIs: Used for the "frontier" (top 5% of complex tasks) where labs like OpenAI still hold a performance edge.
      • Open Source: Used for 90% of general workflows to reduce costs, increase speed, and maintain data security.
  • The Skill Gap Barrier: Historically, open source required specialized ML engineers. This barrier is collapsing as AI agents allow standard software engineers—and even non-technical users—to fine-tune and deploy models.

  • Software as a Service for Agents: Platforms are shifting to accommodate a "Token Factory" economy where agents are the primary customers and decision-makers for which models to run.
  • Reduced Concentration of Power: Unlike proprietary APIs, open-source software is easily "forked" (copied and modified), preventing any single company from maintaining a total monopoly on how AI agents function.

Indigenous peoples represent only 6% of the global population but steward 60% of the world's landmass; their ancient knowledge of adaptation and relational governance offers a critical blueprint for transitioning global economies from extractive models to sustainable "living systems."

The Scale and Resilience of Indigenous Stewardship

  • Disproportionate Impact: Indigenous peoples manage the majority of the world’s most biodiverse and environmentally rich landmasses (60%), effectively outperforming all other conservation models.
  • History of Adaptation: Deen Sanders (Worimi Nation) highlights that Aboriginal Australians have survived three separate ice ages and a 20% loss of physical landmass, proving that Indigenous cultures are built on successful climate adaptation over 100,000 years.
  • Ancient Governance: Sanders notes that despite having 500 separate nation groups in Australia with distinct languages, there is no historical evidence of war, prisons, or borders, suggesting Indigenous systems of "relational regulation" are superior to Western models for managing shared resources.

Reimagining Capital and Economic Metrics

  • Externalities and Debt: Sanders argues that the "profit motive" of extraction relies on treating nature as an externality. He asserts that current corporate and state models are effectively "bankrupt" if they were forced to pay the true environmental cost of their operations.
  • Interconnected Systems: Fawn Sharp (Quinault Indian Nation) emphasizes that Western finance often views nature as a dead commodity, whereas Indigenous knowledge treats it as a living system. For example, a copper mine cannot be evaluated without accounting for its impact on water and the surrounding ecosystem.
  • Shifting Financial Frameworks: Indigenous leaders are advocating for a "dual time horizon":
      • Short-term: Innovating within current systems to value natural capital.
      • Long-term: Moving toward an "Indigenous finance platform" that uses metrics based on ecological thresholds rather than just profit maximization.

Practical Policy and Financial Successes

  • The Washington State Model: Fawn Sharp cites the Climate Commitment Act in Washington State as a prime example of Indigenous leadership.
      • Direct Revenue: The act mandates that 10% of all carbon revenues be dedicated directly to tribal nations.
      • Capital Leverage: Since its inception, the act has driven $1 billion into the state treasury for local communities and Indigenous nations.
  • Strategic Investment Tools: Sharp envisions the creation of "Deep Green" and "Deep Blue" bonds—premium investment vehicles that allow the private sector to deploy capital directly into sovereign tribal nations to fund restoration and sustainable development.
  • Generational Accountability: Sanders introduces the concept of "Mabulubul" (Next Seven Again), a framework where every economic and social decision is weighed against its impact on the next seven generations.

A Universal Invitation to "Place"

  • Shared Identity: Sanders asserts that "everyone is indigenous to somewhere," arguing that the modern world has lost its "deeper relationship to place." Indigenous wisdom is not an exclusive "diversity play" but an invitation for humanity to reconnect with the land it depends on.
  • The Role of "Country": In Indigenous philosophy, "Country" (the land) is the teacher, and humans are merely its spokespeople. Survival depends on shifting from a "power grab" for resources to a "shared responsibility" for the environment.

TL;DR

NBIM is navigating a shift from global cooperation to intense competition by integrating advanced scenario analysis and AI-driven monitoring to manage tail risks across its 20 trillion kroner portfolio. The fund emphasizes that geopolitical, concentration, and sustainability risks are now primary financial drivers, necessitating a move from "predicting" the future to "preparing" for multiple volatile outcomes.

Geopolitical Shifts and Scenario Modeling

  • From Cooperation to Competition: The global order has transitioned from the collaborative era of the 1990s to a fragmented landscape of competition and trade restrictions.
      • The "Fragmented World" Scenario: NBIM models show a total collapse of international trust could result in a 37% loss in fund value.
      • Geopolitical Risk Framework: The fund utilizes four pillars: Inform (alignment across boards), Simulate (tabletop exercises), Integrate (stress testing), and Consult (external expertise).
  • Macro Stress Testing: Current models focus on three primary tail risks:
      • War and conflict between great nations.
      • Financial sanctions.
      • Investment restrictions hindering cross-border trade.
  • AI Integration: AI is now used to monitor early warning indicators for these scenarios between quarterly expert reviews.

Counterparty Risk and Securities Lending

  • Massive Exposure Management: NBIM’s securities lending program involves roughly 2,000 billion kroner (10% of NAV) across 7,000 unique securities.
      • Relative Performance: The program generates stable returns, contributing approximately 25% of the fund's long-term relative performance (6 billion kroner in excess return in 2025).
  • Lessons from Credit Suisse: NBIM successfully mitigated risk during the 2023 Credit Suisse collapse by proactively running down exposure from 60 billion kroner to 600 million kroner before the bank’s forced merger.
  • Operational Readiness:
      • AI News Monitoring: An AI tool scans thousands of international news articles in real-time to provide daily summaries of negative counterparty sentiment.
      • The "Runbook": The fund uses a detailed response plan and cross-departmental drills (legal, trading, operations) to handle potential sudden defaults.

The AI Revolution in Portfolio Risk and ESG

  • AI Correction Scenario: NBIM has stress-tested a hypothetical sharp correction in AI valuations, finding it could result in a 31% fund loss (approx. $650 billion USD) due to high concentration in a small group of "hyperscalers" and suppliers.
  • Automated ESG Screening: AI agents now screen the entire portfolio of 7,000+ companies across 60 countries for human rights and environmental violations.
      • Scale Advantage: Manual screening for a single risk would require 3,000 analysts; AI allows a team of eight to monitor the entire portfolio in real-time across all languages.
      • Two-Phase Process: A "light" model filters for red flags, while a complex "multi-agent" system performs deep dives into supply chains and financial relationships.
  • Divestment as Alpha: Since 2012, NBIM has made 633 risk-based divestments, which have added 68 basis points (12 billion kroner) to cumulative equity returns.

Real Assets and Infrastructure

  • Funding and Relative Risk: Because unlisted real estate (1.7% of fund) and renewable infrastructure (0.4%) are not in the benchmark, they represent significant relative risk.
      • Real assets are funded by selling equity and fixed income, meaning they must outperform those asset classes to be considered successful.
  • Proprietary Tooling (UVM): NBIM developed the "Unlisted Valuation Model" (UVM) to replace fragmented, 80-tab Excel sheets from external partners.
      • Standardization: The tool allows for unified risk aggregation across disparate assets like wind parks (energy price risk) and power grids (regulated return/political risk).
      • AI Extraction: AI is now being used to rapidly ingest external data into the UVM to speed up the due diligence process.
