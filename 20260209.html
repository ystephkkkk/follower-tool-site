The Federal Reserve’s Role and Jerome Powell’s Legacy

  • Core Function: The Fed’s primary tools are setting interest rates to manage the "dual mandate" of maximum employment and stable prices (targeting 2% inflation).
  • Jerome Powell’s Performance: Furman, who originally interviewed Powell for a Fed Governor role during the Obama administration, gives Powell a generally positive review despite some critiques.
      • The 2021 Mistake: Furman argues Powell waited too long to address post-pandemic inflation, failing to account for the massive scale of the Biden stimulus and the speed of the recovery.
      • Decisive Correction: Powell "cleaned up the mistake" in 2022 by raising rates faster than most expected, successfully bringing inflation down toward the 2% goal.
  • Leadership Transition: While Trump has nominated Kevin Warsh to take over as Chair in May 2026, Furman suggests Powell should remain on the Board of Governors (his term there lasts until 2028) to provide a "restraining" vote and ensure institutional continuity.

The Threat to Central Bank Independence

  • Historical Context: Independence was solidified by the 1951 Fed-Treasury Accord. Furman cites Harvard research (by Rogoff, Alesina, and Summers) showing that independent central banks consistently produce lower and more stable inflation.
  • Trump’s Interference: Furman views Trump’s attempts to fire Governor Lisa Cook and the reported criminal investigation into Chair Powell as "terrible" developments.
      • Institutional Resilience: Furman notes that these attacks have largely backfired, as financial markets, the Senate, and even Trump’s own party members have rallied to support Powell and the Fed’s autonomy.
  • Consequences of Political Control: If the Fed becomes a tool of the White House, Furman predicts:
      • Higher interest rates and inflation.
      • Increased economic instability.
      • A weaker U.S. dollar and a potential loss of global economic hegemony.

Analyzing Nominee Kevin Warsh

  • Economic Philosophy: Warsh is characterized as more "hawkish" than Powell. He is notably skeptical of Quantitative Easing (QE), arguing that the Fed’s $6 trillion bond portfolio distorts asset markets and creates unnecessary risk.
  • Methodological Critiques: Warsh has criticized the Fed for being too "data-dependent" and relying on "forward guidance" (predicting future rate moves). Furman counters that while data is volatile, the Fed uses it for "signal extraction" to smooth out policy.
  • The "Unicorn" Conundrum: Furman points out a contradiction in Trump’s selection: Trump wants lower interest rates, but Warsh has a history of being an inflation hawk who favors tighter policy.
      • Credibility Gap: Furman argues Trump couldn't find a "unicorn" candidate who is both globally credible and guaranteed to do the President's bidding, as "only a total political hack" would commit to lowering rates regardless of data.

Institutional Stability vs. Political Pressure

  • Healthy Accountability: Furman distinguishes between "mean posts" or tough Congressional questioning (which are acceptable) and actual firing or criminal investigations (which are destructive).
  • The Ability to Error-Correct: Unlike partisan politicians who rarely admit their tax or spending plans failed, the Fed is unique because it is designed to learn from its mistakes and pivot quickly when data changes.
  • Regulatory Policy: Furman admits that while monetary policy must remain independent, it is appropriate for a President to influence the Fed’s regulatory policy (e.g., bank deregulation), as this reflects the philosophy of the elected administration.
