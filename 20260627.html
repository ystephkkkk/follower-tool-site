TL;DR

Democratic Socialists of America (DSA) candidates have swept key New York congressional primaries, signaling a populist shift in the Democratic party, while China’s new open-weight GLM-5.2 model trained on indigenous Huawei silicon demonstrates that US export controls and regulatory hurdles are failing to slow foreign AI development.

The Socialist Sweep in NYC & the Changing Democratic Party

  • The DSA Victories: Democratic Socialists of America (DSA) candidates backed by NYC Mayor Zoran Mamdani swept three highly competitive congressional primaries in safe Democratic seats.
      • Key Upsets: Brad Lander defeated two-term incumbent Dan Goldman in New York's wealthy 10th district. In the 13th district (Harlem/Bronx), Chevalier—a radical 32-year-old PhD candidate with anti-Western positions—defeated a five-term incumbent backed by House Speaker Hakeem Jeffries.
      • Demographic Shift: Panelists noted that the modern socialist base consists primarily of wealthy, highly educated but downwardly mobile progressives, alongside non-assimilated migrant populations.
  • The Israel Wedge Issue: David Sacks pointed out that the Gaza conflict has become a defining motivator for young Democrats (with 80% of Democrats now disapproving of Israel). Dan Goldman's loss to Lander was largely viewed as a referendum on his pro-Israel stance.
  • The Social Media Debate: To counter youth radicalization, Chamath Palihapitiya advocated for under-16 social media bans (similar to measures in the UK, Canada, and Australia). Travis Kalanick (founder of Adams) countered that such bans are a Trojan horse designed to force adult de-anonymization and implement state-level censorship.

China's AI Ascent & The Silicon Bypassing

  • GLM-5.2 Breakthrough: China's Z.ai released GLM-5.2, a 744-billion-parameter open-weights model with a 1-million-token context window, licensed under a highly permissive MIT license.
      • Competitive Performance: GLM-5.2 scored 51 points on the Artificial Analysis index—the highest ever for an open-weight model—beating Western frontier models like GPT-5.5 on coding benchmarks at an 85% cheaper API cost.
  • API Distillation: Gavin Baker (Atreides Management) explained that Chinese labs are using vast "iPhone farms" to query Western APIs (such as Anthropic's Claude), harvesting reasoning traces to train their own models at a fraction of the cost.
  • Bypassing US Sanctions: Z.ai claims the GLM-5 family was trained entirely on domestic Huawei Ascend 910B chip clusters. This signals that China is successfully establishing an independent, end-to-end semiconductor and model ecosystem.
  • Regulatory Self-Sabotage: Sacks criticized US safety regulations and federal bottlenecks championed by players like Anthropic's Dario Amodei. He argued that halting US frontier releases for "safety audits" only hands an advantage to Chinese competitors who are not bound by US laws.

Micron Smashes Earnings Amid the AI Memory Crunch

  • The DRAM Bottleneck: Micron reported a massive earnings blowout, with revenues up 4x year-over-year ($9B to $42B run rate) and its entire High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) supply sold out through 2026. Gavin Baker identified DRAM as the single most critical bottleneck in AI scaling, projected to consume 30% to 40% of all hyperscaler capex next year.
  • Consumer "AI-Flation": The extreme demand for DRAM in AI data centers has starved consumer supply chains. Apple has begun passing these costs to consumers, raising MacBook Neo prices by 14% ($799) and Mac Studio prices by 25%. Similar price hikes are expected for gaming consoles (Xbox, PlayStation).

The Economics of Orbital and Distributed Compute

  • Tesla’s "MEGAPOD": Tesla recently filed a trademark for "MEGAPOD," a modular, self-contained AI data center hardware unit. This could allow Tesla to deploy decentralized GPU clusters at Supercharger stations or bundle them directly into residential Powerwall systems.
  • Terrestrial Energy & Zoning Limits: Building a 1-gigawatt terrestrial data center currently costs roughly $35 billion in silicon and $25 billion in cooling and electrical infrastructure, with local power grids increasingly unable to support the load.
  • The Math Behind Space-Based Compute: Gavin Baker outlined the economics of orbital data centers. With SpaceX's Starship expected to drop 1-gigawatt launch costs to $5 billion, modular orbital compute linked by space-lasers becomes highly competitive ($40B total space cost vs. $60B+ terrestrially), completely bypassing land, cooling, and environmental regulations.
  • Distributed Inference: While distributed training remains unviable over long physical distances due to latency, distributed inference over peer-to-peer consumer networks (utilizing projects like Bittensor or local Groq/Cerebras hardware) is poised to absorb vast amounts of global query volume.

Tech IPO Market Dynamics

  • The Cerebras Broken Deal: Following its IPO, Cerebras fell below its initial deal price. Baker explained that many large institutional portfolio managers automatically sell a stock the moment it breaks its deal price, viewing it as a "broken promise" from bankers. He strongly advised future tech IPOs to utilize Dutch auctions to ensure realistic market pricing.
  • Massive Private Backlogs: The panel discussed the massive backlog of private AI giants preparing to go public, with Baker estimating that Anthropic could trade at a $3 trillion valuation based on future high-margin inference scale.
