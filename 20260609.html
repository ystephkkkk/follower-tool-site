TL;DR

The artificial intelligence boom represents a massive, unprecedented "L-curve" adoption cycle that is currently under 1% penetrated in the enterprise. To capitalize on this shift, Whale Rock Capital Management leverages its proprietary S-curve framework to back dominant foundational models like Anthropic and capture exponential, de-commoditized earnings power across the entire physical hardware supply chain.

The AI "L-Curve" and Anthropic as a High-Conviction Bet

  • The Enterprise AI L-Curve: Unlike typical technological S-curves that feature a slow, decade-long adoption phase, enterprise AI is experiencing a vertical "L-curve" that is straight up. The market is currently less than 1% penetrated, with only 10 basis points (0.10%) of the world's knowledge workers fully utilizing advanced AI primitives.
  • Anthropic as a Private Anchor: Whale Rock holds Anthropic as its highest-conviction private position.
      • Oligopolistic Structure: The foundational model layer has rapidly consolidated from 60 startups down to a three-horse oligopoly of OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google Gemini—mirroring the highly profitable cloud infrastructure triopoly of AWS, Azure, and GCP.
      • Scale and Product Moat: Anthropic is building an entire software ecosystem (SDKs, Claude for Work, and orchestration layers) around its API to build lock-in.
  • Coding as the Primary AI Unlock: Code generation is the first major commercial proof-point of AI replacing high-cost human labor.
      • Market Size: There are roughly 20 million coders globally. If users spend an average of $100/day on tokens (equivalent to $20,000–$30,000/year), coding represents a potential $500 billion addressable market on its own.
      • Agentic Shift: Coding tools have evolved from simple grammar-checkers (e.g., early Copilot at $20/month) to fully agentic platforms like Claude Code, flipping development from 80% handwritten to virtually 100% AI-generated via natural English prompts.

The De-Commoditization of Hardware and the Chip Renaissance

  • A Historic Paradigm Shift: For 40 years, data center architectures remained stagnant under Intel x86 standards, with hardware largely commoditizing. Because AI workloads are growing 10x annually, every hardware component is being pushed to its physical limit, creating a massive wave of "de-commoditization" where hardware players hold powerful IP and soaring margins.
  • Network Infrastructure and Celestica: AI clusters are incredibly network-intensive, shortening standard 7-year upgrade cycles (from 100G to 400G to 800G) down to an annual cadence.
      • Ethernet Monopolization: Celestica, once a low-margin contract manufacturer, leveraged its supercomputing background to become the sole supplier of the Google TPU server and now commands a 50% to 60% market share in the cloud Ethernet white-box switch market.
  • Printed Circuit Boards (PCBs): Traditional servers require basic 10-layer PCBs. In contrast, AI servers require complex, liquid-cooled 40-layer boards with drastically higher Average Selling Prices (ASPs). Whale Rock holds positions in Elite Materials, the dominant supplier of the copper-clad laminate used in these high-performance boards.
  • Cabling and Power Supply Shortages: Modern clusters require extraordinary scale-out cabling. A single new Microsoft data center contains enough fiber cabling to circle the Earth four and a half times.
      • Vendor Lock-in: Corning holds a near-monopoly on specialized, ultra-bendable fiber. Similarly, power supply leaders like Delta and Advanced Energy are seeing 40% annual ASP growth for the next four years because each new Nvidia GPU rack demands 50% to 125% more power.

The Software Threat: Value Migration from SaaS to Silicon

  • The Software Divestment: Five years ago, software represented 40% to 50% of Whale Rock's portfolio. Realizing that legacy software providers were struggling to monetize AI, Whale Rock systematically exited almost all application software positions, even turning net short on the sector in early 2024.
  • Budget and Seat Headwinds: Traditional SaaS companies face a multi-pronged threat:
      • Budget Crowding: CIOs are freezing software spend to redirect capital toward purchasing hardware and foundational model tokens.
      • Seat-Model Erosion: AI-driven labor efficiencies are flattening employee growth, directly threatening the "pay-per-seat" pricing model of enterprise SaaS.
  • The New "Rule of 40" for Tech Investing: Instead of the traditional software metric (revenue growth rate + operating margin), Whale Rock utilizes a modified framework to evaluate AI enablers:

$$\text{Percentage of Sales from AI} + \text{Market Share in that Category}$$

      • Under this framework, companies scoring above 60 are prime investment candidates, whereas traditional software firms remain laggards with AI contributing only 1% to 2% of sales.

Private Allocation and the Whale Rock Research Machine

  • Securing Private Allocations: To invest in elite private tech firms (e.g., Stripe, Databricks, Anthropic) without a traditional venture fund structure, public investors must build deep, multi-year corporate relationships. For its Anthropic investment, Whale Rock secured its allocation by presenting a 90-page, Claude-scoured deep dive on the global coding economy directly to the executive team.
  • The Stripe Diligence Case Study: Whale Rock entered its first private trade with Stripe in 2020 at a $35 billion valuation. By conducting exhaustive diligence on Stripe’s public competitor, Adyen, Whale Rock deduced that Stripe’s actual Total Payment Volume (TPV) was closer to $1 trillion (vastly exceeding public estimates of $550 billion) and carried a superior take-rate of 40 to 50 basis points.
  • The Learning Machine: Whale Rock is structured as a continuous learning machine composed of 10 highly experienced analysts conducting 2,500 to 3,000 face-to-face management meetings annually (10% to 15% with private companies). The firm utilizes the "scuttlebutt" method popularized by Philip Fisher in the 1950s—relying on extensive customer, supplier, and competitor interviews to build conviction.
  • The Megacap Tech Fund: Whale Rock recently launched a fund targeting the top 30 global tech market caps to combat systemic institutional under-allocation to mega-caps. They manage a concentrated portfolio of the 12 to 13 strongest structural winners, betting that the digital economy naturally rewards dominant, global leaders with outsized profit pools.

TL;DR

Bitcoin’s ultimate utility lies in its dual nature as a highly secure store of value and a transactional foundation, where complex financial engineering and leverage should be avoided in favor of simpler, mathematically verifiable layer-1 tools and a disciplined "HODL" investment strategy.

Bitcoin as Collateral and the Evolution of Financialization

  • Redefining Store of Value: Adam Back views savings not as an idle asset, but as "a payment to your future self" that can be used later or deployed as financial collateral.
  • Real Estate Collateralization: Companies like Milo are pioneering Bitcoin-related mortgages that integrate Bitcoin alongside real property in a combined collateral package.
      • This structure allows buyers to secure a home loan without being forced to sell their Bitcoin and trigger early tax liabilities.
      • Because the loan is primarily backed by the stable, underlying real estate value, it functions as an essentially non-liquidatable product, mitigating Bitcoin’s mark-to-market volatility risks.
  • The Dangers of Leverage: Using Bitcoin as collateral to borrow dollars and buy more Bitcoin creates a highly reflexive, quadratic liquidation risk.
      • While extremely low debt-to-equity ratios can be safe, tight margins put traders at risk of rapid liquidation during standard market downturns.
      • Borrowing against Bitcoin to purchase illiquid assets (like real estate) is highly hazardous due to the mismatch in liquidity; Bitcoin can crash in minutes, whereas a property trade takes weeks to finalize.

CeFi, DeFi, and Custodial Counterparty Risks

  • Protocol Complexity in DeFi: Virtual Machine (VM)-based smart contracts introduce immense technical and structural risks due to coding complexity.
      • Audited DeFi contracts are regularly exploited live because high-level languages make it easy to introduce security loopholes that are difficult to anticipate.
      • DeFi effectively exchanges traditional CeFi counterparty risk for massive protocol and smart contract risk.
  • AI-Driven Security and Vulnerabilities: While AI can help hackers scan for software defects, it is also being deployed for defense.
      • The non-profit Bitcoin development group Brink employs AI specialists to perform automated fuzz testing on the Bitcoin codebase, which has successfully uncovered and resolved minor, esoteric bugs.
  • The Post-FTX Custody Landscape: High-profile bankruptcies like Mt. Gox and FTX have pushed institutional traders toward "trilateral agreements."
      • Triparty Storage: Institutions keep their physical Bitcoin with a qualified custodian (such as Comainu, in which Blockstream holds an equity stake) while securing a trading line of credit on an exchange.
      • This arrangement aims to protect assets from being frozen in lengthy exchange bankruptcy proceedings, which historically took nearly a decade in the case of Mt. Gox.

Technical Scaling: Simplicity, Schnorr, and BitVM

  • Replacing Complex Cryptography: Blockstream warns against Multi-Party Computation (MPC) because its highly interactive client-server messaging protocols are overly complex and prone to developer error.
      • Instead, Adam Back advocated for Schnorr signatures (proposed in 2013), which enable MuSig—a simpler, mathematically tidy framework for compact multi-signatures.
  • The Simplicity Programming Language: Developed by Russell O'Connor and live on the Liquid sidechain, Simplicity is a low-level language designed to bring formal security proofs to Bitcoin.
      • UTXO Safety: Unlike EVM chains that use balance-based state models, Simplicity relies on Bitcoin's UTXO model, which inherently eliminates race conditions.
      • Jets Technology: Simplicity utilizes "jets"—optimized native software components—to accelerate complex cryptographic tasks without requiring a network-wide soft fork.
  • L1 Upgrades vs. Workarounds: The Bitcoin development space is split between restoring old opcodes and relying on off-chain execution engines.
      • The Great Script Restoration: Proposed by Rusty Russell, this initiative seeks to fix and restore the original opcodes disabled by Satoshi Nakamoto, making layer-1 extension politically easier to pass.
      • BitVM (Robin Linus): While highly clever, BitVM is an inefficient workaround that requires vast challenge-response games and massive collateral. Resolving these inefficiencies on layer-1 would render such "Frankenstein constructions" unnecessary and streamline layer-2 protocols like Liquid, Lightning, and Ark.

Market Volatility and the Ultimate HODL Strategy

  • Redenominating Savings: Surviving market cycles (such as the painful 2015 bear market where Bitcoin hit a low of $170) requires shifting one's mental unit of account from fiat to Bitcoin.
      • Once investors adopt a "one Bitcoin is one Bitcoin" mindset, short-term fiat price swings lose their psychological impact.
  • The Cost of Market Timing: Bitcoin’s price history exhibits a statistical "fat tail" distribution, meaning a tiny handful of high-performance days account for the asset's yearly gains.
      • Being out of the market to trade short-term trends is highly dangerous; historically, missing just the 12 most profitable days of a year results in net losses.
  • The 200-Week Moving Average (200-WMA): This metric acts as an "up-only" baseline of stable value (sitting around $61,000 at the time of recording).

Long-term success in investing and entrepreneurship requires combining a deep study of historical industry "bedrock" with obsessive learning on the bleeding edge of technological disruptions. Navigating these highly complex, non-linear environments demands a strong foundation in systems thinking, robust storytelling, and an awareness of regulatory capture.

## Systems Thinking and the Financial Bedrock of Investing

  • Systems Thinking as a Shield: Bill Gurley credits systems thinking (specifically citing the book *Thinking in Systems* and his work with the Santa Fe Institute) with helping him navigate "multivariable, non-linear systems" like the stock market.
      • Second-Derivative Effects: Decision-makers must look past single metrics to avoid delayed negative consequences. For example, a major dating platform increased profile lengths to boost short-term engagement, only to discover months later that it severely damaged overall conversion rates.
  • The Importance of Financial Bedrock: While many Silicon Valley venture capitalists lack a deep background in finance, Gurley argues that understanding traditional finance is essential because Wall Street is the ultimate "buyer" of the products VCs create via IPOs and M&A.
      • Gurley's foundation was built on studying classics: Peter Lynch's *One Up on Wall Street*, Burton Malkiel's *A Random Walk Down Wall Street*, Benjamin Graham, Warren Buffett's letters, and Howard Marks.
  • Redefining Value for Tech Growth: Through his friendship with financial writer Mike Mauboussin and legendary investor Bill Miller (who beat the S&P 500 for 15 consecutive years), Gurley learned to view value investing through a growth lens.
      • Miller, a major early Amazon shareholder, argued that "value" simply means an asset is underpriced relative to its future worth—a thesis justified in tech by the long-term compounding power of network effects.

## The Power of Historical Depth vs. Bleeding-Edge Curiosity

  • Differentiating via Historical Mastery: Deeply studying the history of one's field is a powerful, rare differentiator that signals true passion to employers and partners.
      • The John Lasseter Dinner: Benchmark partner Alex Balkanski won a charity dinner with Pixar's John Lasseter, who served a 10-course meal where each course was paired with a classic, historically significant cartoon that Lasseter deemed essential to understanding the evolution of animation.
      • The Magnus Carlsen Trivia Win: World Chess Champion Magnus Carlsen easily won a chess history trivia contest during a tournament break, illustrating that the greatest masters are also historians of their craft.
      • The Picasso Example: Pablo Picasso was a master realist painter by age 14 before transitioning to Cubism; his revolutionary style was built on top of absolute technical mastery.
  • Obsessive Learning on the Edge: While history provides the frame, survival requires rapid, obsessive learning on the dynamic edge of technology waves (e.g., mobile app development in 2008, AI today).
      • Gurley's AI Strategy: Gurley maintains five premium AI accounts to stay competitive. He notes that most users underestimate LLMs because they do not utilize advanced prompting—such as asking models to categorize, rank, and outline pros and cons in a single step, rather than executing sequential manual tasks.
      • Model Choice by Use Case: Gurley notes a highly fragmented model landscape: ChatGPT is favored for its memory/project features, Google Gemini is used for deep restaurant and menu analysis due to integrated Google Review data, Claude is preferred by coders, and Perplexity is highly regarded for financial and international company research.

## AI Dynamics, Western Regulation, and China's Open-Source Engine

  • China's Open-Source Speed Advantage: China has roughly 10 major open-source AI models that share weights, methodologies, and training techniques. Gurley believes this collaborative system allows China to innovate far faster than the highly siloed, proprietary competitive landscape of the West.
  • Regulation as an Oligopoly Moat: Gurley warns that leading US AI firms are actively lobbying for complex, expensive regulations under the guise of safety to establish protective moats, intentionally raising the bar to shut out open-source competitors.
  • The Training Data Wall: The AI industry is running out of organic internet data to train models, a phenomenon Gurley calls "painting in the corners."
      • Companies are spending thousands of dollars an hour to hire human experts for fine-tuning, but Gurley aligns with Meta’s Chief AI Scientist Yann LeCun, who believes LLMs will hit a hard asymptote because language-based models cannot capture non-linguistic, real-world complexity (unlike specialized, constrained models such as Google’s AlphaGo).

## Venture Capital Dynamics, Circular Deals, and Benchmark’s Equal Partnership

  • The Rise of "Circular Deals": The venture capital ecosystem is seeing unprecedented capital inflation. Large cloud service providers are giving billions of dollars in capital to AI startups (such as Anthropic), which is immediately spent back on the providers' own cloud infrastructure. This loop artificially inflates growth rates and obscures true unit economics.
  • Unprecedented Burn Rates: While burning $1 million a month was considered highly risky a decade ago, top AI startups are now burning upwards of $100 million a month ($5 billion a year), making risk management and financial tracking incredibly difficult.
  • Benchmark’s Equal Partnership Structure: Gurley attributes much of Benchmark's success to its non-hierarchical, five-equal-partner structure, which directly addresses the structural flaws of traditional "patriarchal" VC firms:
      • Recruiting Advantage: It makes recruiting elite talent from hierarchical firms easy, as incoming partners are immediately given equal economic standing.
      • Absolute Alignment: Because the pie is split equally, there is no internal competition or annual compensation reviews. Partners actively share deal flow and resources because one partner's win is mathematically equal to everyone else's.
      • The Operational Tradeoff: Without a designated CEO, Benchmark struggles with operational tasks and scaling. For example, rather than managing a highly complex corporate website, former partner Matt Cohler replaced their site with a single-sentence splash page 15 years ago, which remains unchanged today.

## Market Inefficiencies and the Threat of Regulatory Capture

  • Credit Card Duopolies and Stablecoins: Visa and Mastercard enjoy massive 60% operating margins largely due to regulatory capture. While nations like the UK (*Faster Payments*), Argentina (*Pix*), and China (*WeChat Pay*/*Alipay*) long ago adopted instant, free bank-to-bank transfers, US banks have lobbied to delay government alternatives like *FedNow*.
      • The Stablecoin Workaround: Stablecoins like USDC (backed dollar-for-dollar by US Treasuries) bypass the three-day ACH settlement delays and 2.5% credit card transaction fees, offering instant, borderless, and low-cost payments over crypto rails.
  • The Wall Street IPO "Heist": Gurley remains an outspoken critic of the traditional IPO underwriting process, calling the investment banks' ability to hand-pick share prices and select favored institutional buyers a "greedy power grab."   • The Rise of Passive Indexing and ISS Overreach: The massive shift toward passive indexing has left institutional giants (like BlackRock) without the time to evaluate individual corporate proxy votes. Consequently, they outsource decisions to proxy advisory firms like ISS.
      • Gurley criticizes ISS for operating as a "black box" where companies must hire them to learn how they are scored. He argues that ISS focuses on risk mitigation rather than shareholder wealth creation, pointing to their opposition to Elon Musk’s Tesla performance-based compensation package as a key example of misaligned incentives.

TL;DR

Global geopolitics is defined by a fundamental divide between continental powers (like Russia and China) that seek security through territorial conquest and exclusive spheres of influence, and maritime powers (like the US and UK) that leverage geographic moats and international alliances to pursue wealth through open trade.

***

The Geopolitical Framework: Elephants vs. Whales

  • Geopolitics vs. Grand Strategy: Strategic thinker Sarah Paine defines geopolitics as the influence of geography on politics. Grand strategy is the integration of all instruments of national power—diplomacy, military, finance, agriculture—to achieve long-term security.
  • The "Moat" Requirement: Maritime powers ("whales") can defend themselves primarily at sea, whereas continental powers ("elephants") cannot. This geographic reality dictates their entire military, economic, and political architectures.
  • The Three Great Geopoliticians:
      • Alfred Thayer Mahan (Maritime Theory): Argued that global power stems from trade-generated wealth. True maritime power requires a protective moat, a dense internal transport grid, reliable sea egress, a dense coastal population, and stable government institutions (such as transparent, regular elections). Both Russia and China lack these.
      • Halford Mackinder (Heartland Theory): Argued in 1904 that Eurasia is the "pivot area" (the Heartland)—a massive natural fortress insulated from sea power by mountains, deserts, and ice. He believed whoever controls this Heartland could dominate the world.
      • Nicholas Spykman (Rimland Theory): Asserted in 1943 that US security depends on keeping the "rimland" (coastal Eurasia) out of enemy hands. He argued that US security relies on a maritime shield and strong continental allies to act as bases of operation.

***

The Continental Paradigm: Security Through Conquest

  • The Zero-Sum Approach: Lacking protective moats, continental powers face constant border threats. They view security as a zero-sum game of territorial expansion: they must either absorb their neighbors or risk being invaded by them.
  • Primary Mission of the Army: Unlike maritime armies designed for overseas expeditions, a continental power's army has three internal priorities: protecting the ruling regime from its citizens, garrisoning the empire to prevent defection, and defending the borders.
  • The Catastrophic Cost of Land Wars: Because land wars are fought on home soil, continental powers suffer devastating military and civilian casualties compared to maritime nations.
      • *World War II Military Deaths:* Land powers suffered staggering losses (8.5 million Soviet soldiers, 1.3 million Chinese) compared to maritime powers (326,000 British, 295,000 Americans).
      • *World War II Civilian Deaths:* The contrast is even sharper, with over 25.5 million Soviet and 11 million Chinese civilian deaths, while maritime home fronts remained virtually untouched.
  • Rules for Continental Strategy:
      • No Two-Front Wars: Avoid being ganged up on by multiple neighbors.
      • No Great-Power Neighbors: Take on neighbors sequentially, destabilizing the rising ones and ingesting the failing ones.
      • The "Jackal State" Maneuver: Sow discord among neighbors and step in to take the spoils after they have weakened one another.

***

The Maritime Paradigm: Prosperity Through Trade

  • The Positive-Sum Approach: Secured by their ocean moats, maritime powers prioritize national prosperity over raw territorial defense. They treat the world's oceans as a shared "commons" and focus on market access to build compound wealth.
  • Grand Strategy for "Elephant Hunting": To defeat aggressive continental powers without fighting land wars on their terms, maritime powers historically employ a six-rule strategy:

1. *Keep the home economy growing* to fund long-term efforts.

2. *Prevent the continental power from "foraging"* by using naval blockades to cut off their trade.

3. *Rent an "elephant"* by funding and arming a continental ally to fight the main land campaign.

4. *Fight in peripheral theaters* where sea access is highly efficient and land transit is not.

5. *Do not attack the enemy's main land force directly* as an opening move.

6. *Commit land forces only after the enemy is severely weakened* and surrounded by a grand alliance.

***

How Industrialization and Technology Favored Sea Trade

  • The Shifting Currency of Power: The Industrial Revolution changed the source of national power from land ownership (which provided agricultural commodities and peasant conscripts) to commerce, industry, and global trade networks.
  • The Suez Canal Shift: Opened in 1869, the canal routed trade away from overland routes like the Silk Road.
      • *The Supertanker Revolution:* During the Suez Canal's closure from 1967 to 1972, shipping companies adapted by building massive supertankers. Moving oil around the tip of Africa in a supertanker cost roughly $4 per ton, compared to $13 per ton in a smaller ship traveling through the canal.
  • Containerization: In the mid-20th century, Malcom McLean revolutionized maritime commerce by standardizing shipping containers. This single innovation plummeted loading costs from $5.83 per ton to under $0.20 per ton and vastly reduced port times.
  • The Failure of Land-Based Trade (Belt and Road): Xi Jinping’s land-based Belt and Road Initiative cannot compete with maritime transit.
      • *Capacity limits:* The longest trains carry roughly 600 containers and face delays from differing rail gauges. In contrast, modern container ships carry over 21,000 containers, with cargo values exceeding $1 billion.
      • *Security issues:* Maritime shipping can simply detour around conflict zones. Land transit requires end-to-end military control through some of the most unstable regions on Earth.

***

Modern Global Friction and the Post-WWII Order

  • The Invisible Victory: Maritime order is functionally invisible because its objectives are negative—preventing piracy, stopping territorial aggression, and preserving freedom of navigation. Success means nothing happens, making it difficult to prove the value of the defense spending that secures it.
  • Alliances as an Insurance Policy: Following World War II, a generation of leaders built global institutions (the UN, NATO, IMF, and WTO) to substitute ruinous land wars with diplomatic and legal dispute resolution.
  • The Putin and Xi Challenge: Vladimir Putin and Xi Jinping represent a return to the zero-sum continental mindset. They seek to break down international institutions, hollow out alliances, and return the globe to warring, exclusive spheres of influence.
  • The "Chemotherapy" of Sanctions: While economic sanctions rarely trigger immediate collapse, they act as economic chemotherapy. By stripping 1% to 2% of GDP growth annually, they compound over generations. This creates stark regional wealth disparities—such as the gap between North and South Korea—and limits a rogue state's strategic piggy bank.

TL;DR

OpenAI CFO Sarah Friar and UC CIO Jagdeep Singh Bachher discuss how artificial intelligence is transforming finance from a discipline of resource scarcity to one of abundance by automating routine tasks, emphasizing that adaptability, curiosity, and trust remain the ultimate drivers of career and fundraising success.

The Evolution of a Nonlinear Career Path

  • Embrace the Zigzag: Friar highlights that successful careers are rarely linear, sharing her transition from growing up in Northern Ireland during the Troubles to pursuing material science engineering, and eventually pivoting to finance and operations.
  • Prioritize Learning Over Titles: Early in her career, a headhunter challenged Friar’s ambitions by telling her she did not understand what a CFO actually did. This prompted her to work under Salesforce CFO Graham Smith to learn the "guts of the operation" before pursuing executive roles.
      • The "Protagonist" Lesson: Friar advises against making career choices based on external perceptions: *"We are all the protagonist in our own movie and everybody else is watching their movie, no one's watching your movie."*
  • Identify Personal Primitives: Rather than focusing on job titles, students and professionals should focus on their core skills, what brings them energy, and what is economically viable.

Transforming Finance from Scarcity to Abundance

  • Operating Lean with High Leverage: OpenAI’s finance department is highly leveraged, supporting a massive global footprint of over 200 million weekly active users with a lean team of approximately 200 people.
  • Shifting from Sampling to 100% Auditing:
      • Traditional Scarcity: Historically, limited time and human resources forced auditors to rely on "sampling" (e.g., testing 10 random invoices out of 1,000).
      • AI-Enabled Abundance: Using agentic AI, OpenAI’s team can audit 100% of transactions automatically, dramatically increasing financial controls, precision, and risk management.
  • Automating Global Tax Compliance: The tax team has automated the retrieval, formatting, and pre-filling of complex local tax forms worldwide.
      • This automation shifts high-value tax professionals from manual data entry to strategic verification and business planning.
  • Custom GPTs for Investor Relations (IR): During OpenAI's recent multi-billion dollar fundraising round, the finance team built an IR-specific custom GPT trained on internal diligence materials to instantly answer investor queries.
      • Real-time Translation: Friar recalled using the custom IR GPT in South Korea to instantly photograph, translate, and formulate accurate answers to a two-page document written in Korean.

Three Essential Career Skills for the AI Era

  • Curiosity: Professionals must actively experiment with AI tools to demystify technology and overcome fear.
      • Interactive Technology: Friar noted a paradigm shift in how humans interact with tech—such as using OpenAI's developer platform, Codex, and simply asking the tool to "please fix yourself" when a script fails.
  • Adaptability: AI will inevitably automate routine, mundane tasks, but it will also create entirely new jobs. Friar compares the shift to the societal integration of electricity: the fundamentals of work will change, requiring continuous learning.
  • Kindness and Empathy: Despite technological advancements, business remains fundamentally relational. Empathy and helping others without expecting immediate returns is a critical driver of long-term professional success.

Human Connection and the Future of Education

  • The Irreplaceability of Trust: Friar and Bachher emphasize that despite sophisticated AI modeling, fundraising and high-stakes investing still rely entirely on human trust, shared values, and relationship-building.
  • Educational Integration Over Fear: Rather than focusing on students using AI to cheat, educational institutions must shift to "AI-native" learning.
      • AI acts as an on-demand tutor, helping students maintain their "flow state" (e.g., troubleshooting a chemistry problem at midnight instead of waiting days for a teaching assistant's office hours).
  • Job Market Resilience: Friar dispels fears of job automation by pointing out that the global software engineering population has actually grown by 6% since the launch of ChatGPT in 2022, as AI lowers the barrier to entry for coding.

TL;DR

IBM CEO Arvind Krishna projects that the company is on track to achieve quantum advantage by 2026, driven by exponential hardware and algorithmic breakthroughs. Concurrently, IBM is leveraging AI to radically lower operating costs and reshape its workforce, while investing heavily in cybersecurity to safeguard critical tech infrastructure.

Quantum Advantage and Practical Applications

  • Accelerated Timeline: IBM remains on track to demonstrate quantum advantage using its hardware by the end of 2026 or mid-2026, supported by early progress from partners like the Cleveland Clinic and Oak Ridge National Laboratory.
  • Exponential Scale in Molecular Modeling:
    • Rapid Progression: Krishna noted that IBM moved from modeling a 5-atom molecule in June 2025 to a 300-atom molecule by November. By April, they modeled a 12,000-atom molecule (half of the trypsin protein) and are currently pacing toward 30,000 atoms.
    • Computational Limits: At the 12,000+ atom scale, molecular simulations can no longer be performed using classical supercomputers, marking a functional shift toward quantum reliance.
  • Target Industries: Initial applications will focus on materials science and small-molecule drugs.
    • Energy & Chemistry: Key targets include magnetic materials, more efficient EV batteries, advanced lubricants, weather modeling, chemical plant flow simulations, and aerodynamics for aerospace.

National Security and the US Quantum Foundry

  • Public-Private Funding: The US federal government is investing $1 billion to establish a dedicated quantum chip foundry in upstate New York.
  • An Open Ecosystem: IBM has committed to opening this quantum foundry to all US-approved allies and organizations (approximately three dozen companies).
  • The Geopolitical Race:
    • US Leadership: Krishna believes the US maintains a definitive lead over China in quantum technology, estimating the US is "ahead by a couple of years."
    • Strategic Advantage: Securing quantum supremacy is framed as a critical priority for both long-term economic competitiveness and national security.

Driving Near-Term ROI through AI Integration

  • Beyond Personal Productivity: Krishna argues that the real return on investment (ROI) for AI lies in changing corporate operating models rather than simple desktop productivity tools.
  • M&A Cost Synergies:
    • Instant Profitability: IBM utilizes AI to automate contract migration, sales automation, and revenue forecasting during mergers, yielding up to 10 percentage points of profit synergy on "day one" of an acquisition.
    • Proven Case Study: In its most recent acquisition, IBM successfully eliminated 60% of G&A (General and Administrative) expenses immediately upon closing.
  • Workforce Reallocation:
    • Entry-Level Hiring Surge: IBM tripled its college and entry-level hiring in 2026 compared to 2025, betting that AI-enabled junior employees will scale output rapidly.
    • Structural Shifts: Total corporate headcount is expected to remain stable, but the workforce will shift away from classic back-office roles and double down on value-driving areas like R&D, sales, marketing, and creative development.

Project Lightwell and IBM’s Financial Outlook

  • Defensive Cyber Investments: IBM has committed $5 billion toward "Project Lightwell," a security initiative designed to proactively identify and patch critical vulnerabilities in open-source software and the AI supply chain.
  • Market Outlook: Krishna compares the current commercial runway of quantum processing units (QPUs) and cyber tech to where GPUs stood in 2015–2016. He expects to see significant revenue and margin returns within the next two to three years.
  • Capital Adequacy: IBM remains highly active in debt markets, securing favorable rates. Krishna asserts that IBM possesses the internal capital and wherewithal to fund its strategic roadmap over the next five years.

The Math and Misaligned Incentives of Venture Capital

  • The Exit Math Reality: Large funds require exit volumes that often exceed realistic market capacity to return meaningful capital to limited partners (LPs).
      • The $500M Fund vs. $7B Fund Math: A $500 million fund with an average 10% ownership stake requires $5 billion in company exits to return 1x capital, and $15 billion for a 3x return.   • Broken Industry Incentives: The compensation structure of mega-funds disincentivizes actual fund performance.
      • Management Fee Playbook: A General Partner (GP) managing a $5 billion fund that returns a flat 1.01x will generate significantly more personal wealth through management fees than a GP managing a $500 million fund returning 3x.
      • Valuation Inflation: Large funds are incentivized to artificially inflate valuations (e.g., pricing an early-stage startup at $4 billion instead of $100 million) simply to deploy their massive capital reserves, setting up structural hurdles for the startup's future.

AI’s "Atari Stage" and the Impending Token Price War

  • Commoditization of AI Tokens: Maris warns of a critical threat to foundation model companies like OpenAI and Anthropic if cash-rich incumbents weaponize pricing.
      • Google's Spoiler Potential: Google has the financial capacity to arbitrarily slash Gemini's token costs by 80% to capture market share.   • The Atari Analogy: Today's AI models are in a primitive, brittle "command-line" state, comparable to 1980s text games like *Zork* or *Planetfall* that suffered from session resets, lack of memory, and high friction.
      • The Five-Year Leap: Just as gaming evolved from text interfaces to photorealistic open worlds, AI will advance from the "Atari command line stage" to the "PlayStation 10 stage" over the next five years, featuring ambient computing and seamless memory.
  • Investing in the "Machinery": To capture value in this evolution, Maris is avoiding direct investments in large language models. Instead, Section 32 is targeting the underlying infrastructure: physics engines, controllers, specialized GPUs, and software platforms.

Delayed IPOs and the Ethics of Value Creation
