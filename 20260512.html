TL;DR

Codex now features "computer use" capabilities that allow it to interact with local desktop applications via an independent cursor, enabling the agent to complete complex tasks in the background without interrupting the user's workflow.

Background Multitasking and User Experience

  • Non-Invasive Execution: Unlike previous "computer use" implementations that take over the entire screen, Codex operates with its own independent cursor.
      • This allows users to continue working on other tasks while the agent clicks, types, and navigates through local applications in the background.
  • Visual Intuition: OpenAI designed the agent's cursor movement to feel "natural and whimsical," with the arrow turning in the direction of motion to help users visually track what the agent is doing.
  • Multi-App Orchestration: Codex can drive multiple applications simultaneously.
      • Example: In a single session, Ari Weinstein demonstrated Codex downloading a macOS virtual machine in UTM, playing music in Spotify, and adding a tax-related task to the Reminders app.

Technical Framework: Accessibility and Multimodal Integration

  • Beyond Screenshots: Traditional agents rely solely on screenshots and coordinate-based clicking. Codex enhances this by leveraging the macOS Accessibility Framework.
      • Deep Context: By pulling textual data from the accessibility layer, Codex can identify the role of UI elements and "see" content that is currently scrolled off-screen.
  • Model Versatility:
      • Multimodal Models: Use visual data to understand layouts and perform tasks by coordinate.
      • Codex Spark: A faster, non-multimodal model that relies on accessibility data to perform tasks at "superhuman" speeds, often faster than a human could physically move a mouse.
  • Streamlined Development: OpenAI has moved away from training dedicated models for computer use, instead integrating these capabilities into the mainline GPT models available via API.

Security and Granular Permissions

  • App-by-App Authorization: To ensure user trust, Codex does not have blanket access to the file system or screen.
      • Users must explicitly "Allow" Codex to interact with each specific application the first time it is used.
  • Privacy Isolation: If Codex is granted permission to use a productivity app, it remains unable to see or interact with sensitive data in other open applications (e.g., private messages or financial data) that have not been authorized.
  • Onboarding Flow: The setup process is integrated into the macOS settings, using a simple drag-and-drop interface to authorize the agent within the system framework.

The Future of "Superhuman" Computing

  • Speed Benchmarks: Ari Weinstein noted the long-term goal is for Codex to operate 2x to 10x faster than a human, making it an indispensable tool for routine computing.
  • End-to-End Task Management: The vision is to transition Codex from a coding assistant to a general "teammate" capable of handling administrative burdens, such as updating financial spreadsheets in local apps like Numbers.
  • Platform Availability: Computer use is currently available for Codex users on Mac, with a Windows version confirmed to be in development.

TL;DR

True risk management is rooted in contingency planning rather than prediction, requiring leaders to distinguish between being "wrong" and being "reckless" while maintaining a long-term "partnership" culture that prioritizes the institution over the individual.

The Discipline of Risk Management

  • Prediction vs. Contingency Planning: Blankfein argues that while people claim to predict the future, effective management is actually about preparing for various scenarios.
      • The "False Start" Approach: Success comes from being so prepared that you react to a "trigger" (the starting gun) faster than competitors, rather than trying to guess when the gun will go off.
      • Mitigating Costs: Insurance for a disaster is expensive when the disaster is imminent; risk management is about taking low-cost steps to mitigate adverse consequences during "quiet" times.
  • The "Mark-to-Market" Mindset: At Goldman, marking assets to market religiously served as an early warning system.
      • If a trader disputed a mark, the instruction was simple: "Sell a fraction." If the bid wasn't there, the mark was wrong.
  • Leverage and Complexity: Modern technology and AI introduce massive leverage. In the past, a mistake might be contained; today, a software error can execute 70,000 flawed transactions in seconds, creating systemic risks that are difficult to test or verify.

Leadership Under Pressure

  • Temperament in Crisis: Blankfein describes his resting state as "not resting," meaning he stays wound up normally but finds that "things slow down" during a crisis.
      • Disarming Tension: He uses humor (e.g., asking a colleague if they’ll finish their salad during an active shooter drill) to break the "freeze" of chaos and get people back to doing their jobs.
  • The Unpredictability of Resilience: You cannot tell how someone will perform in a crisis based on their "cover."
      • Example: Blankfein noted that some "man's man" types crumbled during the 2008 financial crisis, while others who seemed physically frail remained perfectly composed.
      • Board Selection: His primary advice for picking board members or leaders is to find people who have already successfully navigated a major crisis.
  • The "Socialization" of Decisions: Even in high-pressure moments, a leader should socialize tough decisions with partners. This enlists support from the "pliable" middle and honors the fact that senior staff are co-owners, not just subordinates.

Preserving a Partnership Culture

  • Partnership vs. Corporate Mindset: A partnership culture creates stability because leaders view their fortunes as tied to the whole enterprise rather than a specific silo (e.g., an Amazon retail leader rarely worries about AWS, but a Goldman partner worries about the entire firm).
  • "Long-Term Greedy": This philosophy prioritizes the firm’s reputation and long-term relationships over short-term transactional gains.
      • Commitment: During the financial crisis, Blankfein insisted on honoring lending commitments to clients like Chrysler, even when it was painful, to ensure the firm remained a trusted peer after the crisis.
  • The Role of Alumni: Blankfein established a formal alumni office to maintain lifelong ties with former employees. He emphasizes that "ex-Goldman" becomes a permanent part of an individual's identity, which reinforces the firm's global influence and recruiting power.

The Strategic Implications of AI

  • Winner-Take-All Dynamics: In finance, technology is often a "winner-take-all" game. Being milliseconds faster than a competitor can mean capturing the entire opportunity while others get nothing.
  • The "Black Box" Risk: A major concern with AI is the loss of intuition. In old trading rooms, if a price was wrong, the "shrieking" would stop because everyone's intuition caught the error. With AI, transactions happen behind the scenes without a visible "thought process" or bibliography, making errors harder to catch before they scale.
  • The Hyperscaler Advantage: Blankfein notes that current AI bets are being driven by founders (principals) putting their own "stacks of chips" on the table, which leads to more deeply held convictions—and higher stakes—than bets made by professional managers.

Advice for the Next Generation

  • Become a "Complete Person": Blankfein cautions against being too narrow or "dropping out" too early.
      • Range as Resilience: Understanding history and the humanities provides perspective. Knowing that the world has survived the Civil War, the 1960s, and the Cuban Missile Crisis (DEFCON 2) helps a leader realize that current political or economic polarization is not unprecedented.
  • Peer Influence: Your current cohort will run the world's major institutions in 30 years. Your reputation with them is being built today.
  • The Boss’s Responsibility: Leaders must realize that their subordinates go home and talk about them every night. Blankfein’s question to new managers: "What do you want them saying about you?"

Redefining Culture as the "Organizational Glue"

Culture is often dismissed as abstract, but it serves as the fundamental driver of how an organization makes decisions, takes risks, and delivers on its mission.

  • Behavioral Measurement: Culture is not a value statement or a poster; it is defined by what is measured, what is rewarded, and—most importantly—what is tolerated.
  • The Force Multiplier: True culture exists when employees make the right decisions autonomously. It shifts the organizational default from "No" to "Yes, if..."
  • Leadership Responsibility: Leadership is an earned responsibility to create conditions where teams feel supported rather than policed.

The Decency Quotient (DQ)

While IQ and EQ are standard metrics for leadership, Banga introduces "DQ" as the essential component for building trust and speed.

  • Fairness Over Niceness: DQ is not about being "nice." It is about being fair, direct, and consistent. It requires telling the truth even when uncomfortable and providing feedback with absolute clarity.
  • Driving Speed: A high DQ increases organizational velocity because employees trust the system. When people feel safe to challenge ideas, decision-making improves.
  • Hand vs. Finger: Effective leadership should feel like a "hand on the back" (pushing people forward) rather than a "finger in the face" (intimidation). Fear may drive short-term results but fails to build judgment or initiative.

Clarity of Purpose Through Radical Simplicity

In large organizations like Mastercard or the World Bank, the primary obstacle to progress is often confusion rather than active resistance.

  • The Hardship of Simplicity: Making a complex mission simple is a leader's most difficult task. Speaking without jargon ensures that the mission is accessible to every level of the workforce.
  • "Kill Cash" (Mastercard): To pivot from a payments processor to a technology platform, Banga unified the company under the simple goal of killing cash. This clarity encompassed everything from data analytics to financial inclusion.
  • "Job Creation" (World Bank): To combat poverty, Banga simplified the World Bank’s multifaceted efforts into a single focus: creating jobs.
      • State of Being vs. Mind: Poverty is a state of "being" (solved by aid) and a state of "mind" (solved by the dignity and optimism of productive work).
