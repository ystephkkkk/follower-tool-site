TL;DR

Howard Marks argues that artificial intelligence has rendered the world more unpredictable than at any point in history, while the private credit market is transitioning from a period of "promise without understanding" to a necessary cyclical correction where "warts" and defaults will inevitably emerge.

The Unpredictability of the AI Era

  • Unprecedented Uncertainty: Marks asserts that AI makes the future less predictable than ever before. He notes that while most investors base actions on what they think will happen, they often fail to account for the low probability of those predictions being correct in an AI-driven world.
  • Economic Displacement: The speed of change is exemplified by companies like Block, which recently reduced its workforce by 40% (4,000 employees) in a single day because AI could perform the work faster and cheaper.
  • Investment vs. Technology: While the technology itself is not a bubble, the investments surrounding it may be. Marks cautions against lending to AI companies for 30–100 years when the next five years are unpredictable; investors should seek equity upside if they are taking fundamental business-model risk.

The State of Private Credit

  • Loss of "Specialness": In 2011, non-bank lenders could demand high rates and safety. Today, the "early success" has been emulated to the point that the liquidity premium has shrunk.
      • Yield Parity: Marks notes that with public credit yielding ~7% and direct lending yielding ~8.25%, the 125-basis-point spread represents a fair equilibrium but is no longer "lush" or a "special" deal.
  • Retail Risks: There is significant concern regarding the sale of private credit vehicles (like BDCs) to retail investors who may not fully grasp the ramifications of illiquidity and the absence of mark-to-market pricing.
  • Transparency Issues: Investor unhappiness regarding transparency usually only surfaces when "the stuff hits the fan." These vehicles were always illiquid, but these flaws are ignored during "happy times."

Credit Cycles and the Return of Defaults

  • The 17-Year "Good Time" Cycle: Since the 2009 bottom, the market has seen nearly 17 years of generally easy credit and low defaults. Marks warns that "the worst of loans are made in the best of times" because lenders compete by lowering standards and skepticism.
  • Market Complacency: Current yield spreads on sub-investment grade credit are at the low end of the normal range, suggesting that the market is not currently pricing in an elevated default cycle.
  • The "Cockroach" Theory: Referencing Jamie Dimon and recent bankruptcies like First Brands, Marks suggests that fraud and undeserving credits are often perpetuated when money is freely available, only to be exposed when the cycle turns.

Human Intuition vs. AI Analysis

  • AI as a Predictive Tool: Marks views AI as a engine for "hypotheses" rather than absolute answers. It excels at recognizing historical patterns and marshaling data without emotional or logical errors.
  • The Human Margin: Marks identifies critical areas where AI lacks capability:
      • Intuition: AI cannot experience the "hairs standing up on the back of the neck" when reading a suspicious prospectus.
      • Character Assessment: Humans remain better judges of "bad people" and management integrity.
      • Market Extremes: Marks doubts AI can accurately identify the "buying point" during a psychological market crash, as it relies on extrapolation rather than the "sense" of a seasoned investor.

TL;DR

Anduril is launching "Arsenal-1" in Ohio, a $1 billion hyperscale manufacturing facility designed to mass-produce autonomous fighter jets and restore the U.S. "magazine depth" required to deter superpower conflicts. Founder Palmer Luckey aims to shift defense from a slow, taxpayer-funded contractor model to a high-speed, privately funded "product company" approach.

Arsenal-1: Scale and Production Timeline

  • Pickaway County Campus: A $1 billion project spanning an eventual 5 million square feet, projected to bring 4,000 jobs to Central Ohio over the next decade.
  • The "Fury" Jet: Production of the YFQ-44A Fury, an autonomous fighter jet, is scheduled to begin within weeks at the first building.
      • The prototype was developed and flight-tested in less than a year, a pace Luckey notes is rarely seen in defense.
  • Hyperscale Manufacturing: Luckey is applying lessons from his time at Oculus VR to defense, arguing that unit costs only become affordable when building hundreds or thousands of units rather than a handful of prototypes.
  • Rickenbacker Logistics: Finished aircraft will be flown directly from the nearby Rickenbacker International Airport to customers.

A Disruptive Defense Business Model

  • Private Risk vs. Taxpayer Funding: Unlike traditional contractors, Anduril uses its own capital to develop products and facilities.
      • Incentive Structure: Luckey believes defense companies should "live in fear" of losing their own money if they fail, rather than lounging on taxpayer-funded contracts.
  • Modular Factory Design: The facility avoids "monuments" (large, permanent concrete/steel fixtures).
      • Reconfigurability: Everything—including gantry cranes—is mobile, allowing the factory to be quickly repurposed for cruise missiles or different aircraft models based on immediate military needs.
  • Workforce Development: Anduril does not require college degrees for any role, including engineering, focusing instead on skill-specific training and "problem solver" mindsets.

Autonomous Warfare and "Magazine Depth"

  • The "Twinkie" Concept: Luckey advocates for autonomous systems that can be stored in a warehouse for decades like a "Twinkie in a wrapper," ready to be activated without the massive personnel costs required to maintain a manned fleet.
  • Strategic Deterrence: The primary goal of Arsenal-1 is "magazine depth"—the ability to sustain a high-intensity conflict for hundreds of days, signaling to adversaries like China that the U.S. cannot be out-produced or waited out.
  • AI Morality: Luckey argues that "smart" autonomous weapons are more moral than "dumb" weapons (like traditional landmines).
      • Collateral Damage: He contends that an algorithm capable of distinguishing a tank from a school bus is a moral necessity, provided humans remain accountable for the deployment and rules of engagement.

Geopolitics and Policy Governance

  • Civilian Oversight: Luckey explicitly rejects "corporatocracy," stating that corporate executives should not have the power to unilaterally shut off military systems or dictate foreign policy.
      • He insists that all lethal decision-making and international sales must remain under the control of elected civilian leadership.
  • China's Advantage: Luckey acknowledges that China currently possesses a superior manufacturing supply chain and faster procurement cycles. He views Anduril as a necessary counterweight to prevent the U.S. from falling into a permanent "back foot" position.
  • Meta Partnership: Despite his past firing from Facebook (now Meta), Luckey is now collaborating with the company to apply their multi-billion dollar R&D in VR and AI to U.S. military problems, prioritizing national security over personal grievances.

TL;DR: Coinbase CEO Brian Armstrong posits that crypto has evolved from a niche asset into the foundational infrastructure for global finance, driven by the massive scaling of stablecoins, institutional tokenization, and the emergence of "agentic commerce" where AI agents become the primary users of digital wallets.

The Evolution of the "Everything Exchange"

  • Strategic Shift: Coinbase is moving toward becoming an "Everything Exchange," integrating crypto, stocks, commodities, FX, and prediction markets into a single on-chain platform.
  • Trust as a Moat: Armstrong attributes Coinbase’s market leadership—holding over 12% of all global crypto—to its focus on regulatory compliance, audited financials as a public company, and high-quality design.
  • Institutional Integration: Major players like BlackRock and Apollo are moving toward tokenizing their funds, while banks are using Coinbase’s "Prime" and developer platforms to integrate crypto services.
  • Market Maturation: While Bitcoin remains volatile, Armstrong notes that volatility decreases each cycle as ownership shifts from retail speculators to institutional holders and sovereign wealth funds.

Stablecoins as Global Payment Infrastructure

  • Growth Potential: Armstrong predicts stablecoins could grow 100x to 1000x from their current $300 billion market cap, describing them as the first payment rail to be simultaneously fast, cheap, and global.
  • Primary Use Cases:
      • B2B Cross-Border Payments: Currently the largest driver, replacing slow and expensive SWIFT transfers for companies sourcing goods internationally.
      • Remittances & E-commerce: Facilitating near-instant transfers for less than a tenth of a cent.
  • Impact on Government Debt: Stablecoins are already the 16th largest holders of US Treasuries. Armstrong argues that dollar-backed stablecoins help preserve the dollar’s status as a reserve currency while creating massive demand for T-bills.
  • Private vs. Public Issuance: Armstrong suggests that while Central Bank Digital Currencies (CBDCs) exist (e.g., in China), private stablecoins following regulated standards innovate faster and offer better privacy protections.

The Intersection of AI and Blockchain

  • Agentic Commerce: AI agents are becoming significant economic actors. Since agents cannot open traditional bank accounts (which require human IDs), they are adopting stablecoin wallets to transact.
  • Machine-to-Machine Payments: Armstrong foresees a world with more AI agents than humans, where bots pay other bots for web resources, API calls, or booking travel.
      • Early Traction: A Coinbase-supported protocol (X402) has already facilitated 100 million transactions for agent-to-agent requests.
  • Internal AI Adoption: Coinbase has moved to an "AI-first" internal culture.
      • Engineering: Over 50% of Coinbase’s code is now written by AI.
      • Support: Approximately 60% of customer support tickets are resolved via AI.

Regulatory Realities and Political Engagement

  • Policy Muscle: Originally intending to be "mission-focused" and apolitical, Armstrong shifted to active lobbying and litigation to counter what he termed "unlawful lawfare" from US regulators.
  • US Competitiveness: He views the current US political shift toward being the "crypto capital of the world" as essential to prevent the industry from moving entirely offshore to hubs like London, Dubai, or Singapore.
  • Financial Crime: Armstrong refutes the "crypto is for crime" narrative, noting that illicit activity accounts for less than 0.5% of crypto transactions, compared to roughly 4% for US dollar cash.
  • Quantum Security: To address future threats, Coinbase has formed an advisory council to transition Bitcoin and Ethereum networks to "post-quantum" cryptographic algorithms.

Long-Term Vision: Longevity and Progress

  • NewLimit: Armstrong co-founded NewLimit, a biotech firm focused on "epigenetic reprogramming" to restore cellular function to a younger state.
      • Clinical Milestones: The company aims to start its first drug clinical trials next year.
  • Longevity Escape Velocity: Armstrong believes there is a significant chance that within our lifetime, science will advance by more than one year for every year lived, potentially extending human life by centuries.
  • Entrepreneurial Philosophy: He encourages young founders to "stay hungry and foolish," emphasizing that breakthroughs come from being willing to look stupid and trying many things until one works.

Productivity and the Impact of AI

  • Rising Productivity: Powell noted that the U.S. has experienced several years of unexpectedly high productivity, which allows for rising incomes without necessarily fueling inflation.
  • The AI Effect: While generative AI may expand potential output in the long run, Powell warned of short-term inflationary pressures.
