Bill Ackman: Here's What the Market is MISSING

TL;DR

Pershing Square CEO Bill Ackman discusses his shift toward backing durable, non-disruptable, founder-led businesses in the AI era, while outlining his strategy to transform the Howard Hughes Corporation into a long-term compounding machine modeled after Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway.

Evolution of Activism and Investment Philosophy

  • Prioritizing Business Quality: Ackman’s core philosophy has evolved to focus intensely on long-term, durable, and non-disruptable growth. As Pershing Square's capital base grew, the necessity of holding concentrated, long-term positions eclipsed short-term liquid plays.
  • Constructive Activism: In its early days, Pershing Square had to wage public campaigns just to get corporate attention.
      • *The Wendy’s/Tim Hortons Precedent:* Early on, Ackman couldn't get a phone call back from the Wendy’s CEO. He partnered with Steve Schwarzman at Blackstone to write a public fairness opinion advocating for a Tim Hortons spin-off, which forced the spin-off and doubled their investment.
      • *Modern Relationship Capital:* Today, Ackman’s reputation allows him to collaborate directly with S&P 500 CEOs constructively without aggressive public proxy battles.
  • The Public Market Shield: Ackman views large, concentrated shareholders as crucial buffers for public companies. They provide management the coverage to make multi-year, long-term decisions that may hurt short-term quarterly earnings but build massive enterprise value.

Navigating AI Disruption and the "SaaS-pocalypse"

  • Undervalued Tech Giants: While short-term capital chases chips, energy, and start-ups, legacy giants like Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon are being valued as "old-fashioned" businesses and are currently undervalued relative to their massive AI advantages.
  • The "SaaS-pocalypse" Risk Profile: Independent software companies relying on high-margin, niche software (e.g., charging $30,000 per year per seat) are at immense risk of AI-driven disruption. Conversely, platforms like Microsoft, which charge very low per-seat prices, have a highly defensible moat.
  • Venture Underwriting in Late-Stage Companies: Highly valued, late-stage private companies like SpaceX, OpenAI, Anthropic, and Palantir must be underwritten using venture frameworks rather than traditional discounted cash flow (DCF) models.
      • *The SpaceX Moat:* SpaceX benefits from a near-monopoly on low-cost space launch systems, making it a critical infrastructure provider even for competitors like Amazon. Ackman invested in X, xAI, and SpaceX (encouraged by Ron Baron) via SPVs.
  • Slow Enterprise AI Adoption: Despite the massive hype, actual enterprise implementation of AI remains in the very early, pilot-heavy stages. At Pershing Square, AI is currently used strictly for legal, compliance, and back-office workflows.

The Market "Rubber Band" and the Founder Premium

  • The Rubber Band Effect: Stock valuation serves as a tether. When valuations stretch too far from the fundamental present value of future cash flows—whether in a bubble or a panic—the "rubber band" inevitably snaps back. Recognizing this allowed Ackman to successfully call the market bottoms during the March 2020 COVID panic and again in late 2023.
  • The Founder Advantage: Founder-led companies possess a massive structural advantage over hired CEOs in navigating tech disruption.
      • *Long-Term Alignment:* The average S&P 500 CEO has a tenure of 3 to 4 years and is incentivized to avoid mistakes. Founders, having their entire wealth and reputation tied to the firm, have the internal authority to make massive, risky, but necessary pivots (e.g., Mark Zuckerberg acquiring Instagram and WhatsApp).

Replicating Berkshire Hathaway via Howard Hughes Corp

  • The Insurance Float Engine: Warren Buffett built Berkshire Hathaway by using insurance float (premiums collected upfront) to invest in compounding common stocks. Because traditional investment talent rarely works for insurance companies, Buffett's compounding model is rarely replicated successfully.
  • The Howard Hughes Pivot: Born out of the Chapter 11 restructuring of General Growth, the Howard Hughes Corporation owns massive, master-planned cities (such as Summerlin in Las Vegas, comprising 26,000 acres).
      • *The Strategy:* Rather than reinvesting all real estate cash flows back into development, Ackman is converting the business into a compounding machine. Over the next week, Pershing Square will begin redirecting Howard Hughes' cash flow into building a brand-new insurance operation to compound assets over the next 50 years.
  • Three Paths to Invest with Pershing Square:
      • *Pershing Square Management Company:* An asset-light, high-margin royalty on the compounding of the underlying funds.
      • *Pershing Square Holdings (PSH):* The public fund holding Ackman's core stock ideas, currently trading at an 18% discount to NAV.
      • *Howard Hughes Corporation:* A play on the next Berkshire Hathaway compounding model, currently trading at a steep discount to its liquidation value.

The Paradigm Shift to Agent-Centric Computing

  • The Agent as the Digital Center: The traditional device-centric computing model is changing. Instead of the smartphone or PC acting as the hub of digital life, autonomous AI agents are becoming the center of the user experience, with various devices acting as mere endpoints.
  • On-Intent Action vs. User Initiation:
      • Today's devices are built to respond to human-initiated actions. Agentic AI, however, operates continuously, handles context over time, breaks down high-level goals into multi-step actions, and coordinates autonomously across local datasets and the cloud.
  • Hardware Architecture Redesign:
      • Engineered for Autonomy: Running an always-active agent requires an overhaul of device hardware. It demands high integration across compute domains and extreme power efficiency to prevent battery drain.
      • Heterogeneous Compute: Next-generation edge devices require powerful CPUs for task orchestration, alongside high-compute-density NPUs and GPUs to run complex local models.
  • The Scale of Endpoints: This transition will impact a massive global device footprint, which currently includes approximately 6 billion smartphones, 2 billion personal AI devices (such as smart glasses and wearables), 2 billion PCs, and 500 million connected vehicles.

Physical AI in Automotive, Robotics, and Enterprise

  • AI-Defined Vehicles: The automotive industry is transitioning from software-defined to AI-defined vehicles. These systems utilize two layers of intelligence operating as one:
      • The Cockpit Layer: A highly personalized assistant that travels with the user across devices.
      • The Driving Layer: An autonomous system utilizing cameras, radars, and sensors to perceive, plan, and navigate in real time.
  • Hierarchical Robotic Systems: Qualcomm is deploying unified hardware and software platforms for the rapidly accelerating robotics sector (including AMRs, industrial arms, and humanoids). This architecture requires a three-tiered computing hierarchy:
      • Instant Execution: Reflexive, microsecond-level actions (e.g., maintaining balance or adjusting grip).
      • Action and Grounding: Medium-level task execution and spatial awareness.
      • Reasoning: High-level cognitive planning.
  • Industrial Vision and Enterprise AI: Real-time computer vision is moving to local edge processing. Vision-powered cameras can autonomously monitor security, compliance, and traffic flows in retail, warehousing, energy, and smart cities.

6G: Wireless Infrastructure Tailored for AI

  • AI-Native Air Interface: 6G is being designed as the first generation of wireless built explicitly for the age of AI, structured around three core pillars:
      • Uplink-Centric Connectivity: Designed for continuous context exchange, allowing wearables like smart glasses to stream real-time video feeds back to cloud systems.
      • Distributed Edge Computing: AI inference capabilities will be embedded directly into radio base stations and telecom central offices, transforming the network into a distributed data center.
      • Environmental Sensing: By analyzing RF signal reflections like radar, 6G networks can map physical spaces to create real-time "digital twins" of neighborhoods and cities. This spatial mapping provides instant physical context for autonomous agents and drone delivery management.

The Token Economy and Distributed Intelligence

  • Exploding Token Consumption: The autonomy of agentic AI creates a massive surge in token demand, altering both the economics and architecture of computing.
      • Level 1 (Conversational): Simple prompt-and-response tasks require ~10,000 tokens.
      • Level 2 (Reasoning): Multi-turn logical reasoning requires ~100,000 tokens.
      • Level 3 (Agentic): Autonomous, multi-step agent workflows require 1,000,000+ tokens per task.
      • Global Projections: Global token demand within a 10-second window is projected to increase 40x, rising from 31.7 billion tokens in 2026 to 1.27 trillion tokens by 2030.
  • Hybrid Workload Optimization: To handle this scale, intelligence must be distributed dynamically between the edge and the cloud. Intelligent orchestrators route workloads to the most efficient processing location:
      • Coding Workloads: Distributing tasks via Claude Code yields a 60% reduction in costs and saves up to 1.4 million tokens.
      • Web Creation Workloads: Utilizing local-cloud hybrid execution for page generation tasks delivers identical results with 30% fewer tokens and a 4x reduction in computing costs.

## Scaling Infrastructure and Closing Europe's Compute Gap

  • Infrastructure Commitments: Mistral has committed €4 billion to build out data center capacity across France and Sweden.
      • Capacity Targets: The company aims to reach 200 megawatts of capacity by 2027 and 1 gigawatt by 2030.
      • New Inference Site: A newly announced, high-availability site in France will generate low-carbon "tokens" for Mistral's studio, public cloud, and private cloud offerings.

      • Koyeb Acquisition: Mistral is leveraging its recent acquisition of Koyeb to run serverless, highly efficient GPU environments.
      • US Lab Interest: Due to capacity constraints and latency demands, major US AI firms (such as OpenAI and Anthropic) have inquired about renting Mistral's European infrastructure for training and inference.
  • Dual-Service Model: Mistral provides serverless, high-value API toolkits for enterprises, alongside GPU-as-a-service (via managed Kubernetes) for AI research labs.

## The Macroeconomics of European AI Sovereignty

  • The Two-Year Window: Mensch warns that European policymakers and enterprises have a two-year window to establish independent digital infrastructure, or risk permanent economic dependency on US tech giants.
  • The Digital Trade Deficit:
      • Global wages sit around $50 trillion, with AI spending projected to reach 10% ($5 trillion) over the next five years.
      • Europe represents approximately $9 trillion of the global wage pool, translating to over $1 trillion in potential AI spending.
      • Currently, European reliance on US digital services results in a $250 billion capital outflow. Without domestic sovereignty, this commercial deficit will compound aggressively.
  • Global Sovereign Partnerships: Mistral is exporting its software stack to help other nations build autonomous AI platforms. This includes a partnership with Singtel in Singapore to deliver a fully localized, resilient AI stack.
  • Hardware Independence: While US tech giants build custom ASICs, Mistral is focused on software optimization and utilizing off-the-shelf hardware. Mensch does not rule out designing custom chips in the future, but currently maintains a strong partnership with Nvidia.

## Launching "Vibe" and the Rise of Agentic Enterprise Workflows

  • The Vibe Platform: Mistral has launched "Vibe," an enterprise agentic platform that unifies their coding agent platform with the "Le Chat" user interface.
  • Hybrid Data Control: Unlike closed-source alternatives, Vibe offers a split deployment model.
      • Stateful Component: Resides on the customer's secure tenant, giving them full governance over internal context and data.
      • Stateless Component: Utilizes Mistral’s highly efficient, low-latency GPU infrastructure strictly for token generation.
  • Human-in-the-Loop Workflows: The platform combines autonomous execution (using secure sandboxes) with deterministic gates. For high-stakes operations like procurement or billing, the system pauses to require human validation before proceeding.
  • The SaaS Disruption: Agentic AI is fundamentally shifting the enterprise software landscape. Instead of relying on rigid, siloed SaaS tools, companies are using models to act as orchestrators directly connected to their internal systems of records.

## Enterprise Security and "Real-World" AI Capabilities

  • Addressing Cyber Vulnerabilities: Mistral is actively building open-source red-teaming and blue-teaming harnesses to help organizations detect code vulnerabilities, update legacy codebases, and block network penetration.
  • Dynamic Access Controls: A major challenge of deploying autonomous agents is the "need to know" problem—agents indexing messy company documentation might accidentally expose sensitive files to unauthorized employees. Mistral is building dynamic, contextual security architectures to mitigate this.
  • Moving Beyond "PowerPoint AGI": Mensch dismisses the popular industry focus on Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) as a legacy, "messianic" PowerPoint concept. Instead, he views progress as a practical, messy process of connecting AI to complex real-world variables.

TL;DR

AI-native services companies represent a paradigm shift by selling finished outcomes rather than software tools. By replacing traditional outsourced labor with AI-driven operations, these startups can unlock 50%+ gross margins in trillion-dollar markets like tax, law, and insurance.

1. Selecting the Right Market & The "Sam Altman Test"

  • The Paradigm Shift: Instead of building co-pilots for internal customer use, AI-native service companies deliver the final outcome directly to the client, capturing budgets already allocated for external vendors.
  • Four Attributes of Ideal Markets:
      • Low Trust: The client already outsources the work and cares strictly about the final deliverable rather than how it was produced.
      • Low Judgment at Task Level: Tasks can be broken down so that AI handles the bulk of the workflow, leaving human experts to apply high-value judgment at key milestones.
      • High Intelligence Threshold: The work must be complex enough to require a sophisticated blend of frontier models and human oversight.
      • Regulatory Moats: High regulatory hurdles raise the barrier to entry. For example, YC-backed *Panacea* uses experienced consultants paired with an AI platform to deliver faster, high-quality FDA clearance services for biotechs.
  • The "Sam Altman Test": Founders must ask: *As underlying AI models improve, does our service get stronger, or does the model itself commoditize us?* The goal is to build a workflow layer that benefits directly from model upgrades.
  • Where to Exercise Caution: Avoid markets heavily reliant on physical equipment and on-site labor. These businesses lack software-like scale and leverage.

2. Team Dynamics and Operational Fluency

  • Domain and Model Fluency: To sell to skeptical buyers in regulated industries, founders must possess deep domain credibility. Simultaneously, they require deep technical model fluency to anticipate and build for the capabilities of upcoming frontier models.
  • Operational Rigor: Building a service company means running an operational engine. Founders must master concepts like throughput, cycle times, and Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs).
      • *Case Study:* *General Legal* (an AI-native law firm backed by YC) was founded by alumni of Cooley, Fenwick, and CaseText. They optimized lawyer output and reduced cycle times by integrating shift work into their staffing model.

3. Product Strategy and the Battle Against Variance

  • The Inverted Software Model: In AI services, the human employee is the interface to the customer, while the internal software product acts as the engine that scales human output nonlinearly.
  • The Threat of Variance: Inconsistent output is the single greatest threat to an AI-native service business. Customers prioritize reliability over speed or cost.
  • Operational Product Metrics: Throughput, cycle times, and task completion times must be tracked with the same intensity as Daily Active Users (DAUs) in traditional software.
  • Human-in-the-Loop Scaling: Human labor must scale nonlinearly relative to revenue. If adding revenue requires a 1:1 linear addition of human staff, the business model breaks.

4. Sales, Pricing, and the "Early Demand Trap"

  • The Early Demand Trap: It is incredibly easy to sign early pilots, but over-committing too quickly overwhelms early operational capacity. This forces founders to resolve product bottlenecks with manual labor, halting software development. Founders should limit initial pilots to a small, manageable handful.
  • Value-Based Pricing Models:
      • *Per-Unit Pricing:* Charging per return, per claim, or per loan is clean and highly comprehensible to buyers.
      • *Outcome-Based Pricing:* Pricing based on the completed objective (e.g., *Panacea* pricing per completed study rather than hourly) aligns incentives.
      • *Models to Avoid:* Cost-plus pricing permanently caps upside. Straight-line undercutting of legacy competitors cheapens the brand and implies low quality.

5. P&L Mechanics and the Build vs. Buy Trap

  • AI Operating Leverage: Successful execution drives down Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)—which consists of model API costs, hosting, and human-in-the-loop labor—resulting in high gross margins.
  • Software-Like Margins on Service TAMs: While traditional services firms top out at ~30% margins, AI-native services aim for 50%+ gross margins. This combines the high profitability of software with a Addressable Market (TAM) that is two to three times larger than SaaS.
  • The "Build vs. Buy" Fallacy: Founders are often tempted to acquire an existing legacy services business and layer AI on top of it to accelerate revenue. This is a trap; legacy cultures, processes, and metrics resist modernization. Building from scratch is almost always superior, unless an acquisition is strictly necessary to fast-track a regulatory license.

Under the leadership of CEO Dara Khosrowshahi, Uber has transitioned from a chaotic startup into a highly profitable powerhouse generating over $10 billion in free cash flow. By operating as a supply-led marketplace, leveraging its physical-world scale, and establishing over 30 autonomous vehicle (AV) partnerships, Uber is positioning itself to capture a nascent trillion-dollar physical AI and transport ecosystem.

***

Transitioning Uber from Chaos to Order

  • The "How" of Resolving Corporate Chaos: Khosrowshahi stabilized Uber post-2017 by breaking down massive, systemic crises into distinct, manageable dimensions.
      • Board Alignment: Partnered with new Chairman Ron Sugar to shift the board's focus away from factional control and toward the long-term survival of the firm.
      • Cultural & Executive Turnaround: Rebuilt the executive team, retaining critical legacy talent like Andrew Macdonald (President of Mobility/COO) while hiring key new leaders like Chief Legal Officer Tony West.

      • Work-Life Segregation: Maintains strict personal boundaries by remaining "all in" on family time without device distractions, separating personal self-worth from corporate fortune.

The Core Marketplace Engine: A Supply-Led Strategy

  • The Supply-Led Thesis: Unlike demand-first platforms (such as Expedia), Uber’s growth is fundamentally supply-led.
      • Geographic Expansion: To launch in secondary and tertiary markets (the "next 50 to 200 cities" in a country), Uber first recruits drivers, couriers, and merchants. Once supply is established, consumer demand follows naturally.
      • Scale Metrics: The global platform now supports over 10 million drivers and couriers.
  • Empathy in Supply Aggregation: To improve retention and platform quality, leaders must experience the supplier's pain points directly.
      • Firsthand Experience: Khosrowshahi delivered food via e-bike and drove passengers in a Tesla in San Francisco to understand the UX.
      • Addressing the "P95" Friction: While a typical consumer interacts with the app for 30 seconds, a driver is on the platform for 6–10 hours a day. Consequently, minor bugs (P95 errors) that occur weekly for drivers require far higher product engineering prioritization than consumer-facing bugs.

The Trillion-Dollar AV and Physical AI Frontier

  • Bridging the Digital and Physical Worlds: Uber operates in a probabilistic real-world environment characterized by unpredictable events (traffic, cancellations, late food).
      • Model Scaling: Uber’s search and feed machine learning models are now 10,000 times larger than legacy models, enabling the app to accurately predict a passenger's destination 75% of the time upon opening.
  • The Turnkey AV Ecosystem: Uber has established over 30 partnerships across the AV space, including Waymo, Nuro, Lucid, Nvidia, Waabi, and Wayve.
      • The Value Proposition: Instead of building proprietary hardware, Uber acts as the commercialization partner for AV developers. Uber provides depot management, charging infrastructure, autonomous insurance, and fleet financing (including a $1 billion vehicle financing deal with Santander).
      • The 30% Utilization Lift: AVs operating on the Uber network experience a 30%+ increase in utilization (trips and revenue per vehicle per day) compared to proprietary, single-party AV networks.

TL;DR

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent defended the administration’s economic performance, highlighting a manufacturing renaissance and growth under the Working Families Tax Cut, while facing intense questioning from Democrats over a controversial IRS settlement shielding Donald Trump from tax audits and the ongoing rise of the national debt.

Economic Growth, Cost of Living, and Tax Policy

  • Strong Economic Indicators: Secretary Bessent asserted that the underlying US economy is highly resilient, noting 2.6% to 2.8% GDP growth—outperforming the Congressional Budget Office's (CBO) 1.8% projection. He described current high prices as a "temporary blip."
  • Grocery Inflation Dispute: Bessent argued that grocery prices ("food at home") have risen by only 2.5% since President Trump took office, which he stated is half of the annual average seen under the Biden administration. Democratic senators sharply contested this, pointing out the cumulative toll of high gas, grocery, and utility costs on working families.
  • Impact of the Working Families Tax Cut Act: Bessent championed the legislation passed on July 4th as a driver of a domestic "manufacturing renaissance":
      • Senior and Child Tax Relief: The law made permanent a $2,200 child tax deduction and a $6,000 enhanced senior deduction. The senior deduction was claimed by 35 million Americans, 95% of whom earn under $200,000.
      • Immediate Expensing: Bessent credited the immediate write-offs for R&D, factories, and equipment with boosting US productive capacity, citing manufacturing expansions by Boeing, John Deere, and Winnebago.

The Trump IRS Settlement and Audit Immunity Controversy

  • Audit Immunity Allegations: Democratic Senators (including Wyden, Whitehouse, Cortez Masto, and Lujan) heavily criticized a settlement arising from President Trump’s $10 billion lawsuit against the government. They alleged that an addendum to the agreement grants unprecedented, permanent immunity from IRS audits to Trump, his family, and his businesses.
  • The "Slush Fund": Senator Lujan criticized the creation of a reported $1.776 billion settlement fund, characterizing it as a lawless giveaway.
  • Dodgeball over Ongoing Litigation: Citing ongoing litigation, Bessent repeatedly refused to answer questions regarding whether he or his staff authorized the settlement or if any reciprocal consideration was paid to the government. He instructed senators to direct their questions to Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche.
  • Taxpayer Privacy and Contractor Leaks: Bessent defended the Treasury's defensive posture by highlighting that the tax privacy of Trump and 400,000 other taxpayers (including many low-income Trump Organization employees) was violated by former IRS contractor Charles Littlejohn.
      • Contractor Cancellations: Bessent confirmed that Treasury canceled all active Booz Allen contracts in response to the leaks. Senator Wyden pointed out that the cancellations occurred exactly three days before Trump filed his lawsuit, suggesting coordination.

National Debt, Social Security, and Fraud Prevention

  • The Looming Debt Crisis: Senator Welch highlighted that the national debt has surged from $36.2 trillion in January 2025 to $39.2 trillion. The US now pays $970 billion annually in net interest, consuming 18.5% of all federal revenues—an amount that exceeds the national defense budget.
  • Deficit Targets: Bessent reiterated his target of reducing the deficit-to-GDP ratio to 3% by the end of Trump’s term (down from a peak of 6.9% under the previous administration to 5.4% currently) through economic growth and spending constraints.
  • Social Security Insolvency: With Social Security projected to go insolvent in six years, Senator Cassidy pressed for the administration's plan.
      • Crawl, Walk, Run Strategy: Bessent stated that the administration must stabilize short-term deficits before enacting structural Social Security reform.   • Federal Budget Fraud: Bessent estimated that annual fraud in the federal budget is "at least $500 billion" (representing up to 13.3% of the budget). International Sanctions, Trade, and Geopolitics

  • Operation Economic Fury (Iran): Bessent detailed the administration's maximum-pressure campaign against Iran. Enhanced banking transparency in the Gulf has allowed the US to freeze Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) funds, seize substantial crypto assets, and assist the US Navy in interdicting oil-smuggling vessels. He reported that the Iranian economy is in collapse, with inflation exceeding 200%.
  • De-Risking Trade with China: Bessent advocated for "de-risking rather than decoupling" the US-China economic relationship.
      • Tariff-Free Trade Baskets: Treasury is exploring a bilateral framework to allow up to $30 billion in tariff-free trade of non-critical items on both sides (e.g., US importing fireworks and toys; China purchasing US seafood and energy).
      • Aviation Purchases: China has committed to purchasing 200 Boeing aircraft, with further trade negotiations scheduled for President Xi Jinping's upcoming Washington visit.
  • Countering Chinese Money Laundering: Bessent highlighted the efforts of the Counter-Illicit Finance Task Force (established April 2025) to block Chinese money laundering networks linked to illegal US marijuana grow operations, human trafficking, and agricultural land purchases near sensitive military sites.
