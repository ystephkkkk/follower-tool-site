TL;DR

AI is a monumental platform shift equivalent to—but only equivalent to—the internet or mobile revolutions. Because we are currently in its chaotic "1997" era, long-term value will not accrue to commoditized foundational models but to the specialized application layer, custom enterprise integrations, and distribution moats.

The "1997" Timeline of AI and the Illusion of AGI

  • The 1997 Analogy: Tech analyst Benedict Evans argues that we are currently in the "1997" equivalent of the AI lifecycle.
      • Immaturity of Tech: Most tools do not fully work yet, enterprise sales cycles are long, and predicting the dominant players today is as futile as crowning Excite or Yahoo the winners of the early web.
      • Adoption Gaps: While tech insiders use LLMs hourly, mainstream adoption remains shallow; data shows only 15–20% of teens use AI daily, while 60% do not use it at all.
  • Redefining AI and AGI: Evans notes that "AI" is a moving target that refers only to technology that has not been normalized yet.
      • The Larry Tesler Quote: "AI is whatever machines can't do yet." Once a system successfully performs a task, people dismiss it as "just software" (e.g., databases, OCR, and sentiment analysis).
      • Goalpost Shifting: The definition of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) is being actively watered down by model labs to mean "economically valuable work," a benchmark that mainframes in the 1970s already partially achieved.

Deconstructing the "Job Apocalypse" and the Task vs. Job Fallacy

  • The Jevons Paradox of Labor: Throughout history, automation has not led to permanent mass unemployment.
      • Price Elasticity: Making a task cheaper (e.g., accounting via spreadsheets, writing code via IDEs) dramatically increases total demand rather than reducing headcount. Despite centuries of automation, employment and GDP continue to rise.
      • Unknown Roles: Historical shifts show that you can easily see the job that will be automated away (e.g., typesetters, elevator operators), but you cannot see the new jobs because they do not exist yet.
  • The "Boring Manual Labor" of Coding: Software engineering is currently the most transformed industry because LLMs revealed that massive portions of coding are actually repetitive, manual tasks.
      • Shift in Focus: Just as 90% of modern iOS code is pre-written by Apple drivers, AI allows developers to build complex applications faster, shifting their primary role from writing code lines to software architecture and product-market strategy.
  • The Error of Expert Systems Metrics: Evans dismisses government and consulting datasets (like O*NET) that try to predict exactly what percentage of a profession (e.g., "17% of a lawyer's work") can be automated.
      • Complex Realities: Human professions are organic webs of politics, customer relationships, and physical realities that cannot be cleanly broken down into logical steps.

The Commoditization of Foundational Models and Value Capture

  • No Network Effects for Models: Foundational model labs (such as OpenAI and Anthropic) currently lack strong, winner-take-all network effects.
      • Low-Margin Utilities: Without structural differentiation, these labs risk ending up like global telecom carriers or cloud hosting providers—selling a highly sophisticated, capital-intensive commodity at marginal cost with zero pricing power.
  • The Rise of "Forward-Deployed" Engineering: Foundational model labs are aggressively buying consultancies and hiring professional services teams.
      • Enterprise Bottleneck: Modern enterprises do not have idle, highly skilled employees sitting around to completely redesign internal workflows. Integrating AI requires heavy manual consulting lifting from firms like Bain, McKinsey, or Accenture.
  • The Power of Distribution: As software becomes easier and cheaper to build, noise in the market escalates, making distribution the ultimate moat.
      • Incumbent Advantages: Companies with existing distribution networks (like Google with Android/Workspace or Meta across social feeds) can easily deploy "adequate" commoditized AI to millions of users, winning through convenience and default settings.

Navigating the Anti-AI Backlash and Cultural Anxiety

  • Exaggerated Resource Panic: Evans notes that many public concerns about AI are a "fuzzy mess of truth and fiction."
      • The Water cooling Myth: While data center expansion is a real localized planning and electricity challenge, its overall environmental impact is often overstated. A Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory study estimated US data center water consumption at just 0.017% of total US water use.
  • Real and Decentralized Harms: The real societal threat of AI is not an existential machine takeover (the "Doomer" narrative), but localized, decentralized malice.
      • The Photoshop Parallel: Photoshop has always allowed people to falsify images, but AI lowers the technical barrier, allowing anyone to generate complex deepfakes in minutes, causing immediate societal harm.
  • The Only Viable Professional Strategy: Evans warns against adopting an attitude of moral superiority or avoidance regarding AI.
      • Involvement is Mandatory: The only way to survive the transition is to dive deep into the technology to understand exactly what it can do and how it can augment your unique skillset.
