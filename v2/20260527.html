TL;DR

Seeed Studio is democratizing embodied AI by delivering affordable, open-source robotic hardware powered by NVIDIA Jetson, enabling developers to control and train physical agents using intuitive hand-guiding and natural-language commands.

The Power of Open Source in Embodied AI

  • Lowering the Barrier to Entry: Seeed Studio aims to make physical AI accessible to everyone. By utilizing open source modules, customizable developer kits, and extensive tutorials, they allow hobbyists, researchers, and small businesses to quickly adopt advanced robotics.
  • Fostering a Collaborative Ecosystem:
      • Rapid Evolution: Open-source development operates like an ecosystem where diverse ideas compete and evolve quickly, rather than relying on a single closed-source humanoid design.
      • Establishing Trust: Open source ensures that users deploying physical AI in sensitive environments—such as homes, retail spaces, or factories—maintain complete control and customization of their devices without vendor lock-in.
  • Empowering Small and Medium Businesses (SMBs): Historically restricted by the high cost and complexity of industrial robotics, SMBs and startup innovation labs are using Seeed’s platforms to deploy specialized, domain-specific robots efficiently.

Democratizing Hardware: From the $200 SOR-ARM to the Re-Bot

  • The $200 SOR-ARM: Developed in partnership with Hugging Face, this highly affordable robotic arm lowers the barrier to entry for schools, makers, and small businesses, shifting robots from expensive industrial investments to accessible tools for everyday tasks.
  • The Re-Bot Arm:
      • Affordable and Robust: Starting at under $1,000, the Re-Bot is designed for practical, real-world deployment. It features a robust design with 3D-printable parts, allowing users to customize physical components to fit their specific environments.
      • Sim-to-Real Integration: The Re-Bot is fully mirrored in NVIDIA Isaac Sim. This digital twin capability allows developers to simulate and validate trajectories in a virtual environment before deploying them to physical hardware.

Intuitive Training and Agentic Frameworks on NVIDIA Jetson

  • "Dog-Like" Training Methods: Instead of writing complex spatial planning code over several months, users train the robot organically by physically holding the arm and guiding it through a task.
      • On-Device Execution: The guided training data is sent to the cloud for training, and the resulting diffusion model is deployed locally on NVIDIA Jetson devices, utilizing camera feeds to dynamically execute and adjust movements.
  • Natural-Language Control via OpenClaw (Open-COLA): By running OpenClaw locally on NVIDIA Jetson Orin alongside a Qwen 1.5 35B model, developers can bypass traditional programming entirely. Users can send text commands (e.g., "move up 20 centimeters" or "pick up the claw") to control the physical arm.
  • Hugging Face LeRobot Integration: Seeed’s hardware supports Hugging Face’s LeRobot framework, providing end-to-end learning policies, datasets, and models that allow developers of any technical background to train and operate physical agents.

Modular Hardware Design vs. Monolithic Humanoids

  • The Modular Approach: Rather than building a single general-purpose humanoid robot that tries to do everything, Seeed Studio focuses on modular sub-assemblies.
      • Custom Assemblies: By manufacturing separate heads, torsos, arms, chassis, and wheels, Seeed allows developers to combine parts organically to suit their exact physical requirements.
  • Rapid Scale and Manufacturing Services: Beyond selling off-the-shelf kits, Seeed acts as an ODM partner to help hardware startups scale quickly.
      • Rapid Prototyping: As a demonstration of their design-to-manufacturing speed, Seeed co-developed, manufactured, and shipped 3,000 units of the Hugging Face "Re-Chair Mini" in just five months.

The Unified Data Foundation and the Jevons Paradox

  • The Single Postgres Advantage: YC's greatest structural advantage when adopting AI was keeping all operations—founder records, company tables, CRM logs, and financial transactions—on a single, proprietary Postgres database rather than fragmenting data across third-party SaaS tools.
  • Unrestricted Database Access: A major breakthrough occurred when YC partner Jared bypassed restricted, narrow API tools and gave their agent loop direct, read-only SQL access to the entire production database.
  • The Jevons Paradox of Data: Dramatically lowering the friction to query database schemas did not simply save time; it exponentially increased the volume and complexity of inquiries. Users went from spending hours manually writing SQL for BI tools to instantly querying highly specific trends, such as identifying all investors who backed space-tech companies in the last four YC batches.
  • Denormalizing for Agents (G-Brain): For legacy organizations with fragmented data silos, the solution is "denormalization." Re-architecting data into a single, comprehensive table layout optimized specifically for agent retrieval (RAG, graph-RAG, and re-ranking) is necessary to give agents the holistic context they need to make intelligent, localized decisions.

The Tool Registry and "Just-in-Time" Software

  • A Shared Tool Registry: To turn raw LLMs into productive employees, YC built an internal tool registry that has grown from 20 basic functions to over 350 today. This registry allows agents to perform specialized business tasks like booking journal entries, managing event logistics, and scheduling office hours.
  • The Multiplayer Agent Era: While popular developer platforms (e.g., Claude Code, Cursor) operate in a "single-player" environment on individual local machines, the next frontier is building "multiplayer" organizational systems that share tools, context, and capabilities.
  • Skillify and DRY/MECE Resolvers:
    • Skillify: YC uses meta-skills to automatically turn successful prompt workflows into reusable tools (or "skills") on the fly.
    • DRY & MECE: To prevent tool registries from bloating, YC applies software engineering principles like *DRY* (Don't Repeat Yourself) alongside the management consulting framework *MECE* (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive). This keeps the agent's core capabilities streamlined and prevents redundant skill paths.
  • The Rise of Just-in-Time Software: Software architecture is shifting from deterministic code that wraps around an AI feature to a highly flexible model where a small AI agent wraps around deterministic tools. Instead of writing hundreds of thousands of rigid lines of code, developers can write tiny, dynamic TypeScript or Markdown parameters, allowing agents to generate custom, just-in-time code blocks on demand.

Continuous Optimization and the Self-Improving "Dream Cycle"

  • The Nightly Dream Cycle: Instead of requiring manual engineering updates, YC's infrastructure runs an autonomous loop every night. This "dream cycle" scans all agent-to-employee conversation logs from the day, flags instances where the agent failed or lacked necessary context, and autonomously updates and refines the agent's prompt structures.
  • The Two-Sentence Pitch Optimization: Partner Tom designed a skill to condense complex company ideas into clear, "two-sentence descriptions." To refine it:
    • Partners recorded and transcribed group office hours where founders brainstormed pitches.
    • The raw meeting transcript was fed directly back into the agent loop.
    • The agent analyzed the real-time feedback loop and automatically improved the core prompt logic, rendering the resulting "pitch tool" more effective at writing descriptions than the individual partners themselves.

Cultivating an AI-Native Culture

  • Transparency-by-Default: YC broadcasts all internal agent conversations to a public Slack channel open to all full-time employees. This extreme transparency has two massive benefits:
    • Egalitarian Learning: Employees quickly learn how to maximize the AI's utility by watching their colleagues' creative prompting styles.
    • Social Security Controls: High-trust environments can utilize public peer accountability to monitor data safety, bypassing rigid, slow security locks that usually paralyze corporate AI utility.
  • The Personal Computer Moment: Pete Koomen compares the current state of AI to the 1970s Homebrew Computer Club. Companies face a critical choice: build centralized, locked-down systems that limit user agency (analogous to 1960s corporate mainframes), or build open, customizable, decentralized agent environments that empower the end-user (the PC revolution).
  • Accelerated Onboarding and Apprenticeship: Rather than waiting six months to fully ramp up, new hires can use internal agents to rapidly query company history and run simulations of senior partners' decision-making processes, effectively democratizing institutional knowledge through automated mentorship.

      • Financial Integration: The company is deploying cross-platform micro-credit and frictionless payment services across its 20 Indian portfolio companies to build a unified commerce model.
  • Europe (The Tech-First Pivot): Prosus recently acquired Just Eat with the explicit goal of transforming it from a traditional food-delivery business into an AI-first technology company, supported by a newly established AI lab in Amsterdam.

## The "Ambidextrous" Management Model and "Jet Skis"

Bloisi attributes his success to balancing institutional scale with extreme, decentralized entrepreneurial speed:

  • The "Jet Ski" Innovation Model: To prevent the bureaucratic stagnation of typical corporate budgeting, Prosus launches "Jet Skis"—autonomous, 5-to-10-person teams empowered to operate like independent startups.
      • Iterative Failure: These small teams are given the mandate to fail fast and test dozens of iterations without waiting for massive budgets or annual planning cycles. Once a "Jet Ski" finds a successful path, Prosus deploys its massive corporate scale to institutionalize it.
  • Ambidextrous Organizations: Bloisi stresses that a successful tech giant must simultaneously respect financial discipline and operational scale (the "boring" management) while fiercely protecting and funding disruptive innovators.
  • Radical Talent Philosophy: Bloisi actively seeks out entrepreneurs who thrive under extreme uncertainty and big goals. He practices radical empowerment but pairs it with a clear-cut willingness to part ways with underperforming staff.

## The Under-Hyped AI Revolution and "Agentic" Operations

Bloisi strongly rejects the notion that AI is over-hyped, arguing that the technological leaps achieved between late 2023 and mid-2024 are profoundly misunderstood by non-technical executives.

  • Autonomous Organizations: AI is moving past simple individual productivity enhancements (such as drafting emails) toward fully autonomous workflows where custom-built agents run corporate operations for days at a time.
  • AI-Driven Efficiencies at iFood: iFood’s core operations—including landing page personalization, customer push notifications, anti-fraud measures, and CRM—are entirely automated by AI, resulting in higher customer satisfaction (NPS) and reduced marketing spend.
  • "Tukan" Platform and Executive Coding: Prosus built "Tukan," an internal platform allowing non-technical employees to build custom AI agents. Bloisi mandated that all portfolio CEOs take software development courses to learn how to automate their own workflows, moving the company from monthly to weekly operating cycles.
  • The Agentic Lifestyle Ecosystem: Prosus’s primary R&D focus is building "life assistants"—autonomous agents that do not just text with users but securely execute real-world financial transactions, such as booking flights, ordering food, and hailing rides on behalf of the consumer.

## Geopolitical and Regulatory Friction in Europe

Despite his optimism, Bloisi expressed deep frustration with Europe’s regulatory framework, which he argues actively stifles the continent's tech sector:

  • Forced Divestments: European regulators forced Prosus to sell its stake in Delivery Hero, despite Bloisi’s expressed willingness to invest $10 billion into building a European tech champion.
  • Disadvantage to Domestic Players: Bloisi notes that while the US, Chinese, and Indian governments actively foster and protect their domestic tech champions, European M&A regulations hinder local consolidation. This creates a vacuum that allows American and Asian tech giants to dominate European markets.
