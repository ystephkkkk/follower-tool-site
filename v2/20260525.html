TL;DR

Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang affirmed the company's commitment to competing in China despite a vacuum created by US export controls that fueled Huawei’s rapid growth, while regional dynamics shift with key state visits, energy market fluctuations, and China’s historic Shenzhou-23 space mission.

AI & Semiconductors: Nvidia’s Strategy and China's Domestic Push

  • Nvidia's China Commitment: CEO Jensen Huang clarified that Nvidia has no plans to step back from the Chinese market, despite US export restrictions severely limiting the company's growth.
      • Market Vacuum: Huang conceded that US bans on advanced AI chips left a market vacuum that Chinese companies like Huawei and local startups rapidly filled, leading to record-growth years for these domestic firms.
      • The "Five-Layer Cake" of AI: Huang described AI as a "five-layer cake," noting that Nvidia only competes in one layer. By continuing to participate in the Chinese market, Nvidia hopes to support the growth of the other four layers of the local ecosystem.
  • Huawei’s Lithography Workaround: Huawei announced a timeline to produce next-generation 1.4-nanometer chips by 2031, aiming to trail Taiwan's TSMC by only three years.
      • Logic Folding Technology: To bypass US sanctions that block access to advanced lithography tools, Huawei is using a design process called "logic folding." This approach makes chips faster by shortening internal component communication times rather than relying solely on shrinking transistor sizes.

Regional Geopolitics: Strategic Alliances in East Asia

  • China-Pakistan Diplomacy: Chinese President Xi Jinping welcomed Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif in Beijing, highlighting their "unshakable 75-year friendship" and thanking Pakistan for its role in mediating US-Iran tensions in the Middle East.
      • Economic and Corporate Ties: Sharif’s four-day visit concluded with the signing of $1.2 billion in corporate agreements in Hangzhou, involving major Chinese firms such as Alibaba and CATL.
  • Japan-Philippines Defense Alignment: Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. initiated a historic four-day state visit to Japan, meeting with Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi to upgrade bilateral relations to a "comprehensive strategic partnership."
      • Maritime Security Cooperation: Driven by mutual concerns over Chinese maritime assertiveness near Taiwan and the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands, the two nations began laying the groundwork for a General Security of Military Information Agreement (GSOMIA).
      • Defense Acquisitions: The Philippines is actively exploring the acquisition of second-hand Japanese Abukuma-class destroyers, alongside participating in the joint Balikatan 26 military exercises.

Global Finance & Energy: Oil Slumps, Japan's Budget, and Capital Crackdowns

  • Hormuz Breakthrough Optimism: Global crude prices fell by roughly 5%, with Brent sliding to $97 per barrel and WTI to $91.80, following reports of progress in US-Iran talks to reopen the critical Strait of Hormuz.
  • Japan's $19 Billion Cushion: Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi announced plans to submit a $19 billion supplementary budget next week to fund household utility and gasoline subsidies. The emergency funding will be financed through new deficit-financing bonds.
  • China’s Capital Flight Crackdown: Beijing launched an aggressive campaign targeting unauthorized offshore investment services, prohibiting overseas firms from opening accounts or trading on behalf of mainland Chinese clients.
      • Hong Kong Liquidity Hit: Analysts estimate the regulatory clampdown could impact up to $32 billion of assets in Hong Kong, directly threatening the city's IPO market.
  • Plunging Consumer Credit in China: In a stark sign of weak consumer spending, Chinese citizens discarded 9 million credit cards in Q1. This extends a 14-quarter contraction from the peak in September 2022, compounding rising default rates for mainland banks.

Space Exploration: China’s Shenzhou-23 Mission and Hong Kong’s Historic Milestone

  • Shenzhou-23 Launch: Three Chinese astronauts safely boarded the Tiangong space station after completing an automated, highly efficient three-hour docking sequence.
  • Hong Kong's First Astronaut: Payload specialist Lai Ka-ying, a former Hong Kong police officer with a PhD in forensic science, became the first Hong Kong citizen to join a Chinese space mission.
      • Scientific Role: Prof. Quentin Parker of the University of Hong Kong highlighted that Lai’s scientific and forensic background makes her uniquely suited to manage the complex array of 100+ microgravity experiments planned for the mission.
  • Year-Long Endurance Test: One of the three astronauts will be selected for a historic 12-month orbit. This extended mission is designed to study the physiological impacts of long-duration spaceflight as China prepares to land humans on the moon by 2030.

  • The Aerospace Fleet Duopoly: Global air traffic correlates strongly with GDP (historically growing at ~5%), meaning the global fleet of commercial aircraft doubles every 15 years.
  • Boeing's "Bean-Counter" Legacy: Following its 1997 merger with McDonnell Douglas, Boeing’s management prioritized cost-cutting. Rather than spending 5 years and billions of dollars developing a clean-sheet plane to counter Airbus, they opted to put larger engines on the legacy 737 frame, resulting in the flawed 737 Max software and subsequent crashes.
  • Diluted Free Cash Flow: Boeing was forced to issue massive amounts of equity to shore up its balance sheet during the crisis. While it was once projected to reach $40 in free cash flow per share, dilution has capped that potential to the low $20s. However, because there are no expensive new airplane development cycles on the horizon, Boeing remains a strong multi-year cash-generation story with a target price of $300 to $400.
