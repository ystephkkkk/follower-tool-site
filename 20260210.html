There is a specific feeling about the state of the world that many are trying to name right now. For almost thirty years, we operated under assumptions that felt like laws of nature: the unipolar moment of U.S.-style democratic capitalism and the Great Moderation of low inflation and market volatility. Geopolitics and economics were kept in separate lanes, as were national security and economic policy. Economic interdependence was seen as a shield against conflict, and the notorious "isms" of the 20th century—nationalism, authoritarianism, communism—seemed to have ended. Today, however, we see the return of great power rivalry, economic warfare, and industrial policy. With the resurgence of fiscal dominance over central banks and a race to secure supply chains, it is clear that we have returned to an old normal. This is not a move toward a brand-new future, but a reversion to a much older historical baseline defined by a contested, fragmented world where economics, geopolitics, and technology are part of the same conversation. Jake Sullivan helped rewire the connection between national security and economic policy, recognizing that supply chains are now front lines and a line of code is a diplomatic lever. Reflecting on this shift, it feels as though we have reached the end of the "end of history."
