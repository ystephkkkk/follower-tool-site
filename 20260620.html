TL;DR

SEC Commissioner Hester Peirce highlights the agency's efforts to modernize market structures through the proposed removal of the 20-year-old Rule 611 and the upcoming "innovation exemption," emphasizing that future regulations must pragmatically support on-chain finance while fiercely protecting investor privacy, self-custody, and developer freedom.

Reconsidering Legacy Market Structure (Rule 611)

  • Rule 611 Elimination: The SEC has proposed a rule to eliminate Rule 611 (the "trade-through rule"), a 20-year-old equity market regulation.
      • Historical Context: Originally established in 2005, the rule was met with a 44-page dissent by then-Commissioner (now Chairman) Paul Atkins and Commissioner Cynthia Glassman.
  • Obsolete Protections: Designed to protect the National Best Bid or Offer (NBBO) across fragmented markets, Peirce argues the rule is no longer necessary.
      • Market Mechanisms: Arbitrageurs naturally bind markets together and align prices across venues without regulatory intervention.
      • Technological Upgrades: Modern, highly interconnected exchange technology has resolved the fragmentation issues that existed when the rule was written.
      • Tokenization Enablement: Removing Rule 611 is a key structural step that will allow future tokenized, on-chain trading systems to function without being forced to fit antiquated execution requirements.

The Scope and Reality of the "Innovation Exemption"

  • Dispelling Myths: Peirce clarifies that the highly anticipated "innovation exemption" has not yet been officially released, contrary to media rumors.
  • Exclusion of Synthetics: The exemption will not apply to synthetic securities (the third tier in the SEC staff's tokenization taxonomy, where SPV wrappers offer economic exposure without direct asset custody).
  • On-Chain vs. Decentralized Finance: The framework targets "on-chain finance" rather than fully decentralized finance (DeFi).
      • Controlled Experimentation: The exemption will serve as a permissioned, real-world sandbox for firms to test tokenized assets, allowing regulators to observe and determine where proper points of regulation lie.
  • Value Proposition of Tokenization: Transferring securities to smart contracts on-chain unlocks significant efficiencies:
      • Collateral and Lending: Streamlines the process of lending out securities and posting collateral without relying on costly intermediaries.
      • Programmable Compliance: Enables automatic enforcement of restrictions, such as founder lock-up periods, directly within the asset's code.
      • Investor Communication: Allows companies to directly distribute communications or voting rights to wallets (e.g., via NFTs) without requiring personal investor identities.

SEC Priorities and Regulatory Harmonization

  • Commissioner Transition: Although her official term ended last June, Peirce can remain in her seat until the end of the year unless a replacement is confirmed. Her immediate post-SEC plan is to teach at a law school.
  • Immediate Policy Priorities: For her remaining tenure, Peirce is focusing on:
      • Preparing the agency to coordinate rule-writing under the proposed legislative Clarity Act.
      • Building regulatory frameworks for using tokens in capital fundraising.
      • Tackling "nuts-and-bolts" issues including crypto custody (for broker-dealers and advisers), transfer agent blockchain integrations, and defining the role of user interfaces under broker-dealer rules.
  • CFTC Harmonization: The SEC is actively collaborating with the CFTC to provide joint clarity on product classification and jurisdiction, particularly for complex retail products like perpetuals (perps).
      • Proactive Engagement: Peirce urges builders to bring concrete business models to the SEC’s crypto task force to identify required regulatory reliefs early.

Reinvigorating Public Markets and Capital Formation

  • Encouraging Earlier Public Listings: Peirce is advocating for reforms that encourage high-growth companies to go public sooner rather than remaining private through excessively late funding rounds (e.g., "Series M").
  • Reducing Burdensome Disclosures: To combat the negative associations of going public, Peirce recommends trimming boilerplate disclosure requirements that do not serve investor decision-making.
  • Mitigating Litigation Risks: The SEC is exploring ways to allow public companies and shareholders to voluntarily utilize arbitration rather than facing class-action shareholder litigation, without the regulator putting a "thumb on the scale."

Protecting Core Principles in the Digital Age

  • Non-Negotiable Regulatory Pillars: Peirce outlines three fundamental principles that future regulatory regimes must respect:
      • Self-Custody: Protecting the basic right of individuals to hold their own digital assets.
      • Developer Freedom: Ensuring software developers can write and publish open-source code without prior government permission.
      • Financial Privacy: Recognizing that the desire to protect financial privacy is natural and should be defended.
  • Credible Neutrality and Layering: To balance privacy and law enforcement, Peirce suggests keeping the base layer of blockchain technology credibly neutral.
      • Application-Level Compliance: Specialized features—such as freezing or reversing transactions—can be built as opt-in application layers on top of a neutral base layer, rather than compromising the underlying blockchain architecture.
