TL;DR: While LLMs are mathematically precise Bayesian inference engines, they are limited by their inability to learn continuously (plasticity) and their reliance on correlation over causation; true AGI requires a shift from pattern matching (Shannon entropy) to causal simulation (Kolmogorov complexity).

The LLM as a "Giant Matrix"

  • Mathematical Abstraction: Vishal Misra describes an LLM as a gargantuan, sparse matrix where every possible prompt is a row and the distribution over the vocabulary (e.g., 50,000 tokens) forms the columns.
      • Sparse Distribution: Because most token combinations are gibberish, the matrix is extremely sparse. LLMs act as a compressed representation of this "true" probability distribution.
  • Bayesian Updating: In-context learning (ICL) is the process of the model updating its "beliefs" in real-time.
      • Evidence-Based Inference: As a user provides examples in a prompt, the model shifts its posterior probability distribution.
      • Domain-Specific Example: Misra proved this by creating a cricket-specific language (DSL) that the model had never seen; by providing few-shot examples, the model’s probability of producing the correct "nonsense" tokens moved toward 100%.

The "Bayesian Wind Tunnel" Experiments

  • Proven Precision: To counter critics who argued "anything can be Bayesian," Misra’s team created a "Bayesian wind tunnel"—a controlled environment where models were given tasks with analytically calculable solutions.
      • Architectural Performance: Transformers matched the precise mathematical Bayesian posterior down to $10^{-3}$ bits of accuracy.
      • Hierarchy of Architectures: The research found that Transformers are the most capable Bayesian engines, followed by Mamba. LSTMs were only partially successful, and MLPs (Multi-Layer Perceptrons) failed the tasks entirely.
  • Geometric Signatures: By examining the gradients of frontier open-weight models, Misra found that the same mathematical geometries observed in small experimental models persist in massive production LLMs.

Fundamental Barriers to AGI

  • Lack of Plasticity: Unlike the human brain, which remains plastic throughout its life, LLM weights are frozen after training.
      • Memory Limitations: While LLMs "learn" during a conversation context, they "forget" everything once the context window is cleared. AGI requires "continual learning" without "catastrophic forgetting."
  • Correlation vs. Causation: Misra argues that deep learning is currently trapped in "Shannon entropy" (predicting the next token via association) rather than "Kolmogorov complexity" (finding the shortest causal program to explain data).
      • The Simulation Gap: Humans use causal models to simulate the world (e.g., dodging a thrown pen). Current LLMs only match patterns and lack this internal "simulator."
  • The Consciousness Debate: Misra rejects the idea of LLM consciousness, describing them as "grains of silicon doing matrix multiplication."
      • Objective Functions: Humans are driven by "survival and reproduction," whereas LLMs are driven solely by "next-token accuracy."

The "Einstein Test" for Intelligence

  • The Manifold Limitation: LLMs are excellent at navigating existing "manifolds" of data (the space of human knowledge). However, they cannot yet create *new* manifolds.
  • Theory of Relativity Challenge: Misra proposes a benchmark for AGI: train an LLM on physics data only from before 1911.
      • The Goal: If the model can independently derive the Theory of General Relativity—rejecting existing Newtonian axioms to create a new representation of space-time—it has reached AGI.
      • Current Failure: Current models treat anomalies as "noise" or outliers rather than using them to synthesize a more efficient, shorter "program" (Kolmogorov complexity) for the universe.

Kalshi is the first federally regulated prediction market in the US, achieving 11x volume growth in six months ($10.4B in February 2024) after successfully suing its own regulator, the CFTC, to permit election markets. By leveraging a decentralized network of "super-forecasters" rather than traditional institutions, Kalshi is evolving from a binary "yes/no" platform into a full-scale financial exchange for non-traditional commodities like GPU compute and weather events.

      • The Legal Basis: The court ruled that elections have clear economic impacts and do not fall under prohibited categories like war, terrorism, or assassination.
      • Outcome: The victory allowed Kalshi to list 2024 election markets, which became a primary driver for mainstream adoption and institutional credibility.

Hyper-Growth and Product Roadmap

  • The Four Pillars of Expansion: To become the "biggest derivatives exchange in the world," Mansour and Lopes Lara are focused on:

      • Market Structure: Transitioning from simple binary (Yes/No) contracts to complex futures, swaps, and options.
      • Margining Systems: Moving away from 100% upfront capital requirements to allow for more capital-efficient hedging on long-term events (e.g., hurricane insurance).

TL;DR

Cisco is transitioning to an "Agent Era" where AI acts as an autonomous co-worker rather than just a tool, with plans to have 60–70% of its entire product portfolio written by AI by the end of 2027. This shift demands a new focus on agent-specific security and a heterogeneous infrastructure strategy that revitalizes the role of the CPU alongside GPUs.

The Evolution of AI-Generated Engineering

  • Product Roadmap: Jeetu Patel revealed that Cisco’s "AI Defense" is their first product 100% written by AI.
      • By the end of 2026, Cisco expects to have a half-dozen products written entirely by AI.
      • By the end of 2027, the goal is for 60% to 70% of the entire portfolio to be AI-generated.
  • Redefining Engineering Roles: Writing code currently only accounts for 30–35% of an engineer's time. AI will handle the "plumbing," allowing humans to focus on higher-level architecture, problem-solving, and judgment.
  • The New Scrum Team: Patel predicts a fundamental shift in team structure. A traditional eight-person scrum team will evolve into a team of two humans managing six AI agents.

Infrastructure: The Renaissance of the CPU and the Edge

  • Heterogeneous Compute: While the initial AI wave focused almost exclusively on GPUs, the "Agent Era" requires a mix of GPUs, accelerators, and CPUs.
  • CPU Resurgence: CPUs are seeing a "renaissance" because they are superior for the orchestration required by complex AI agents. Patel noted that the market for high-performance CPUs is currently "on fire."
  • Token Generation at the Edge: Cisco is pushing for AI processing to move out of the data center and toward the edge.
      • Unified Edge: Cisco is integrating the Nvidia Spectrum 4500 chip into its unified edge platforms to allow for simple, plug-and-play AI deployment in branch offices that lack dedicated IT support.

Security and Trust in the Agent Era

  • Agent Security as an Industry: Patel identifies agent security as a massive emerging sector. Unlike traditional security, which was the primary concern of the CISO, agent security impacts every user directly.
  • The Trust Gap: Currently, many enterprises are running agents on isolated hardware (like Mac Minis) because they do not yet trust agents on their main production networks.
  • Dynamic Guardrails: As agents become more autonomous, security must move from static rules to dynamic guardrails that can prevent an agent from making catastrophic errors, such as accidentally deleting production databases.

Key Announcements at GTC 2026

  • AI Networking: Cisco announced support for Nvidia’s Spectrum 4 and Spectrum 6 (the 100-terabit chip), providing customers with more choice in high-speed networking fabrics.
  • HyperShield & BlueField: Cisco’s HyperShield (a hybrid mesh firewall) will now support Nvidia BlueField DPUs.
  • AI Defense Integration: Cisco is positioning its AI Defense product to secure the specific agent-based technologies being released by Nvidia.

TL;DR

William Hockey, founder of Column and co-founder of Plaid, argues that the modern venture capital model encourages "safe" entrepreneurship and consensus-driven thinking. By self-funding a nationally chartered bank and focusing on the "boring" infrastructure of the global US dollar, Hockey advocates for a high-risk, high-conviction model of specialist building that prioritizes long-term profitability and national security interests over the "VC hamster wheel."

Rejecting the "VC Hamster Wheel"

  • The Heroin of Venture Capital: Hockey describes VC funding as a "heroin-like" addiction where founders optimize for the next fundraise rather than long-term product viability. This leads to "windy" business paths where strategies shift to follow trends like AI or stablecoins just to secure capital.
  • Extreme Founder Risk:
      • Hockey funded Column by pledging over $1 billion in Plaid shares to secure a $70 million loan to buy a bank, nearly facing bankruptcy and margin calls three times in the process.
      • He argues that Silicon Valley has de-risked the founder role too much, attracting people who want the "founder" resume item without the "raw intensity" of potential failure.
  • Employee Equity and Liquidity:
      • Annual Tenders: Column uses 25% of its earnings every year to buy back shares from employees, providing predictable liquidity that traditional startups lack.
      • Anti-Dilution: Because the company avoids external funding rounds, employees face zero dilution, making a 1% stake in Column significantly more valuable than 1% in a heavily diluted VC-backed firm.

The Strategic Moat of "Boring" Problems

  • The Specialist Advantage: Value is found in deep, unglamorous niches that "Gemini deep research" cannot solve. Hockey emphasizes becoming the world’s leading expert in "confusing, boring-sounding" areas.
      • Extreme Research: Hockey studied 2,000-page histories of 19th-century Japanese and Chinese banking to find single product ideas that create millions in leverage.
  • Software Company + Bank: Column is a software company that owns its own regulatory rails.
      • Unlike traditional "BaaS" (Banking-as-a-Service) providers that sit on top of other banks, Column is the bank.
      • 90% of revenue comes from software fees (API calls), not the interest rate "spread" typical of traditional banking.
  • Customer Longevity: In financial services, customers like Ramp, Brex, and Wise pay for safety and longevity. Profitability is a feature that proves the company can be a "15-year partner" without relying on a VC’s investment committee.

Emerging Markets as Innovation Labs

  • The Silicon Valley Consensus Problem: Silicon Valley and Beijing are the two most consensus-oriented places in the world. Elites build software for other elites, losing touch with how "everyday America" or the rest of the world operates.
  • The "Kinshasa" Perspective:
      • Hockey frequently visits emerging markets like the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) to observe how founders build under extreme constraints.
      • Leapfrogging: Just as China skipped laptops for mobile, emerging markets are leapfrogging Western financial services. For example, Kaspi in Kazakhstan and Rawbank in the DRC offer integrated vertical apps (paying taxes, renewing licenses) that far exceed the utility of US banking apps.
  • The Dollar as a Global OS: The US dollar is most critical in "implicitly dollarized" emerging markets where local central banks are untrusted. These regions often need US financial infrastructure more than developed European markets do.

The US Dollar as National Security

  • Sanctions Before Missiles: Hockey views the US dollar as a primary national security asset. Controlling the global financial OS allows the US to enforce its will through sanctions—collapsing an enemy's trade ability—before ever putting "boots on the ground."
  • Infrastructure vs. Implementation:
      • Contrary to the narrative that US financial tech is "built on cobalt," Hockey asserts the Federal Reserve's underlying technology is actually quite robust and capable of 24/7 instant settlement.
      • The friction in the system (e.g., banks being closed on weekends) is a result of business model constraints (staffing, fraud protection for the vulnerable) rather than a lack of fundamental technology.

AI and the Future of Value Accrual

  • Distribution Over Technology: Value will not accrue to "AI companies" but to those with massive distribution and brand moats.
  • The Beneficiaries:
      • Inefficient legacy brands with high headcount costs are the biggest potential winners of AI, as they can use the technology to drastically cut operational costs.
      • Fraud Detection: AI’s greatest contribution to fintech will be matching human-level fraud detection instantly. This will allow banks to remove the "protection friction" that currently slows down money movement for everyone to protect a small percentage of vulnerable users.

TL;DR

Starcloud is migrating AI compute to space to bypass terrestrial energy and land constraints, leveraging plummeting launch costs and superior solar efficiency to make space-based data centers the primary infrastructure for AI inference within the next decade.

The Economic and Logistical Case for Space

  • Terrestrial Bottlenecks: Building 100MW+ energy projects on Earth currently faces 5-10 year lead times due to permitting, regulatory hurdles, and land scarcity.
  • Solar Efficiency: Space-based solar provides 8x the energy of terrestrial solar per square meter because it lacks atmospheric interference and operates in 24/7 sunlight, eliminating the need for expensive battery storage.
  • Cost Break-even: Philip Johnston estimates the breakeven point for space-based compute is a launch cost of $500/kg. As terrestrial land and power costs rise, this threshold may shift closer to $1,000/kg.
  • Marginal Cost Dynamics: Unlike Earth, where the marginal cost of each additional data center increases due to grid strain, space-based infrastructure costs decrease at scale through mass manufacturing and reusable launch vehicles.

Technical Engineering and Maintenance

  • The Heat Dissipation Problem: Because a vacuum acts as an insulator, traditional convection cooling is impossible. Starcloud uses infrared radiation and "phase change" materials to manage thermals.
      • Advanced Radiators: Next-generation satellites (Starcloud 2) will feature large deployable radiators using liquid cooling loops and heat pumps to artificially boost radiator temperature for better dissipation.
  • Radiation Resilience: To combat bit-flips and hardware degradation, Starcloud conducts extensive testing at particle accelerators (e.g., Brookhaven National Lab) to simulate five years of radiation in 24 hours.
  • Hardware Longevity: The company targets a 5-6 year useful life, mirroring the replacement cycle of terrestrial chips.
      • Reliability Data: The H100 GPU currently in orbit has reported zero restart failures, suggesting that GPU workloads (which are stochastic) are naturally resilient to minor space-induced errors.

Scaling with SpaceX Starship

  • Launch Capacity: SpaceX’s Starship is the primary catalyst. Johnston anticipates Starship will eventually launch 10MW of compute capacity per flight using a "Pez dispenser" deployment mechanism.
  • Deployment Velocity: Once Starship reaches a high flight cadence, Starcloud expects to deploy several gigawatts of new capacity monthly.
  • Inference vs. Training: Starcloud focuses on AI inference rather than frontier model training. Inference is less hindered by the speed-of-light latency (sub-50ms via Starlink) and represents the vast majority of future AI workloads.
  • Business Model: Starcloud aims to be the "Equinix of Space," providing the "box" (power, cooling, and connectivity) while allowing hyperscalers or customers to provide their own chip architectures.

Strategic Outlook and Market Predictions

  • CapEx Shift: Johnston predicts that within 10 years, close to $1 trillion in annual CapEx will flow toward space-based compute infrastructure.
  • The "Space Force" Factor: Security is managed via orbital velocity (moving at 27,000 km/h makes satellites harder to hit than stationary Earth targets) and protection from the U.S. Space Force.
  • Initial Customers: Early adoption is driven by military and government sectors needing to process high-bandwidth data (like Synthetic Aperture Radar) in-situ to bypass data downlink bottlenecks.
  • Long-term Vision: Johnston envisions a future where 99.9% of GDP is tied to inference compute, potentially moving toward a "Dyson Sphere" level of energy and compute utilization.

President Trump is pushing for a "coalition of the willing" to secure the Strait of Hormuz amid escalating Iran tensions, while the financial sector prepares for the "Super Bowl" of regulation with the formal Basel III Endgame proposal this Thursday.

Geopolitics and the Iran Conflict

  • Strait of Hormuz Escorts: President Trump has called for an international "coalition of the willing" (including China, the UK, Japan, South Korea, and Australia) to provide naval escorts for tankers.
      • International Response: Current feedback is muted; Japan, Australia, and Germany have indicated they have no immediate plans or have not received formal requests.
  • US-China Relations: Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt suggested President Trump may delay his planned summit with President Xi Jinping, stating the President will not leave U.S. soil "at times of war."
      • Trade Negotiations: U.S. and Chinese officials are currently in Paris negotiating tariffs, with outcomes expected shortly.
  • Defense Supplemental Funding: The Pentagon is reportedly seeking ~$50 billion in an Iranian supplemental defense bill.
      • Legislative "Christmas Tree": Congressional committees are already looking to attach riders, including $15 billion in farm aid and $20 billion for disaster insurance. The final package could reach $100–$200 billion, though floor action is unlikely before summer.

Banking and Financial Regulation

  • Basel III Endgame: The formal proposal is expected this Thursday, kicking off a 90-day comment period.
      • Capital Requirements: Requirements for operational and market risk are expected to increase, while credit risk for mortgages may decrease to incentivize bank originations.
  • G-SIB Surcharge: A "Method 2" tweak to the G-SIB surcharge is anticipated, which could provide approximately $32 billion in capital relief for the six largest "Global Systemically Important Banks" (JPMorgan, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and Citigroup).
  • Prediction Markets: The CFTC issued guidance classifying most prediction markets as "swaps" under the Commodities Exchange Act.
      • Rulemaking Timeline: A formal rule is not expected to be finalized until mid-2027, making it difficult for future administrations to quickly reverse the stance via simple guidance.

Digital Assets and Crypto Policy

  • Stablecoin Regulation: The Senate Banking Committee is expected to intensify work on a "clarity draft" regarding stablecoin yields in April.
  • OCC "Genius Act" Implementation: The Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC) has proposed rules requiring stablecoin issuers to hold 100% high-quality liquid assets.
      • Yield Ban Controversy: The OCC proposal includes a provision that would prohibit "handshake agreements" between issuers and third-party platforms offering reward programs, a move heavily opposed by the crypto industry.
  • CBDC Ban: The Senate-passed housing bill includes a ban on a Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) through 2030, though House Republicans are pushing for a permanent ban.

Congressional and Administrative Outlook

  • Housing Legislation: The "21st Century for Road Housing Act" has moved to the House after passing the Senate.
      • Institutional Investors: A contentious provision would require single-family REITs to dispose of rental properties within seven years, which critics argue will stifle new construction.
  • The SAFE Act: President Trump has signaled he will not sign any legislation until this voter identification bill passes.
      • Filibuster Standoff: Senator John Thune (R-SD) lacks the 60 votes required for passage, but is expected to move through procedural motions to demonstrate effort to the White House.
  • Fed Chair Nomination: Senator Tom Tillis continues to block Kevin Warsh’s nomination for Federal Reserve Chair.
      • Interim Leadership: If the nomination remains stalled by May, Governor Miron may step in as acting Chair.
