TL;DR

To support the massive surge in AI data center demand—projected to drive global data center energy consumption past 1,000 TWh by 2030—the industry must transition from component-level sourcing to co-designed, factory-integrated systems while proactively navigating lagging regulatory and technical standards.

The Exponential Demand Curve

  • Unprecedented Power Trajectory: Global data center power consumption is rising rapidly, driven primarily by AI workloads.
    • Historically, consumption grew moderately from 200 terawatt hours (TWh) in 2010 to 240 TWh in 2020 (a 25% increase).
    • Demand is projected to spike to 600 TWh by 2025 and exceed 1,000 TWh by 2030.
    • The AI Share: The AI-specific portion of power demand is projected to jump from 90 TWh in 2025 to over 400 TWh by 2030—a four- to five-fold increase in just five years.
  • Massive Capital Expenditure: Annual global spend on AI data center infrastructure is projected to reach $1 trillion by 2029.
  • Grid and Supply Bottlenecks: Power availability on utility grids remains a critical constraint.
    • Operators are increasingly moving toward on-site power generation.
    • Small nuclear reactors (SMRs) are anticipated to be integrated into data center power strategies by 2030.

The Three Axes of Scaling

  • Scaling Up (Density and Power): Rack-level power consumption is experiencing exponential growth, requiring radical cooling and power delivery modifications.
    • Three years ago, a standard AI rack consumed 10 to 20 kilowatts (kW).
    • Today's systems (such as the NVIDIA NVL72) consume 150 to 200 kW per rack.
    • Next-generation platforms are targeting up to 1 megawatt (MW) per rack, representing a 50x density increase over a five-year period.
  • Scaling Out (Interconnection): High density requires re-architecting physical systems around the compute cluster.
    • Operators must redesign busway topologies, power conversion architectures, and cooling loops to minimize latency and maintain coherent, large-scale compute clusters.
  • Replicating Globally (Deployment Speed): Building rates that once took years are being compressed into months.
    • This rapid globalization has dramatically increased supply chain complexity, creating a surge in unique part numbers (SKUs) and supplier components.

Critical Technical & Physical Transitions

  • Transition to Higher Voltage DC: Physics dictates a move away from traditional 48-volt racks.
    • At 150 kW+ per rack, low-voltage currents require impractical amounts of copper.
    • The industry is moving to 400-volt and 800-volt DC systems, with 1,500-volt DC on the horizon. This reduces copper usage and allows operators to move power conversion systems outside the IT rack to free up compute space.
  • Mandatory Liquid Cooling: Air cooling is hitting its physical limits.
    • Air cooling is only effective up to 50–100 kW per rack; beyond this, liquid cooling is mandatory.
    • Open Compute Project (OCP) flow-rate guidelines have increased from 1 liter per minute per kilowatt (L/min/kW) to 1.5–2 L/min/kW in just two years to keep pace with thermal demands.
  • Shift to Factory Integration: Increased density has altered rack weights, floor-loading limits, and facility layouts.
    • Integration and commissioning of power, cooling, and controls must happen in the factory rather than in the field to prevent severe scheduling delays.

Hidden Risks: Market Fragmentation & Lagging Standards

  • Market Fragmentation: Multiple sectors are scaling rapidly but using highly customized, non-standardized architectures.
    • Total AI chip power is scaling from 2 gigawatts (GW) to 13 GW in just two years.
    • While hyperscalers comprise the majority of this growth (scaling from 1.5 GW to 8.5 GW), the "other" segments—including sovereign AI, neo-clouds, and enterprise—are growing at a faster rate, scaling 4.5x to 5x (from 0.5 GW to over 2 GW).
  • Lagging Standards and Certifications: Technical standards are not keeping pace with hardware innovation, creating immense regulatory uncertainty.
    • Voltage Standards: UL and IEC certification frameworks for 800V DC systems are still maturing and remain unharmonized.
    • PUE Inconsistencies: Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) is calculated differently across regions (e.g., EN in Europe vs. ASHRAE guidelines vs. internal hyperscaler specs), leading to expensive regional design re-work.
    • Cooling Specs: Rapidly shifting OCP specs mean cooling designs can become undersized or obsolete before certifications are finalized.

TL;DR

As generative AI shifts from training to complex agentic inference, SSDs are evolving from traditional storage devices into critical system-level memory extensions that offer the scalability and cost efficiency required to bypass high-bandwidth memory (HBM) and DRAM bottlenecks.

The Shift to AI Inference & Storage Bottlenecks

  • Rapid Storage Demand: The data center NAND market is projected to grow at a 34% CAGR from 2025 to 2031. However, AI inference workloads are growing much faster at a 56% CAGR, serving as the primary driver of storage demands.
  • The Rise of Agentic AI: Inference is transitioning from simple, single-prompt models to agentic AI, which requires multi-step reasoning, planning, and tool integration.
    • This evolution dramatically increases token usage, KV (Key-Value) cache storage, and the need for frequent, large-scale data access via Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG).
  • The Memory-Cost Dilemma: While HBM and DRAM deliver extreme speed, they are highly expensive and difficult to scale. SSDs offer the optimal balance of massive capacity, scalability, and cost efficiency needed to handle ballooning AI datasets.

Architectural Innovations: Context Memory & GPU Direct Storage (GDS)

  • Context Memory Storage: To support long-context inference, modern architectures are shifting to "warm KV reuse." Utilizing SSDs as a scalable memory layer allows systems to offload and share KV cache across GPUs, reducing redundant compute overhead and lowering latency.
  • GPU Direct Storage (GDS): Traditionally, data transfers from SSDs to GPUs must pass through the CPU, causing high latency. GDS bypasses the CPU entirely, streaming data directly between SSDs and GPUs.
    • This architecture enables SSDs to act as an extension of HBM, permitting GPUs to process massive datasets, such as vector databases, much more efficiently.

Kioxia's Specialized SSD Hardware Portfolio

To address diverse AI workloads, Kioxia has developed a three-pronged hardware strategy:

  • CM Series (Context Memory Storage): Combines enterprise-class performance with high capacity. It is optimized for storing, sharing, and reusing massive KV caches in long-context inference systems.
  • LC Series (Ultra-High Capacity): Supports up to 245 terabytes (TB) per drive. Designed to store massive AI datasets and enable high-performance tiering directly above nearline HDDs, optimizing total cost of ownership (TCO).
  • GP Series (GPU-Driven I/O Performance): Built to overcome HBM capacity constraints. It delivers low-latency data access and over 1 million IOPS per GPU, making it ideal for GDS and real-time inference pipelines.

Software Innovation & The NVIDIA-Kioxia Collaboration

Hardware alone cannot solve the AI data bottleneck; deep software integration across the ecosystem is required.

  • AiSAQ Software Library: Kioxia’s open-source, SSD-optimized library for large-scale approximate nearest neighbor vector search. By storing vector data directly on SSDs, AiSAQ removes the constraint of needing massive, expensive DRAM arrays.
  • NVIDIA Integration: AiSAQ integrates with NVIDIA cuVS (a GPU-accelerated vector search library) and the Milvus open-source vector database to accelerate index building.
    • Performance Benchmarks: In a single Milvus server test running 5 billion high-dimensional vectors, the joint solution achieved a 57-millisecond latency and 200 queries per second (QPS) at 90% recall.
    • Efficiency Improvements: Using GPU acceleration reduced the index build time from a full month to just 4 days (an 8x improvement), while demonstrating a 7x lower search media cost compared to DiskANN.
    • Future Roadmap: The partnership is actively working toward scaling systems to support 100 billion vectors, with the ultimate goal of handling trillion-vector workloads.

TL;DR

The global tech landscape is transitioning from a trillion-dollar internet economy built on search and recommendation engines to a new era of "Large Reasoning Machines" and Agentic AI. This evolution, exemplified by Google DeepMind's Project Astra, will replace traditional information retrieval with real-time, multimodal, universal AI assistants that reason like humans.

The Foundation: Search and Recommendation (1995–2015)

  • The Trillion-Dollar Paradigm: The modern internet economy—including Google, Apple's App Store, YouTube, and Instagram—is built on search and recommendation architectures.
    • This sector comprises $500 billion to $1 trillion of the global internet industry.
    • The Missed Software Opportunity: While the US aggressively commercialized these software breakthroughs, Taiwan and Southeast Asia largely missed out on this 20-year wave of software-driven growth.
  • Core Computational Pillars: Three foundational ideas drove this era:
    • Reverse Indexing: Pioneered at scale by Google using MapReduce (invented by Jeff Dean) to index the entire web offline.
    • Vector Space Models: Computing mathematical similarity vectors between users and content items to predict user preferences.
    • Neural Network Optimization: Utilizing early Tensor Processing Unit (TPU) liquid-cooled clusters to improve recommendation algorithms more than 1,000-fold.

The Cognitive Shift: Generative AI & Human Reasoning (2015–2025)

  • Sequential Transduction: The scientific breakthrough that defines "Generative AI."
    • Mimicking human biology, these networks convert sequential inputs (such as audio, pixels, or text) into sequential outputs token by token.
    • Multimodal Consolidation: Instead of utilizing separate models for different tasks, sequential transduction allows a single model to handle translation across 100 languages, summarization, and question-answering simultaneously.
  • Chain-of-Thought Reasoning: The transition from simple input-output pattern matching to active problem-solving.
    • By inserting step-by-step explanations in training data (similar to teaching a child), models improved their reasoning capabilities by 2x.
    • This advancement shifts the definition of AI from "Large Language Models" to "Large Reasoning Machines."

The Future: Agentic AI & Universal Assistants

  • Project Astra: Google DeepMind's research prototype for a universal, context-aware AI assistant running on Gemini 2.0.
    • Agentic Capabilities: Astra acts as a proactive agent that follows users through daily life via smartphones or prototype display glasses.
    • Real-World Multimodal Utilities: The assistant can recall past physical objects (e.g., retrieving a door code from a past email), interpret physical environments (e.g., explaining washing machine symbol settings through a camera feed), and offer localized, real-time context-specific recommendations.
  • On-Device vs. Cloud: The next major industry debate and developmental frontier will focus on whether these massive reasoning models should run locally on-device or remain cloud-based.

TL;DR

As trillion-parameter models and agentic workflows drive demand for high-speed "premium inference," traditional GPU-only architectures have hit a critical cost and performance bottleneck. SambaNova's heterogeneous disaggregated architecture resolves this by offloading memory-bound decoding to specialized Reconfigurable Dataflow Units (RDUs) while leaving compute-heavy prefill to GPUs, delivering up to a 3x speedup.

The Shift to Premium Inference and Compute Starvation

  • The Definition of Premium Inference: Modern frontier AI requires a combination of massive model size (trillion-parameter scale) and high token generation speeds.
    • Users are no longer forced to choose between slow, highly capable models and fast, low-capability models.
    • Top-tier open-source models, such as DeepSeek V2 and Kimi k1.5, are rapidly crossing the trillion-parameter threshold.
  • The Agentic Paradigm Shift: AI workloads are transitioning from short-lived chatbots to autonomous, long-horizon agents.
    • Agentic systems plan, write code, self-correct, and run for hours or days rather than minutes.
    • A single long-horizon coding task on OpenAI Codex can require over a day of active runtime, generating nearly 13 million tokens and requiring immense Key-Value (KV) cache context reuse.
  • Extreme Compute Bottlenecks: Enterprise demand for fast, premium inference heavily outstrips current supply.
    • Anthropic’s annual recurring revenue (ARR) grew from $1 billion in 2023 to $47 billion in May 2024, yet its growth is entirely limited by compute availability.
    • Anthropic's "fast mode" for Claude Opus runs twice as fast but costs six times more than standard mode due to the high operational costs of GPU-bound inference.
    • OpenAI, serving over 800 million weekly active users, is actively securing low-latency partnerships (such as with Cerebras) to bypass traditional GPU decoding limits.

Disaggregated Inference Architecture (GPU vs. RDU)

  • The Core Architectural Flaw: GPUs are highly parallel systems optimized for compute-bound tasks like training and prefill (processing input tokens), but they are highly inefficient for decoding (generating tokens), which is a memory-bound task.
  • SambaNova's RDU Solution: Reconfigurable Dataflow Units (RDUs) are purpose-built to handle memory-bound decoding, freeing up precious GPU capacity.
    • Three-Tier Memory Architecture: To run massive models at scale, RDUs keep large model pools in DDR, stream active weights through High Bandwidth Memory (HBM), and keep activations close to compute in local SRAM.
    • SRAM-Only Bottlenecks: Competitors like Groq and Cerebras use SRAM-only architectures. While fast, their extremely limited memory capacity forces them to split up decoding phases, making it highly difficult to scale to frontier-class models.
  • Strategies for Disaggregation:
    • *Full Decode Disaggregation:* Prefill runs on the GPU, and decoding runs entirely on the RDU with a single KV cache handoff. This is SambaNova's primary deployment model.
    • *Speculative Decoding:* Utilizing a small draft model on the RDU to speculate tokens, which are then verified in batches by a larger target model. This is being added to minimize latency in high-context agentic workloads.
    • *FFN-Only Decoding:* Splitting the Feed-Forward Network phase from attention. SambaNova is bypassing this approach, calling it a hardware-constrained workaround used by memory-limited chips rather than an efficient architecture.

Performance Benchmarks & The VectorCORE Cloud

  • VectorCORE Compute (VC2): SambaNova—in partnership with Intel CEO Pat Gelsinger, Vista Capital CEO Robert Smith, and Cambium Capital’s Landon Downs—launched VC2, a dedicated cloud designed to host heterogeneous disaggregated inference.
  • Real-World Agentic Performance: During a live demo run out of the VC2 Los Angeles data center, a 10,000-record data science agent pipeline completed an end-to-end analysis, modeling, and report generation task in 45 seconds.
    • This represented a 2x to 3x speedup compared to running the exact same agentic workflow on NVIDIA B200 GPUs alone.
  • Execution Resource Allocation: Metrics from the live run demonstrated how tasks were split to maximize efficiency:
    • RDUs (Decode Phase): Processed 56% of the execution time.
    • CPUs (Agentic logic & orchestrating): Processed 29% of the execution time.
    • GPUs (Prefill Phase): Processed 15% of the execution time.

Scaling Hardware: SN40 to SN50

  • Spatial Execution: Unlike GPUs that run inference kernel-by-kernel with high communication overhead between steps, RDUs utilize spatial execution to keep compute and communication moving in parallel across the chip.
  • SambaRack SN50 Specifications:
    • A single SambaRack SN50 contains 16 RDUs designed for agentic inference.
    • The system scales up to 256 interconnected RDUs across racks, capable of running models with up to 10 trillion parameters.
    • The Ultimate Heterogeneous Stack: The future of enterprise AI requires combining top-tier GPUs (for prefill), SambaNova SN50 RDUs (for decoding), and Intel Xeon 6 CPUs (for tool calling and logic offloading).
