Anthropic’s refusal to allow the U.S. military to use its models for surveillance or autonomous weapons creates a fundamental conflict of sovereignty; if AI is as transformative as nuclear weapons, the U.S. government cannot tolerate a private corporation acting as an independent power base that dictates national security terms.

The Anthropic vs. Department of Defense Standoff

  • Point of Conflict: Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei has restricted the U.S. military from using Claude for mass domestic surveillance and fully autonomous weapons, citing "democratic values" and current technical unreliability.
  • The Pentagon’s Response: The Department of Defense (DoD) has threatened to designate Anthropic a “supply chain risk”—a label usually reserved for foreign adversaries—and may invoke the Defense Production Act to force the removal of these safeguards.
  • Competitive Landscape: While Anthropic resists, OpenAI has recently reached an agreement with the DoD to allow its models to be used in classified settings, effectively replacing Anthropic in the military's tech stack.
  • The "Maduro Incident": Tensions spiked after Anthropic officials expressed disapproval to Palantir regarding the use of their technology during a U.S.-monitored operation involving Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro.

The Logic of Sovereign Power

  • The "International Law" Analogy: Ben Thompson argues that international law is only as "real" as the entity capable of enforcing it; similarly, a private company cannot dictate terms to a state that possesses the ultimate monopoly on force.
  • The Nuclear Comparison: Dario Amodei has compared selling AI chips to China to "selling nuclear weapons to North Korea."
      • Consequence of the Analogy: Thompson asserts that if AI is truly on the level of nuclear weapons, the U.S. government must treat it as a matter of existential national security that cannot be left in the hands of "unelected corporate executives."
  • A Binary Choice: Thompson posits that the U.S. government faces two options regarding Anthropic:

1. Force Anthropic into a subservient position where it yields decision-making power to the President and Congress.

2. Remove the leadership or destroy the entity to prevent it from becoming a rival power base.

Corporate Governance vs. Democratic Oversight

  • The Definition Problem: Restrictions like "do not target civilians" sound simple but are "moral minefields." Thompson references Palmer Luckey (Anduril) to argue that definitions of "civilian," "innocent," and "autonomous" should be decided by elected officials, not CEOs.
  • The American Experiment: There is a fundamental tension between a constitutional republic and a future where the "real levers of power" are outsourced to billionaires and their shadow advisors.
  • Enforcement Paradox: Anthropic’s position that it can "align" the military is seen as untenable because the state cannot accept a dependency on a vendor that might withdraw support during an active conflict.

Geopolitical Realism and AI Safety

This is not an article about the campaign being waged by the U.S. against Iran, but it’s a useful and timely analogy. There is a never-ending debate that can be had about the concept of International Law and who might be violating it. Some will argue that the U.S. is in violation for the attacks; others will note that Iran has been serially violating International Law with both its overt actions and its support of terror networks for my entire life. What is important to note is that the entire debate is ultimately pointless. The very concept of “international law” is fake, not because pertinent statutes and agreements don’t exist, but because their effectiveness is ultimately rooted in their enforceability. That, by extension, means there must be an entity to enact such enforcement, with the capability to match, and such an entity does not exist. Yes, there is the United Nations, but said body only operates by the agreement of its members and their willingness to subjugate themselves to not only its edicts, but to also put forward the capabilities to enforce its mandates. In other words, the only agents that matter are nation-states themselves. The relative power of those nation-states is not a function of lawyers and judges but rather their ability to project force and coerce others. To put it another way, if after this weekend you want to hold onto the concept of International Law, then realize the debate has been resolved: Iran was in violation because their military just had their clock cleaned by the U.S., which means the U.S. decides who is right and who is wrong. Complex Systems. The important takeaway that is relevant to this article is that Taiwan is the flashpoint in both scenarios. TL;DR

Max Hodak, founder of Science and co-founder of Neuralink, discusses how the field of Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCI) is shifting medicine from incremental drug discovery to a precise neural engineering paradigm capable of restoring sight today and potentially enabling human-to-human consciousness sharing by 2035.

Restoring Sight: The PRIMA Retinal Implant

Science has successfully completed a clinical trial for its PRIMA implant, a breakthrough that restores "form vision" to patients with age-related macular degeneration.

  • Technology Overview: The system uses a 2x2mm silicon chip implanted under the retina.
      • Mechanism: Patients wear glasses equipped with a camera and a laser projector. The laser hits the implant's "solar panels," which excite the bipolar cells directly.
      • Strategic Advantage: By stimulating bipolar cells rather than the optic nerve, the device preserves the retina’s natural data compression/processing, resulting in coherent images rather than just flashes of light.
  • Clinical Results: Over 40 patients have received the treatment. One patient, blind for over a decade, was able to read a full novel after the procedure.
  • Agnostic Treatment: The technology is "disease-agnostic," meaning it can restore sight regardless of why the rods and cones died (e.g., Macular Degeneration, Stargardt’s, or Diabetic Retinopathy).

The Neural Engineering Paradigm Shift

Hodak argues that the brain is effectively a computer with a well-defined "API," and engineering it is more efficient than traditional pharmaceutical approaches.

  • Drug Discovery vs. Engineering: Humanity is historically poor at drug discovery, which is often slow and incremental. Neural engineering allows for direct, empirical manipulation of the brain's information processing.
  • The Brain’s API: All information enters and leaves the brain through a limited number of "cables" (12 cranial nerves and 31 spinal nerves).
      • Latent Space: Modern AI research has revealed that the "latent spaces" and representations in LLMs look remarkably similar to how the brain represents objects and faces.
  • Neuroplasticity: The brain is highly plastic under feedback. Even in adults, the cortex can learn to interpret new signals if given consistent feedback, allowing the brain and the BCI to "handshake" and learn together.

The Next Frontier: Bio-hybrid Interfaces

Beyond electrical stimulation, Science is developing "bio-hybrid" interfaces that function like a biological "internet nerve."

  • The "Avatar" Concept: Inspired by the "ponytails" in the film *Avatar*, this involves growing engineered, stem-cell-derived neurons that wire directly into the host's brain.
  • Immune Evasion: Science has developed "hypoimmunogenic" stem cells that are hidden from the patient’s immune system, meaning the graft doesn't require a custom manufacturing process for every patient.
  • Safety Profile: Unlike gene therapy (a "one-way door"), the bio-hybrid approach only edits the graft cells. If the graft fails or dies, the patient's original brain tissue remains unmodified and unharmed.

Vessel: Miniaturizing Life Support

Science's "Vessel" program aims to take "perfusion" technology—historically confined to hospital ICUs—and make it portable.

  • The "Bridge to Nowhere" Problem: Current heart-lung machines (ECMO) are massive and expensive, often creating ethical dilemmas when patients are kept alive but cannot leave the hospital.
  • Miniaturization Goal: Hodak envisions a world where a patient can carry a heart-lung circuit in a backpack or check a donor kidney as luggage on a commercial flight.
  • Longevity Impact: These advancements in perfusion and BCI lead Hodak to believe the first people to live to 1,000 years may already be alive today, as we move toward a future where the brain can be supported by "conscious machines."
