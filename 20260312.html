TL;DR

Brent crude has surged back above $100/barrel as the 13-day conflict with Iran escalates, characterized by systemic attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure and a "risk-off" collapse in global equity markets. Despite an unprecedented 400-million-barrel emergency oil release by the IEA, the U.S. remains committed to "finishing the job" while Iran shifts to a strategy of "strike after strike."

Systemic Attacks on Energy Infrastructure

  • Maritime Disruptions: Two tankers are currently ablaze in Iraqi waters following overnight attacks, marking an expansion of hostilities into the northern Persian Gulf.
  • Gulf Export Terminals: Oman has evacuated all vessels from its Mina Al Fahal oil export terminal (which handles ~1 million barrels per day) following strikes on a fuel depot. This is significant as Oman previously served as the primary mediator between the U.S. and Iran.
  • Targeting the GCC: Attacks have intensified against critical energy assets across the region:
      • Bahrain: The Muharraq fuel facility was targeted by projectiles.
      • Saudi Arabia: The Shaybah oil field was subjected to a fresh barrage of projectiles.
      • UAE: Infrastructure in Fujairah continues to report disruptions.
  • Iranian Strategy: Analysts suggest Iran is waging a war of economic attrition, systematically targeting GCC oil and gas facilities to impose domestic political pressure on the U.S. through higher gasoline prices.

Global Energy and Market Response

  • IEA Emergency Measures: The International Energy Agency has approved a massive release of 400 million barrels of oil.
      • U.S. Contribution: President Trump announced a 172-million-barrel release from the Strategic Petroleum Reserve.
      • Japan: Releasing 80 million barrels from its strategic reserves.
  • Market Volatility: Oil prices briefly topped $100/barrel, overshadowing the IEA’s relief plan. Global equity futures (S&P 500) and Asian stocks (MXAP) fell over 1.6%, erasing recent gains.
  • Currency Pressure: The U.S. Dollar is firming following high CPI data, while the Japanese Yen has weakened to the 159 level, complicating the Bank of Japan’s upcoming rate decisions.
  • Investment Outlook: Martin Hennecke (St. James’s Place) advises investors to reduce leverage and favor liquid assets. He notes that while oil drives short-term spikes, long-term inflationary risks from high sovereign debt and rising producer prices remain a primary concern.

Political Deadlock and Military Posture

  • U.S. Positioning: President Trump maintains a "victory" narrative, claiming the U.S. has already "fully diminished and degraded" Iran’s capabilities, even as he vows to "finish the job."
  • Iranian Defiance: While President Masoud Pezeshkian offered truce conditions—including U.S. reparations and "international guarantees"—hardliners within the IRGC have contradicted him, signaling a shift to a "strike after strike" military policy.
  • Stalemate: Experts describe Iranian diplomacy as "performative," as the conditions for a ceasefire (such as the U.S. paying reparations) are deemed non-starters for the Trump administration.

Regional Operational Disruptions

  • Aviation Crisis: Dubai International Airport (DXB) has experienced twice-daily operational halts due to intercepted drones and falling debris.
      • Airline Status: Kuwait and Bahrain airliners have halted operations entirely. Bahrain has moved its entire fleet to foreign airports to avoid "war premiums" on insurance.
      • Safe Corridors: While Emirates aims to resume full operations, KLM and other international carriers have canceled flights to Dubai through late March.
  • Financial Sector Precautions: Major financial institutions in Dubai (Goldman Sachs, Citi, Standard Chartered, and HSBC) have advised employees to work from home out of "an abundance of caution" following multiple missile alert threats sent by the UAE Ministry of Interior.

The "crisis of masculinity" is a systemic issue where young men are falling behind in education, economic viability, and mental health, yet Scott Galloway argues that lifting men up is entirely compatible with—and necessary for—the continued progress of women and Western society.

The Data: A Rapid Decline for Young Men

  • Stagnating Social Milestones: One in three men live at home at age 25, and one in five still live at home at age 30. Only one in three men are currently in a relationship, compared to two in three women.
  • Educational Gaps: There is a 60/40 female-to-male ratio in college attendance, with men dropping out at significantly higher rates.
  • Mental Health Crisis: Men account for four out of every five suicide deaths.
  • The Empathy Gap: Galloway suggests young men receive less empathy because older generations of men held an "unfair advantage" (white, heterosexual males garnered 1/3 of global economic growth between 1945–1995). Current young men are being "held accountable" for the privileges of their predecessors.

Redefining Masculinity and "The Code"

  • Aspirational Masculinity: Galloway argues that "toxic masculinity" is an oxymoron; masculinity should be defined as the instinct to protect, provide, and procreate.
  • Valor vs. Aggression: While male aggression is often pathologized, Galloway cites the Carnegie Award for heroism—77 of 81 awards last year went to men—as proof that the same traits can manifest as life-saving valor.
  • Rejecting the Extremes:
      • The far right recognizes the crisis but offers a "coarse and cruel" return to the 1950s.
      • The far left often suggests men should simply "act more like women," which Galloway argues is unhelpful.
  • The Need for a Operating System: With the decline of religious institutions and the military, young men lack a "code" to guide decision-making.

Macro-Economic and Policy Reforms

  • Education Reform: "Red-shirt" boys by starting them in kindergarten a year later than girls to account for biological developmental gaps (roughly 18 months). Galloway also advocates for expanding freshman class sizes at universities to end "rejectionist culture."
  • Wealth Redistribution: Current economic policies (mortgage interest deductions, capital gains tax breaks) transfer wealth from the young and mobile to the old and established.
  • Housing and Infrastructure: Shift from "drill baby drill" to "build baby build" to make homeownership attainable for young people, which is a key driver of romantic and social stability.
  • National Service: Implement mandatory national service to foster empathy across different demographics and reduce the isolation and depression seen in the "screen-only" generation.

Individual Agency: The "Rule of Threes"

Galloway encourages young men to exercise their agency through three specific habits to put themselves in the "top 5%" of their cohort:

  • Physical Health: Work out at least three times a week.
  • Economic Engagement: Spend at least 30 hours a week working outside the home (e.g., Uber, TaskRabbit, or office work) to build professional skills.
  • Social Connection: Engage with strangers three times a month via sports leagues, volunteer groups, or religious organizations.
  • Platonic Friendships: Galloway emphasizes that having "girl-friends" (platonic) is the best way to understand what women value and to build the social intelligence required for romantic success.
